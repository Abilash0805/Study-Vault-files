Why we even study Chemical Kinetics

Imagine you cook Maggi noodles. Two questions pop up:

  • Will it actually cook? → Yes, because heat + water makes the chemicals react. This is thermodynamics.
  • How fast will it cook? → 2 minutes! This is chemical kinetics.

Chemistry asks three big questions for any reaction:

Thermodynamics says "yes it CAN happen"; Kinetics says "but here's HOW FAST"
Chemical Kinetics: The branch of chemistry that deals with the study of rates of chemical reactions, the factors that affect these rates, and the mechanism (steps) by which reactions occur.

The word "kinetics" comes from the Greek word "kinesis", meaning movement.

The Diamond Paradox 💎

Thermodynamics says: "Diamond should convert into graphite (it's more stable)."
Reality: This conversion is so unbelievably slow that "diamonds are forever" in your lifetime. This is why kinetics matters — feasibility ≠ speed!

Thermodynamics = the WILL 🎯
Kinetics = the SPEED 🏃‍♂️
A reaction can be allowed but still take 1,000,000 years!

Real-life uses of Kinetics

  • 🍞 How fast does food spoil? (Choose preservatives)
  • 🦷 How quickly does dental filling set?
  • 🚗 How fast does petrol burn in your car engine?
  • 💊 How long before a medicine works in your body?
  • 🌎 How fast is the ozone layer depleting?

Three speeds of reactions (Examples)

SpeedExampleTime
Very Fast ⚡AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃ (white ppt of silver chloride forms instantly)Instantaneous (microseconds)
Moderate 🐢Inversion of cane sugar; Hydrolysis of starchMinutes to hours
Very Slow 🐌Rusting of iron; Diamond → GraphiteYears to centuries
02

Rate of a Chemical Reaction

You already know how speed of a car works:

Speed of car = (Distance covered) ÷ (Time taken)

Reaction rate works almost the same way — but instead of distance, we track concentration (how much reactant is left, or how much product has formed).

Rate of a Reaction: The change in concentration of any reactant or product per unit time.

It can be expressed as:
  • Rate of decrease in concentration of a reactant (it's going down), OR
  • Rate of increase in concentration of a product (it's building up).
Concentration of R falls; concentration of P rises. Rate measures BOTH.

For a simple reaction R → P, where 1 mole of R gives 1 mole of P:

If at time t₁ the concentration is [R]₁ and at time t₂ it is [R]₂, then:

Δt = t₂ – t₁
Δ[R] = [R]₂ – [R]₁   (this is NEGATIVE because R decreases)
Δ[P] = [P]₂ – [P]₁   (this is POSITIVE because P increases)

(Note: square brackets [ ] mean "concentration in mol/L")

Rate of disappearance of R = – Δ[R]/Δt    ...(eq. 3.1)

Rate of appearance of P = + Δ[P]/Δt    ...(eq. 3.2)
Why the minus sign for R? Because Δ[R] is already negative (reactant decreases). The minus sign makes rate positive — because rate is a SPEED, and speed cannot be negative!
Reactant rate = "–" (minus sign added so rate stays +ve)
Product rate = "+" (already +ve, no fix needed)
📌 Rate is ALWAYS reported as a positive number.
03

Average Rate vs Instantaneous Rate

This is one of the most-tested topics on boards. Let's break it down with a car analogy.

The Car Analogy 🚗

You drive 200 km in 4 hours.

  • Average speed = 200/4 = 50 km/h (over the WHOLE trip)
  • Instantaneous speed = what your speedometer shows RIGHT NOW (could be 0, 80, or 30 at this exact second)

Average Rate

Average Rate (r_avg): The change in concentration of reactant (or product) divided by the time interval over which the change occurred.
r_avg = – Δ[R]/Δt = + Δ[P]/Δt

Instantaneous Rate

Instantaneous Rate (r_inst): The rate of a reaction at a particular moment of time. It is obtained when Δt becomes very, very small (Δt → 0).
r_inst = – d[R]/dt = + d[P]/dt

How to find it graphically: Plot concentration vs time. At the point you want, draw a tangent line. The slope of the tangent = instantaneous rate.

Average = slope joining TWO points; Instantaneous = slope of tangent at ONE point.

Why we need instantaneous rate

Average rate is okay, but it tells you about a range of time, not a single moment. If someone asks "what's the rate at t = 30 seconds?", you can't say "well, on average over 0–60s it was X" — that's lazy! For that one moment, only instantaneous rate is correct.

Average = chord (line joining two points)
Instantaneous = tangent (touching just one point)
📐 Both are slopes — different ways of drawing them.

Units of Rate

Rate = concentration ÷ time

  • If concentration is in mol L⁻¹ and time in seconds → unit is mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹
  • For gases (when partial pressure is used instead of concentration) → unit is atm s⁻¹ or bar s⁻¹
  • If time is in minutes → mol L⁻¹ min⁻¹
04

Rate with Stoichiometric Coefficients

Here's where most students get confused. Let me make it crystal clear.

Case 1: Coefficients are all 1

Hg(l) + Cl₂(g) → HgCl₂(s)

Here, every species has coefficient 1. So:
Rate = – Δ[Hg]/Δt = – Δ[Cl₂]/Δt = + Δ[HgCl₂]/Δt
Rate of disappearance of reactants = Rate of appearance of product (all numerically equal).

Case 2: Coefficients are different

Take this reaction:

2 HI(g) → H₂(g) + I₂(g)

Now, when 2 moles of HI vanish, only 1 mole of H₂ forms. So the rate of disappearance of HI is twice the rate of formation of H₂!

To make all three rates equal (one single number for the reaction), we divide each by its coefficient:

Rate of reaction = – (1/2) · Δ[HI]/Δt = + Δ[H₂]/Δt = + Δ[I₂]/Δt

Master Rule (memorize this!)

For a general reaction:

aA + bB → cC + dD

Rate = – (1/a)·Δ[A]/Δt = – (1/b)·Δ[B]/Δt = +(1/c)·Δ[C]/Δt = +(1/d)·Δ[D]/Δt
Rule: Divide by the coefficient. Put minus for reactants, plus for products.
Tough example: 5Br⁻(aq) + BrO₃⁻(aq) + 6H⁺(aq) → 3Br₂(aq) + 3H₂O(l)
Rate = –(1/5)Δ[Br⁻]/Δt = –Δ[BrO₃⁻]/Δt = –(1/6)Δ[H⁺]/Δt = +(1/3)Δ[Br₂]/Δt = +(1/3)Δ[H₂O]/Δt
Coefficient on top of the species → that number goes on the BOTTOM of the rate fraction (as divisor).
Reactant = minus. Product = plus.
📌 This is why "rate of reaction" is unique — only ONE value, no matter which species you pick.
Don't confuse "rate of reaction" with "rate of disappearance of A". They're related, but not always equal! Rate of disappearance of A = a × (rate of reaction).

Rate Law, Rate Constant & Rate Expression

The Idea

Since rate depends on concentration, can we write a formula?
For a reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD

Rate ∝ [A]ˣ [B]ʸ

Rate = k [A]ˣ [B]ʸ    ...(eq. 3.4a)

– d[R]/dt = k [A]ˣ [B]ʸ    ...(differential rate equation)

Here:

  • k = rate constant (proportionality constant)
  • x, y = order with respect to A and B (found from EXPERIMENT, not from the balanced equation!)

Three big definitions

Rate Law (or Rate Expression / Rate Equation): The expression which relates the rate of a reaction to the concentration of reactants, with each concentration term raised to some power (which may or may not be the same as the stoichiometric coefficient).
Rate Constant (k): The proportionality constant in the rate law. It is equal to the rate of reaction when the concentration of each reactant is unity (1 mol L⁻¹). Also called specific reaction rate.

Why exponents ≠ coefficients (always)

The biggest misconception students have: "Rate law comes from the balanced equation." WRONG! Rate law comes from experiments. Sometimes the powers in the rate law happen to match the coefficients — but that's a coincidence, not a rule.

Real Examples

ReactionExperimental Rate LawPowers match coefficients?
2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂Rate = k [NO]²[O₂]✅ Yes (coincidence)
CHCl₃ + Cl₂ → CCl₄ + HClRate = k [CHCl₃][Cl₂]^(1/2)❌ No
CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O → CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OHRate = k [CH₃COOC₂H₅]¹[H₂O]⁰❌ No (it's a pseudo-1st-order!)

How to find rate law experimentally (the method of initial rates)

For the reaction 2NO(g) + O₂(g) → 2NO₂(g), suppose we measured:

Expt[NO][O₂]Initial Rate (mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹)
10.300.300.096
20.600.300.384
30.300.600.192
40.600.600.768
How to crack it:
STEP 1 Compare Expts 1 & 2: [NO] doubles, [O₂] same. Rate becomes 4× (0.096 → 0.384). So rate ∝ [NO]². Order in NO = 2.
STEP 2 Compare Expts 1 & 3: [O₂] doubles, [NO] same. Rate doubles (0.096 → 0.192). So rate ∝ [O₂]¹. Order in O₂ = 1.
STEP 3 Rate law: Rate = k [NO]² [O₂]¹
STEP 4 Overall order = 2 + 1 = 3 (third order)
To find order w.r.t. a species: change ONLY that one, see how rate changes.
If conc. doubles and rate doubles → order 1
If conc. doubles and rate quadruples → order 2
If conc. doubles and rate is unchanged → order 0
08

Order of a Reaction

Order of a Reaction: The sum of the powers of the concentration of the reactants in the experimentally determined rate law expression.

If Rate = k [A]ˣ [B]ʸ, then:

  • Order with respect to A = x
  • Order with respect to B = y
  • Overall order = x + y

Key features of "order"

  • Order can be 0, 1, 2, 3, or even a fraction (like 1/2 or 3/2).
  • Order can even be negative for some species (rare but possible).
  • Order is determined experimentally, NOT from the balanced equation.
  • Order of 0 means rate is independent of that species' concentration.
Practice (Example 3.3 from NCERT):
Find overall order:
(a) Rate = k [A]^(1/2) [B]^(3/2) → Order = 1/2 + 3/2 = 2 (second order)
(b) Rate = k [A]^(3/2) [B]^(–1) → Order = 3/2 + (–1) = 1/2 (half order)

Types based on order

Shape of the Rate vs [A] graph tells you the order at a glance!

Pseudo First Order Reaction (boards love this!)

Pseudo First Order Reaction: A reaction that is actually second (or higher) order, but BEHAVES like a first order reaction because one of the reactants is present in large excess (so its concentration hardly changes).
Classic Example: Hydrolysis of ester
CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O → CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH

Theoretically, Rate = k [ester][H₂O]. But H₂O is the solvent — its concentration (~55.5 M) hardly changes. So [H₂O] is constant. Then:
Rate = k [ester][H₂O] = k' [ester]   where k' = k[H₂O]

It LOOKS like first order, but it's really second order in disguise. ⇒ Pseudo first order.

Another example: Inversion of cane sugar in dilute acid:
C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + C₆H₁₂O₆
Pseudo = "fake" or "pretending". A pseudo first order reaction PRETENDS to be first order because water is in huge excess. 🎭

This is a guaranteed 2-marker on board exams. Let's derive the formula once and for all.

Derivation of units of k
STEP 1 For a reaction of order n: Rate = k [conc]ⁿ
STEP 2 Therefore k = Rate / [conc]ⁿ
STEP 3 Units of k = (mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹) / (mol L⁻¹)ⁿ = mol^(1–n) L^(n–1) s⁻¹

Molecularity of a Reaction

Molecularity: The number of reacting species (atoms, ions or molecules) that must collide simultaneously to bring about an elementary reaction in one step.

Think of it as: "How many guests need to bump into each other at the same time for the chemistry party to happen?"

Types based on molecularity

Three molecules colliding at the same instant is statistically rare → termolecular reactions are slow & uncommon
TypeSpecies CollidingExample
Unimolecular1NH₄NO₂ → N₂ + 2H₂O
Bimolecular22HI → H₂ + I₂
Termolecular (Trimolecular)32NO + O₂ → 2NO₂
Why no 4-molecular or 5-molecular reactions? Probability of 4 molecules colliding at the EXACT same moment in the EXACT correct orientation is practically zero! So all "high-molecularity" reactions are really sequences of simpler steps.

Elementary vs Complex Reactions

Elementary Reaction: A reaction that takes place in one single step (one collision = one reaction). For these, rate law CAN be written from the balanced equation, and molecularity = order.
Complex Reaction: A reaction that takes place through a sequence of elementary steps. The set of steps is called the mechanism. For complex reactions, rate law must be found experimentally.
The "fake high-order" trap:
KClO₃ + 6FeSO₄ + 3H₂SO₄ → KCl + 3Fe₂(SO₄)₃ + 3H₂O

Looking at the equation, you might think order = 1 + 6 + 3 = 10.
But experimentally, this is only second order! That's because it actually happens in many tiny steps. The balanced equation is a LIE about how it really works. 😱

12

Rate Determining Step (RDS) & Mechanisms

The Relay Race Analogy 🏃‍♀️🏃‍♂️🏃

Imagine a relay race team with 4 runners. One of them is super slow. Even if the others sprint like Usain Bolt, the team's overall time is decided by the slow guy. Same with chemistry: in a multi-step reaction, the slowest step decides the overall rate.

Rate Determining Step (RDS): The slowest step in a multi-step (complex) reaction mechanism. It determines the overall rate of the reaction.

The Decomposition of H₂O₂ (NCERT example)

Overall reaction (catalysed by I⁻ in alkaline medium):

2H₂O₂ → 2H₂O + O₂

Experimentally determined rate law:

Rate = – d[H₂O₂]/dt = k [H₂O₂][I⁻]

Order = 1 + 1 = 2 (first order each in H₂O₂ and I⁻).

Proposed Mechanism (two steps):

STEP 1 (slow, RDS) H₂O₂ + I⁻ → H₂O + IO⁻
STEP 2 (fast) H₂O₂ + IO⁻ → H₂O + I⁻ + O₂

Notice:

  • IO⁻ is an intermediate — it forms in step 1, is used up in step 2, and doesn't show up in the overall equation.
  • I⁻ is the catalyst — used in step 1, regenerated in step 2.
  • The rate law for the slow step is Rate = k[H₂O₂][I⁻] — and this matches the overall rate law! ✅
The HIGHEST energy barrier = the slowest step = the rate determining step!

Why we need Integrated Rate Equations

You've just learnt the differential rate equation: –d[R]/dt = k[R]ⁿ. It gives the rate at any moment, but to use it, you'd have to draw a tangent on a curve every time. That's tedious and error-prone!

So chemists integrate the differential rate equation. The result is the integrated rate equation, connecting three measurable things:

[Conc at time t]  ↔  [Initial conc]  ↔  [Time]

Now you can measure concentrations at different times (easy with experiments) and directly find k and the order. No tangents needed! 🎉

Differential form = the "speedometer reading" right now ⏱
Integrated form = the "trip log" connecting start, end, and time 🗺
📐 Both describe the same reaction — different viewpoints.

NCERT does derivations for two orders only: Zero order and First order. These are guaranteed on board exams.

Zero Order Reactions — Full Derivation

Zero Order Reaction: A reaction whose rate is independent of the concentration of the reactants — rate is proportional to [reactant]⁰ = 1.

Sounds weird, right? "The speed doesn't care how much reactant you have?" Yes! Happens because of special situations (we'll see why).

🔬 Derivation of the Integrated Rate Law

Step-by-step derivation (BOARD EXAM FAVORITE — 5 marks)
STEP 1 Consider a zero order reaction: R → P
STEP 2 Write the differential rate law:
Rate = – d[R]/dt = k [R]⁰
Since anything raised to the power 0 is 1:
– d[R]/dt = k × 1 = k
STEP 3 Rearrange (move dt to right):
d[R] = – k dt
STEP 4 Integrate both sides:
∫ d[R] = ∫ – k dt
[R] = – k t + I   ...(eq. 3.5)
where I is the integration constant.
STEP 5 Apply initial condition: at t = 0, [R] = [R]₀
[R]₀ = –k(0) + I   ⇒   I = [R]₀
STEP 6 Substitute back:
[R] = – kt + [R]₀   ...(eq. 3.6)
STEP 7 Rearrange to express k:
k = ([R]₀ – [R]) / t   ...(eq. 3.7)

What the Equation Tells Us (Graph)

Compare [R] = –kt + [R]₀ with y = mx + c. So plotting [R] vs t gives a straight line!

  • Slope = –k (negative because conc. decreases)
  • Y-intercept = [R]₀ (initial concentration)
Concentration falls linearly with time. Slope's magnitude = k.

Real Examples of Zero Order Reactions

"But sir, why doesn't rate depend on concentration?" It happens when the reaction site is saturated.

Example 1: NH₃ decomposition on hot Pt (high pressure)
2NH₃(g) ──Pt, 1130K──→ N₂(g) + 3H₂(g)   Rate = k[NH₃]⁰ = k

Why zero order? The Pt surface gets fully covered with NH₃ molecules. Adding more NH₃ to the gas doesn't help — the surface is already full!

Example 2: Decomposition of HI on a gold surface.
Example 3: Many enzyme-catalyzed reactions (when substrate is in excess).
The catalyst surface is fully covered. Adding more reactant doesn't increase rate. Hence rate = constant = k.
Zero order = "rate doesn't care about concentration" 🤷
Happens when: enzyme is saturated OR catalyst surface is fully covered
Graph: STRAIGHT line falling down ([R] vs t)

First Order Reactions — Full Derivation

First Order Reaction: A reaction whose rate is proportional to the first power of the concentration of the reactant.

Rate = k [R]¹ = k [R]

This is THE most important order — radioactive decay, many decompositions, hydrolysis (in excess water), and most biological reactions are first order.

🔬 Derivation of the Integrated Rate Law

Step-by-step derivation (5-mark board question every other year!)
STEP 1 Consider R → P (first order in R).
STEP 2 Differential rate law:
Rate = – d[R]/dt = k [R]
STEP 3 Separate variables ([R] on left, t on right):
d[R]/[R] = – k dt
STEP 4 Integrate both sides:
∫ d[R]/[R] = ∫ – k dt
ln[R] = – kt + I   ...(eq. 3.8)
STEP 5 Apply initial condition: at t = 0, [R] = [R]₀
ln[R]₀ = – k(0) + I   ⇒   I = ln[R]₀
STEP 6 Substitute back:
ln[R] = – kt + ln[R]₀   ...(eq. 3.9)
STEP 7 Rearrange (move ln[R]₀ to left):
ln[R] – ln[R]₀ = – kt
Using ln(a) – ln(b) = ln(a/b):
ln([R]/[R]₀) = –kt
STEP 8 Flip signs:
k = (1/t) · ln([R]₀/[R])   ...(eq. 3.10)
STEP 9 Convert to log₁₀ (since ln = 2.303 × log):
k = (2.303/t) · log([R]₀/[R])   ...(eq. 3.15)
STEP 10 Antilog of step 7:
[R] = [R]₀ · e^(–kt)   ...(eq. 3.14)
The famous exponential decay formula!

The 5 Forms of First Order Equation

Memorize these — exam questions can use any:

①   ln[R] = –kt + ln[R]₀

②   ln([R]₀/[R]) = kt

③   [R] = [R]₀ · e^(–kt)   ← exponential decay form

④   k = (1/t) · ln([R]₀/[R])

⑤   k = (2.303/t) · log([R]₀/[R])

Between Two Time Points

Concentrations at t₁ and t₂

From ln[R] = –kt + ln[R]₀:

At t₁ ln[R]₁ = –kt₁ + ln[R]₀
At t₂ ln[R]₂ = –kt₂ + ln[R]₀
SUBTRACT ln[R]₁ – ln[R]₂ = –k(t₁ – t₂) = k(t₂ – t₁)
⇒ k = [1/(t₂ – t₁)] · ln([R]₁/[R]₂)   ...(eq. 3.13)

The Two Graphs to Memorize

Both are STRAIGHT LINES — that's the giveaway for first order. One goes down, the other goes up.

Real Examples of First Order Reactions

ReactionRate Law
Hydrogenation of ethene: C₂H₄ + H₂ → C₂H₆Rate = k[C₂H₄]
Decomposition of N₂O₅Rate = k[N₂O₅]
Decomposition of N₂ORate = k[N₂O]
Radioactive decay: ₈₈²²⁶Ra → ₂⁴He + ₈₆²²²RnRate = k[Ra]
ALL radioactive decay is first order. 🌌
That's how we date fossils with carbon-14!

First Order Gas Phase Reactions (Pressure Method)

For gases, we usually can't measure "concentration" directly. But we CAN measure pressure. Since for ideal gases, pressure ∝ concentration, we just use partial pressures instead of [concentration] in the rate equation. The form stays the same!

🔬 Derivation for gas phase

Standard reaction setup: A(g) → B(g) + C(g)

Suppose initial pressure of A is p_i (total pressure at start = p_i, since only A is present). Let pressure of A decrease by x after time t.

A(g)→B(g)+C(g)
At t = 0p_i00
At time tp_i – xxx

Half-Life of a Reaction (Both Orders, Derived)

Half-Life (t₁/₂): The time required for the concentration of a reactant to fall to HALF of its initial value.

Symbolically: at t = t₁/₂, [R] = [R]₀/2.

Half-Life for Zero Order Reaction

Derivation (1 to 3 mark question)
STEP 1 Start with: k = ([R]₀ – [R])/t   (eq. 3.7)
STEP 2 At t = t₁/₂, [R] = [R]₀/2
STEP 3 Substitute:
k = ([R]₀ – [R]₀/2) / t₁/₂ = ([R]₀/2) / t₁/₂
RESULT t₁/₂ = [R]₀ / (2k)
CONCLUSION For zero order: t₁/₂ ∝ [R]₀ (proportional to initial concentration).
More reactant = longer half-life. Less reactant = shorter half-life.

Half-Life for First Order Reaction

Derivation (VERY IMPORTANT — gets asked nearly every year!)
STEP 1 Start with: k = (2.303/t) · log([R]₀/[R])   (eq. 3.15)
STEP 2 At t = t₁/₂, [R] = [R]₀/2
STEP 3 Substitute:
k = (2.303/t₁/₂) · log([R]₀ / ([R]₀/2))
k = (2.303/t₁/₂) · log(2)
STEP 4 Rearrange:
t₁/₂ = (2.303 × log 2) / k
t₁/₂ = (2.303 × 0.301) / k
t₁/₂ = 0.693 / k
RESULT t₁/₂ = 0.693 / k   (eq. 3.17)
HUGE INSIGHT For first order: t₁/₂ is INDEPENDENT of [R]₀!
No matter how much you start with, the half-life is always the same. This is unique to first order — the principle behind carbon dating and nuclear half-lives.
After 1 half-life: half left. After 2: quarter. After 3: one-eighth. Always equal time between each "halving"!

Rate = k [ester][H₂O]

Rate = k' [ester] ← LOOKS like first order!

Effect of Temperature on Rate of Reaction

You already know from experience: hot reactions are fast, cold reactions are slow.

  • Milk goes sour faster on a hot day than in the fridge 🥛
  • Pressure cooker (high temp) cooks food faster than open pot 🍲
  • KMnO₄ + oxalic acid (the classic titration) decolourises faster when warm 🧪
RULE OF THUMB For most chemical reactions, when temperature is raised by 10°C (or 10 K), the rate constant nearly doubles (or even triples).

The ratio of rate constants at temperatures differing by 10° is called the temperature coefficient (μ):
μ = k(T+10) / k(T) ≈ 2 to 3

Real-World Magnitudes (NCERT data — N₂O₅ decomposition)

TemperatureTime for half decomposition
0°C10 days 🐢
25°C (room temp)5 hours
50°C12 minutes 🏃

Just a 50°C change makes the reaction ~1200 times faster. Wow!

But the "10° rule" is just an approximation. The exact mathematical relation between rate constant and temperature is given by the Arrhenius equation, which we'll derive and use next.

The Arrhenius Equation

First proposed by Dutch chemist J.H. van't Hoff, but the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius gave it the physical meaning (and got his name on it).

Arrhenius Equation:

k = A · e^(–Eₐ/RT)
where:
  • k = rate constant
  • A = Arrhenius factor / frequency factor / pre-exponential factor (a constant unique to each reaction)
  • Eₐ = activation energy (J/mol)
  • R = gas constant = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
  • T = temperature in Kelvin (always Kelvin, never Celsius!)
  • e = Euler's number ≈ 2.718

What does each piece MEAN?

  • A (frequency factor): Roughly, the number of collisions per second between reactant molecules, times the probability of correct orientation. It represents "if every collision worked, how many reactions would happen per second?"
  • e^(–Eₐ/RT): This is the fraction of molecules that have enough energy (≥ Eₐ) to actually react. It's always a number between 0 and 1.

So Arrhenius equation says: Rate constant = (total collisions) × (fraction of collisions energetic enough to react).

Arrhenius equation in plain English:
k = (how often molecules bump) × (how often the bumps are HARD enough)
Higher T → more hard bumps → bigger k → faster reaction 🔥
22

Activated Complex & Activation Energy

Imagine pushing a ball over a hill. The ball can't roll downhill (into the products' valley) unless you first push it OVER the top. That hill is the activation energy.

Activation Energy (Eₐ): The minimum extra energy that reactant molecules must possess (above their average energy) to be able to react and form products. Measured in J/mol or kJ/mol.
Activated Complex (or Transition State): An unstable, high-energy intermediate formed momentarily when reactant molecules collide. It exists for a tiny fraction of a second and then either breaks down to form products or falls back to reactants.

The Classic Example: H₂ + I₂ → 2HI

An H₂ molecule and I₂ molecule collide, forming an unstable 4-atom "activated complex", which then splits into 2 HI

The Famous Energy-Profile Diagram

A → C: reactants climb the hill (need Eₐ). C → B: products roll down. ΔH depends on final vs initial heights.
  • To get from A (reactants) to B (products), molecules MUST first reach C (the top of the hill).
  • The energy needed to reach C from A = Eₐ (activation energy).
  • The energy released when going from C to B depends on the products' stability.
  • ΔH (enthalpy of reaction) = (energy of products) – (energy of reactants). Doesn't depend on the path taken.
Lower Eₐ → easier to climb the hill → faster reaction 🏃
Higher Eₐ → harder to climb → slower reaction 🐢
Catalysts work by giving a LOWER alternative hill to climb! 🧗

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution Curve

Not every molecule in a sample moves at the same speed. Some are slow, some are fast, most are in the middle. Two scientists — Ludwig Boltzmann and James Clerk Maxwell — used statistics to describe this distribution.

Maxwell-Boltzmann Distribution: A graph that shows the fraction of molecules having a particular kinetic energy at a given temperature.

On the graph:
  • X-axis: kinetic energy
  • Y-axis: fraction of molecules (N_E / N_T), where N_E = molecules with energy E, N_T = total molecules
  • The PEAK is the most probable kinetic energy
Higher temp = curve flattens & shifts right. Area beyond Eₐ ≈ doubles for every 10° rise → rate doubles!

Key Insights from the Curve

  • The total area under the curve = total fraction = 1 (always, at any temperature, since all molecules together = 100%).
  • At higher T: the PEAK shifts right (most probable KE increases) and the curve becomes flatter (wider spread).
  • The shaded area to the right of Eₐ represents the fraction of molecules with enough energy to react.
  • This shaded area approximately doubles when temperature rises by 10°C — which is why rate doubles!
Higher temp doesn't just make ALL molecules faster — it spreads them out so MANY MORE cross the activation energy barrier.
More molecules over the hill = more successful collisions = higher rate 🏔

Arrhenius Plots — Taking the Logarithm

Starting from k = A · e^(–Eₐ/RT), we take the natural logarithm of both sides:

Derivation of the logarithmic form
STEP 1 Start with Arrhenius equation:
k = A · e^(–Eₐ/RT)
STEP 2 Take ln on both sides:
ln k = ln(A) + ln(e^(–Eₐ/RT))
STEP 3 Since ln(e^x) = x:
ln k = ln A – Eₐ/(RT)   ...(eq. 3.19)
STEP 4 Rearrange in y = mx + c form:
ln k = (–Eₐ/R) × (1/T) + ln A

If we plot ln k on Y-axis vs 1/T on X-axis, we get a straight line!
• Slope = –Eₐ/R
• Y-intercept = ln A
Straight-line graph! Slope gives you Eₐ; y-intercept gives you ln A.

How to use the graph to find Eₐ and A

  1. Measure k at several temperatures.
  2. Compute 1/T (in K⁻¹) and ln k for each.
  3. Plot ln k (y-axis) vs 1/T (x-axis).
  4. Draw the best straight line.
  5. Slope = –Eₐ/R ⇒ Eₐ = – slope × R
  6. Y-intercept = ln A ⇒ A = e^(intercept)
For Arrhenius plot, the slope is ALWAYS NEGATIVE (because –Eₐ/R is negative). 📉
Don't forget the minus sign when calculating Eₐ from slope!

Two-Temperature Form of Arrhenius Equation

If you know k at two different temperatures, you can find Eₐ without drawing graphs. This is one of the most-used formulas in numerical problems.

Derivation (4-5 mark question!)
STEP 1 Write ln k = ln A – Eₐ/RT at two temperatures:
At T₁:   ln k₁ = ln A – Eₐ/(RT₁)  ...(eq. 3.20)
At T₂:   ln k₂ = ln A – Eₐ/(RT₂)  ...(eq. 3.21)
STEP 2 Subtract eq.(3.20) from eq.(3.21):
ln k₂ – ln k₁ = – Eₐ/(RT₂) – (– Eₐ/(RT₁))
ln(k₂/k₁) = Eₐ/R × [1/T₁ – 1/T₂]
STEP 3 Simplify the bracket:
1/T₁ – 1/T₂ = (T₂ – T₁)/(T₁ T₂)
RESULT (natural log form) ln(k₂/k₁) = (Eₐ/R) × (T₂ – T₁)/(T₁ T₂)
RESULT (log₁₀ form) log(k₂/k₁) = (Eₐ / 2.303 R) × (T₂ – T₁)/(T₁ T₂)
Always make sure T₂ > T₁ when applying this. Temperature must be in Kelvin — NOT Celsius. Convert with K = °C + 273.
Quick illustration: If the rate constant doubles when T rises from 300 K to 310 K, find Eₐ.
log(k₂/k₁) = log(2) = 0.301
0.301 = (Eₐ / 2.303 × 8.314) × (310 – 300)/(300 × 310)
0.301 = (Eₐ / 19.147) × (10/93000)
0.301 = Eₐ × 5.61 × 10⁻⁶
Eₐ = 0.301 / (5.61 × 10⁻⁶) ≈ 53,654 J/mol ≈ 53.7 kJ/mol

Effect of a Catalyst

Catalyst: A substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself undergoing any permanent chemical change. It is recovered unchanged at the end of the reaction.
Inhibitor: A substance that decreases the rate of a reaction. (Note: do NOT call this a "negative catalyst" — modern terminology calls it an inhibitor.)

Classic Example

2 KClO₃ ──MnO₂──→ 2 KCl + 3 O₂

Without MnO₂: this decomposition is extremely slow. With a small pinch of MnO₂ (manganese dioxide): the reaction proceeds rapidly. The MnO₂ is recovered unchanged at the end.

How does a catalyst work? — Intermediate Complex Theory

The catalyst doesn't take a shortcut around the hill — it provides a completely different, lower hill:

  1. Reactant(s) + Catalyst → form a temporary intermediate complex (loose bonds)
  2. This intermediate complex falls apart easily, giving products + catalyst back unchanged
  3. The new pathway has a lower activation energy → faster reaction
The catalyst provides an alternative path with a LOWER hill. Reactant and product energies stay the same.

VERY important properties of a catalyst (board-question gold)

PropertyDetail
Does catalyst change ΔG?NO. ΔG is fixed by reactants and products.
Does catalyst change ΔH?NO. Reactants and products are unchanged.
Does catalyst speed up a non-spontaneous reaction?NO. If ΔG > 0, no amount of catalyst will help.
Does catalyst change Kc (equilibrium constant)?NO. It changes only the SPEED of reaching equilibrium.
Does it catalyse the forward reaction only?NO. It speeds up forward and backward reactions equally.
How does it speed things up?By LOWERING the activation energy (Eₐ).
Amount needed?Tiny! A small amount of catalyst can speed up huge amounts of reactant.
A catalyst is like a SHORTCUT TUNNEL through the hill 🛤
Same start, same end — just an EASIER PATH.
📌 Catalysts can't make impossible reactions happen — only speed up reactions that WERE already going to happen!
Memorize this 1-mark trick: Catalyst doesn't change Kc, ΔG, or ΔH. It ONLY lowers Eₐ. If a question asks "does catalyst increase yield?" — the answer is NO (Kc unchanged).

Collision Theory of Chemical Reactions

Arrhenius equation works pretty well, but it doesn't TELL you why reactions happen. To understand the actual mechanics of molecules colliding, we use Collision Theory, developed by Max Trautz and William Lewis (around 1916–18). It's based on the kinetic theory of gases.

The core idea

Treat molecules as hard spheres (like little billiard balls). Reactions happen when they collide with each other. Simple, right?

Collision Frequency (Z): The number of collisions per second per unit volume of the reaction mixture.

The Bimolecular Rate Equation

For a simple bimolecular elementary reaction A + B → Products:

Rate = Z_AB × e^(–Eₐ/RT)   ...(eq. 3.23)

where:

  • Z_AB = collision frequency between A and B molecules
  • e^(–Eₐ/RT) = fraction of those collisions that have enough energy (≥ Eₐ)

Comparing with Arrhenius equation (k = A·e^(–Eₐ/RT)), we see that the Arrhenius factor A is closely related to the collision frequency Z_AB.

Threshold Energy: The minimum kinetic energy that reactant molecules must possess for an effective collision.

Threshold Energy = Activation Energy (Eₐ) + Average energy of reactant molecules
28

Orientation, Steric Factor & Effective Collisions

Collision theory works great for atoms and simple molecules. But for big complex molecules, it overpredicts the rate! Why? Because not every "energetic-enough" collision actually leads to a reaction. The molecules also need to hit each other in the RIGHT way.

Effective Collisions: Collisions in which molecules collide with (1) enough energy (≥ Eₐ) AND (2) proper orientation to break old bonds and form new ones. Only effective collisions lead to products.

The Famous Orientation Example: CH₃Br + OH⁻ → CH₃OH + Br⁻

For methanol formation from bromomethane, the OH⁻ must attack from the side opposite to where Br⁻ leaves:

Same energy, different orientation → different outcomes. That's why we need the "steric factor" P.

The Modified Equation with Steric Factor (P)

Steric Factor / Probability Factor (P): A factor introduced to account for the requirement of proper orientation in a collision. It's the fraction of collisions that have the geometrically right setup.
Rate = P × Z_AB × e^(–Eₐ/RT)

So now we have three conditions for a successful reaction:

  1. Molecules must collide (factor Z_AB).
  2. Collision must have enough energy (factor e^(–Eₐ/RT)).
  3. Collision must have correct orientation (factor P).

Drawbacks of Collision Theory

  • Treats molecules as hard spheres — ignores their actual shape and electronic structure.
  • Doesn't explain reactions in solution very well.
  • P is empirical — has to be measured, not predicted from first principles.
Successful reaction = Right COLLISION + Right ENERGY + Right ORIENTATION 🎯
Like trying to throw a basketball through a hoop:
1) You have to throw it (collision),
2) Hard enough (energy),
3) In the right direction (orientation)!

Define order of a reaction. How is it different from molecularity?

Let rate = k[A]ⁿ
27 × rate = k(3[A])ⁿ = k × 3ⁿ × [A]ⁿ
⇒ 3ⁿ = 27 = 3³
⇒ n = 3 (third order)

Derive the integrated rate equation for a first order reaction.
See Section 15. Full step-by-step derivation gives:

(b) For a first order reaction, show that the time required for 99% completion is double the time required for 90% completion.

(b) See NCERT 3.18 solution: t₉₉/t₉₀ = log(100)/log(10) = 2/1 = 2 ✓
PYQ (CBSE 2016) — 2 marks★★
A reaction is first order in A and second order in B. Write the differential rate equation. How is the rate affected when concentrations of both A and B are doubled?
Rate = k[A][B]² (first order in A, second in B)
When both doubled: new rate = k(2[A])(2[B])² = 8 × k[A][B]² = 8 × original rate
PYQ (CBSE 2020) — 3 marks★★★ numerical
The rate of a particular reaction triples when the temperature changes from 50°C to 100°C. Calculate the energy of activation. (R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)
T₁ = 323 K, T₂ = 373 K, k₂/k₁ = 3
log(3) = (Eₐ/19.147) × (50/(323×373))
0.477 = (Eₐ/19.147) × (50/120479)
0.477 = Eₐ × 2.167 × 10⁻⁵
Eₐ ≈ 22014 J/mol ≈ 22.01 kJ/mol
PYQ (CBSE 2015) — 5 marks★★★ classic numerical
A first order reaction takes 69.3 minutes for 50% completion. Calculate the time needed for 80% completion.
For first order, t₁/₂ = 69.3 min ⇒ k = 0.693/69.3 = 0.01 min⁻¹

For 80% completion: [R] = 0.20[R]₀
t = (2.303/k) × log(1/0.20) = (2.303/0.01) × log(5)
= 230.3 × 0.699
≈ 161 minutes
PYQ (CBSE 2019) — 2 marks★★ Arrhenius
Define activation energy. Why does a catalyst increase the rate of a reaction?
Activation energy (Eₐ): The minimum extra energy reactant molecules need above their average energy to be able to react and form products.

A catalyst increases rate by providing an alternative reaction path with a lower activation energy. From k = A·e^(–Eₐ/RT), lower Eₐ → larger k → faster reaction. Note: catalyst does NOT change ΔG, ΔH, or Kc.
PYQ (CBSE 2017) — 3 marks★★★ collision theory
What is collision theory? Explain the term "effective collisions" and the role of the steric factor (P).
Collision theory (Trautz & Lewis, 1916–18) treats reactant molecules as hard spheres reacting when they collide. Rate = Z_AB · e^(–Eₐ/RT), where Z_AB is collision frequency.

Effective collisions: only those with (i) sufficient energy (≥ Eₐ) AND (ii) correct orientation lead to a reaction.

Steric factor P accounts for the orientation requirement. Modified equation:
Rate = P · Z_AB · e^(–Eₐ/RT)
PYQ (CBSE 2018) — 1 mark★
Why does a catalyst not change the equilibrium constant of a reaction?
A catalyst speeds up both the forward and the backward reactions to the same extent. So equilibrium is reached faster, but the ratio of forward to backward rates (which determines Kc) stays the same.
PYQ (CBSE 2020) — 2 marks★★ Arrhenius rate ratio
A catalyst lowers the activation energy of a reaction from 80 kJ/mol to 60 kJ/mol at 300 K. By what factor does the rate increase?
Rate ratio = k_cat/k_uncat = e^[(80000 – 60000)/(8.314 × 300)]
= e^[20000/2494] = e^8.02
= ≈ 3037 (about 3000 times faster!)
PYQ (CBSE 2016) — 3 marks★★★ threshold energy
Define threshold energy. How does it differ from activation energy?
Threshold energy: The minimum total kinetic energy that reactant molecules must possess to produce a successful collision (forming products).

Activation energy (Eₐ): The EXTRA energy required by molecules above their average kinetic energy.

Relation: Threshold energy = Eₐ + average energy of reactants
PYQ (CBSE 2015) — 2 marks★★ characteristics
State two important characteristics of a catalyst.
(1) A small amount of catalyst can speed up large amounts of reactant — not consumed.
(2) A catalyst does not change ΔG, ΔH, or Kc — it only lowers Eₐ.

HOTS & Theory Questions

Higher Order Thinking Skills questions — the kind that test if you really UNDERSTAND, not just memorized.

HOTS-1conceptual
Why is molecularity defined only for elementary reactions and not for complex reactions?
Because in a complex reaction, the overall reaction happens through multiple elementary steps, each with its own molecularity. There's no single "number of colliding species" for the overall reaction — only for each step. So molecularity has no meaning for the overall complex reaction.
HOTS-2conceptual
Can a reaction have order 0 but molecularity greater than 0? Explain.
Yes! Example: decomposition of NH₃ on hot Pt surface. It's molecularity 1 or 2 (one or two NH₃ molecules on the surface react), but order is 0 because the surface is saturated — rate doesn't depend on [NH₃]. Order ≠ molecularity in general.
HOTS-3graphical reasoning
A plot of log([R]₀/[R]) vs t gives a straight line passing through origin with slope = 1.92 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹. What is the order and rate constant?
A straight line for log([R]₀/[R]) vs t means first order.
Slope = k/2.303 ⇒ k = slope × 2.303 = 1.92 × 10⁻³ × 2.303 = 4.42 × 10⁻³ s⁻¹
HOTS-4conceptual
Why does the rate of a first order reaction decrease with time, even though the rate constant is, well, constant?
The rate constant k is fixed (at a given temperature). But rate = k[R]. As reactant gets consumed, [R] keeps decreasing, so rate decreases too. k is unchanged — what changes is the concentration of available reactant.
HOTS-5tricky
The half-life of a first order reaction is 30 minutes. How much of a 5 g sample will be left after 90 minutes?
90 min = 3 half-lives.
After 1 half-life: 2.5 g; after 2: 1.25 g; after 3: 0.625 g ✓
Or formula: [R] = [R]₀ × (1/2)ⁿ = 5 × (1/2)³ = 0.625 g
HOTS-6conceptual
Why is the activation energy of a reverse reaction usually different from that of the forward reaction?
Forward Eₐ = (energy of activated complex) – (energy of reactants).
Reverse Eₐ = (energy of activated complex) – (energy of products).
Since reactants and products have different energies (that's ΔH), the two Eₐ values differ. In fact: Eₐ(forward) – Eₐ(reverse) = ΔH.
HOTS-7conceptual
A catalyst is added to a reaction that is at equilibrium. What happens?
Nothing visible — the system stays at equilibrium! The catalyst speeds up BOTH forward and backward reactions equally. Since the system was already balanced, both rates speed up equally and the system remains balanced.
HOTS-8conceptual
Can a catalyst make a thermodynamically non-spontaneous reaction happen?
No. A catalyst only changes Eₐ — it doesn't change ΔG. If ΔG > 0 (non-spontaneous), no amount of catalyst can make it spontaneous. The catalyst only speeds up reactions that would have happened anyway.
HOTS-9conceptual
For a given temperature, would you expect a reaction with low A (frequency factor) to be faster or slower than one with high A, assuming same Eₐ?
Slower. A measures how often molecules with proper orientation collide. Low A → fewer effective collisions → slower reaction (k = A × e^(–Eₐ/RT) is smaller).
HOTS-10conceptual
Why do collision theory predictions match experimental rates for simple molecules but not for complex molecules?
For simple molecules (atoms, small molecules), orientation isn't very important — any energetic collision works. For complex molecules, orientation MATTERS hugely — only collisions hitting the right reactive site work. Collision theory doesn't account for this without the empirical steric factor P.
HOTS-11tricky math
Two reactions have the same Eₐ. Reaction 1 has A = 10¹³; Reaction 2 has A = 10⁸. Which is faster, and by what factor?
Since k ∝ A (with same Eₐ and T), Reaction 1 is faster. Factor = 10¹³/10⁸ = 10⁵ times faster.
HOTS-12conceptual
If activation energy of a reaction is very low (close to 0), would the rate be very sensitive to temperature? Explain.
No. If Eₐ ≈ 0, then e^(–Eₐ/RT) ≈ e⁰ ≈ 1 regardless of T, so k ≈ A — essentially temperature-independent. Reactions with very low Eₐ proceed at nearly the same rate at low and high temperatures.

Top Exam Traps to Avoid

❌ Using order = stoichiometric coefficient.
✅ Order must be found EXPERIMENTALLY. Coefficients are a coincidence at best.
❌ Reporting rate as a negative number.
✅ Rate is always POSITIVE. The minus sign for reactant rate is built into the formula.
❌ Forgetting to divide by stoichiometric coefficient when comparing reactant/product rates.
✅ Rate of reaction = (rate of disappearance of A) / a. Always.
❌ Confusing average rate with instantaneous rate.
✅ Average = over an interval. Instantaneous = at one moment. Different formulas!
❌ Forgetting units of k change with order.
✅ Zero: mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹. First: s⁻¹. Second: L mol⁻¹ s⁻¹.
❌ Saying first order t₁/₂ depends on [R]₀.
✅ It does NOT. Only zero (and other non-first) order half-lives depend on initial conc.
❌ Computing Arrhenius using Celsius.
✅ T must ALWAYS be in Kelvin. K = °C + 273.
❌ Saying catalyst increases yield of product.
✅ NO! Catalyst doesn't change Kc. Yield at equilibrium is the same. Only SPEED changes.
❌ Saying catalyst lowers ΔG.
✅ NO. ΔG depends only on initial and final states. Catalyst only lowers Eₐ.
❌ Confusing "negative catalyst" with "inhibitor".
✅ Modern terminology: use "inhibitor". "Negative catalyst" is outdated.
❌ Treating threshold energy and activation energy as the same.
✅ Threshold energy = Eₐ + average energy of molecules. They are NOT equal.
❌ Forgetting that orientation matters even when molecules have enough energy.
✅ Effective collision = enough energy AND correct orientation. BOTH required!
❌ Forgetting log vs ln in Arrhenius equations.
✅ ln(k₂/k₁) = Eₐ/R × (T₂–T₁)/(T₁T₂). With log₁₀, divide RHS by 2.303.
