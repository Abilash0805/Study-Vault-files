🌍What is Electrochemistry?
🧒
Hey Abilash, imagine this: Your phone charges overnight (electricity → chemical reaction) and a lemon battery lights a small LED (chemical → electricity). BOTH are electrochemistry! It's the science of converting between chemical energy and electrical energy.
📖 Board-Ready Definition: Electrochemistry is the branch of chemistry that deals with (1) the production of electricity from energy released during spontaneous chemical reactions, and (2) the use of electrical energy to bring about non-spontaneous chemical transformations.
🌟 Real-World Examples:
  • 🔋 Batteries in your phone/car – Chemical → Electrical energy
  • ⚡ Electroplating jewelry – Electrical → Chemical (depositing gold/silver)
  • 🏭 Making chlorine & NaOH – Chlor-alkali process (industry)
  • 🧠 Brain signals – Nerve impulses are electrochemical signals!
  • 🚗 Car rusting – Corrosion is an electrochemical process
  • ⚗️ Making aluminium – Hall-Héroult process uses electrolysis
  • 🔬 Glucose sensors – Diabetics use electrochemical biosensors
🧠 Never-Forget Trick: ELECtrochemistry = ELECtricity ↔ Chemistry. Think of it as a "two-way traffic road" – chemistry can produce electricity, or electricity can drive chemistry!
🔋Electrochemical Cells

An electrochemical cell is a device that converts chemical energy into electrical energy (or vice versa). There are TWO types:

⚡ Galvanic Cell (Voltaic Cell)

  • Chemical → Electrical energy
  • Spontaneous reaction (ΔG < 0)
  • Example: Daniell Cell, dry cell, car battery
  • Anode = negative terminal
  • Cathode = positive terminal
  • Electrons flow: anode → external circuit → cathode

🔌 Electrolytic Cell

  • Electrical → Chemical energy
  • Non-spontaneous reaction (ΔG > 0)
  • Example: Electroplating, electrolysis of water
  • Anode = positive terminal
  • Cathode = negative terminal
  • Electrons flow: external source → cathode
🤔
Confused about anode & cathode? Simple! ANODE = Oxidation (both start with vowels 🅰️-🅾️). CATHODE = Reduction (both start with consonants 🅲-🆁). Also remember: "An Ox, Red Cat" — ANode = OXidation, REDuction = CAThode!
⚠️ KEY DIFFERENCE – Same words, different charge!
In a Galvanic cell: Anode = (−), Cathode = (+)
In an Electrolytic cell: Anode = (+), Cathode = (−)
This confuses everyone! Remember: In galvanic, the anode is the SOURCE of electrons (−). In electrolytic, the anode is connected to (+) terminal of battery.

🔬Galvanic Cell – The Daniell Cell
👨‍🔬
Imagine a tennis match between Zinc and Copper! Zinc REALLY wants to become an ion (it's a better reducing agent). So it gives its electrons to Copper through a wire. Those electrons flow = ELECTRIC CURRENT. Copper ions in solution grab those electrons and become solid copper. Simple!

Construction of Daniell Cell

The Daniell Cell consists of:

  • 🔩 Zinc electrode dipped in ZnSO₄ solution (left half-cell = ANODE)
  • 🟤 Copper electrode dipped in CuSO₄ solution (right half-cell = CATHODE)
  • 🌉 Salt bridge – connects the two solutions, maintains electrical neutrality
  • 📏 Voltmeter – measures cell potential (reads 1.1 V at standard conditions)

Fig 2: Daniell Cell — Zinc oxidizes at anode, copper deposits at cathode. EMF = 1.1V at standard conditions.

What Happens Inside? (Step by Step)

Zinc atoms at the Zn electrode lose 2 electrons and become Zn²⁺ ions that go into solution. OXIDATION
Electrons travel from Zn through the external wire to the Cu electrode. This electron flow = electric current! 🔌
Cu²⁺ ions from CuSO₄ solution grab those electrons at the Cu electrode and become solid copper that deposits on it. REDUCTION
As Zn²⁺ builds up in left beaker (positive charge increases), and Cu²⁺ is consumed in right beaker (negative charge increases), the salt bridge sends ions across to maintain charge neutrality.

Half-Cell Reactions

Anode (Oxidation):   Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
Cathode (Reduction): Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Net Cell Reaction:   Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)

Cell Notation (Shorthand)

How to write cell notation: Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)

Rules: | = phase boundary (metal–solution interface) | || = salt bridge | Anode on LEFT, Cathode on RIGHT | Lower concentration written in brackets if needed

Salt Bridge – Why is it needed?

Without a salt bridge, the reaction would quickly stop because:

  • Left beaker: Zn²⁺ accumulates → net positive charge → repels further oxidation
  • Right beaker: Cu²⁺ decreases → net negative charge → repels further reduction

Salt bridge fixes this by allowing ion migration:

  • Anions (Cl⁻ or NO₃⁻) move to the left (anode) compartment
  • Cations (K⁺ or NH₄⁺) move to the right (cathode) compartment
📖 Definition (Salt Bridge): A salt bridge is a U-shaped tube containing a concentrated solution of an inert electrolyte (like KCl, KNO₃, NH₄NO₃) in agar-agar gel. It maintains electrical neutrality by allowing ion migration between the two half-cells.
📝 Previous Year Board Question (2020)
Q: What is the role of a salt bridge in an electrochemical cell?

Answer: A salt bridge serves two purposes: (1) It completes the electrical circuit by allowing ion migration between the two half-cells, and (2) It maintains electrical neutrality in both the half-cell solutions — anions migrate toward the anode compartment and cations migrate toward the cathode compartment. Without it, the cell would stop working quickly due to charge buildup.

What happens when you apply external voltage?

External Voltage (Eₑₓₜ)What Happens
Eₑₓₜ < 1.1 VNormal cell operation. Electrons flow Zn→Cu. Zn dissolves, Cu deposits.
Eₑₓₜ = 1.1 VNo current flows. No reaction. Equilibrium!
Eₑₓₜ > 1.1 VReaction REVERSES! Cell becomes electrolytic. Cu dissolves, Zn deposits.
📊Electrode Potential & Standard Hydrogen Electrode
📖 Electrode Potential: The potential difference that develops between an electrode and its electrolyte solution due to the tendency of ions to oxidize/reduce at the electrode surface. When all concentrations are 1 M and temperature is 298 K and pressure is 1 bar, it is called the Standard Electrode Potential (E°).
🏠
Think of it like measuring sea level! We can't measure the absolute height of a mountain without a reference point (sea level = 0 m). Similarly, we can't measure absolute electrode potential — we need a reference electrode. The Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) is our "sea level" (E° = 0 V by convention).

Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)

Fig 3: SHE — Platinum electrode coated with Pt black, H₂ gas at 1 bar, H⁺ at 1 M, E° = 0.00 V

📖 SHE Conditions (must memorize!):
  • Electrode: Platinum (Pt) coated with platinum black
  • Gas: Pure H₂ at 1 bar pressure
  • Solution: H⁺(aq) at 1 M concentration
  • Temperature: 298 K (25°C)
  • Assigned potential: E° = 0.00 V by convention

How to measure electrode potential of any electrode?

Connect the unknown electrode to SHE (SHE = anode/left electrode)
Make concentrations of all species = 1 M (standard conditions)
Measure the cell potential with a voltmeter
E°(cell) = E°(right) − E°(left) = E°(unknown) − 0 = E°(unknown)

Examples:

Cell: Pt | H₂(1 bar) | H⁺(1M) || Cu²⁺(1M) | Cu EMF measured = +0.34 V ∴ E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34 V (Cu²⁺ easily reduced) Cell: Pt | H₂(1 bar) | H⁺(1M) || Zn²⁺(1M) | Zn EMF measured = −0.76 V ∴ E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76 V (Zn more easily oxidized)
⚠️ What do positive and negative E° mean?
Positive E°: The species is MORE easily reduced than H⁺. It's a stronger oxidizing agent.
Negative E°: H⁺ is more easily reduced. The metal is a stronger reducing agent.

Calculating Cell EMF

📐 Formula: Cell EMF
E°(cell) = E°(cathode) − E°(anode)
           = E°(right) − E°(left)
           = E°(reduction at cathode) − E°(reduction at anode)

For Daniell Cell:
E°(cell) = E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) − E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = 0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V
⚠️ KEY: Always write both as REDUCTION potentials, then subtract anode from cathode!
Never add! Never use oxidation potential formula unless specifically stated. IUPAC convention = all as reduction potentials.

Mg(s) | Mg²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)

Answer:
Anode: Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻
Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Net: Mg + Cu²⁺ → Mg²⁺ + Cu
E°cell = 0.34 − (−2.36) = +2.70 V
📉Nernst Equation
🧪
Real life = not always standard conditions! Standard conditions (all 1M, 298K, 1 bar) are ideal lab conditions. But your car battery doesn't care! Nernst (a German scientist) gave us a formula to find the actual EMF at ANY concentration and temperature.
📖 Nernst Equation: The Nernst equation gives the electrode potential (or cell potential) at any concentration. It shows that as concentration of reactants changes, the cell potential changes.

The Equation

📐 Nernst Equation (for a cell)
E(cell) = E°(cell) − (RT/nF) × ln Q

OR, at 298 K (substituting R=8.314, T=298, converting ln to log):

E(cell) = E°(cell) − (0.0592/n) × log Q

Where:
• E(cell) = actual cell potential
• E°(cell) = standard cell potential
• R = gas constant = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
• T = temperature in Kelvin
• n = number of electrons transferred
• F = Faraday constant = 96487 C mol⁻¹
• Q = reaction quotient = [Products]/[Reactants]
• 0.0592 V = 2.303RT/F at 298 K (often written as 0.059)

For the Daniell Cell:

Reaction: Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s) n = 2 electrons E(cell) = E°(cell) − (0.0592/2) × log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺]) E(cell) = 1.10 − 0.0296 × log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺])

Implication: Increasing [Cu²⁺] increases E(cell). Increasing [Zn²⁺] decreases E(cell).

⚠️ Solids and pure liquids have activity = 1!
Zn(s) and Cu(s) are NOT included in Q. Only dissolved species in solution appear in Q.

2.3.1 Equilibrium Constant from Nernst Equation

At equilibrium, E(cell) = 0 (no net reaction, voltmeter reads 0) and Q = Kc. Substituting in Nernst equation:

📐 E°cell and Equilibrium Constant
0 = E°(cell) − (0.0592/n) × log Kc

∴ E°(cell) = (0.0592/n) × log Kc

OR: log Kc = (n × E°cell) / 0.0592

OR general form: E°(cell) = (2.303RT/nF) × log Kc
📐 Solved Example (from NCERT)
Q: Calculate Kc for Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s). E°cell = 1.1V, T=298K
E°cell = (0.0592/n) × log Kc 1.1 = (0.0592/2) × log Kc log Kc = (1.1 × 2) / 0.0592 = 2.2/0.0592 = 37.16 Kc = 10^37.16 ≈ 2 × 10³⁷ This HUGE Kc means the reaction is almost completely forward!
📐 Solved Example 2.1 (NCERT)
Q: Mg(s) + 2Ag⁺(0.0001M) → Mg²⁺(0.130M) + 2Ag(s). E°cell = 3.17V. Find E(cell).
Cell notation: Mg | Mg²⁺(0.130M) || Ag⁺(0.0001M) | Ag n = 2 Q = [Mg²⁺]/[Ag⁺]² = 0.130/(0.0001)² = 0.130/10⁻⁸ = 1.3 × 10⁷ E(cell) = 3.17 − (0.0592/2) × log(1.3 × 10⁷) = 3.17 − 0.0296 × 7.114 = 3.17 − 0.21 = 2.96 V
📝 Board Question (2018)
Q: Write Nernst equation. How does the EMF of a Daniel cell change when the concentration of Zn²⁺ ions in the anode compartment is doubled?

Answer: E(cell) = E°(cell) − (0.0592/2) log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺]). When [Zn²⁺] doubles, the log term increases, so E(cell) decreases (since we're subtracting a larger value).
🔗Gibbs Energy & Electrochemical Cells
⚖️
The Grand Unifying Equation! Gibbs energy (ΔG) tells us if a reaction is spontaneous. EMF (E) tells us if a cell can produce voltage. They're CONNECTED! A negative ΔG = positive E = spontaneous reaction = working battery. Three properties of ONE reaction!
📐 Gibbs Energy ↔ Cell EMF
ΔrG = −nFE(cell)

At standard conditions:
ΔrG° = −nFE°(cell)

Where:
• n = moles of electrons transferred
• F = Faraday constant = 96487 C mol⁻¹ (≈ 96500 C mol⁻¹)
• E°(cell) = standard cell potential in Volts (V = J/C)
• ΔrG° will be in Joules (J mol⁻¹)
⚠️ Sign Rules:
If E°(cell) is POSITIVE → ΔrG° is NEGATIVE → Reaction is SPONTANEOUS ✅
If E°(cell) is NEGATIVE → ΔrG° is POSITIVE → Reaction is NON-SPONTANEOUS ❌

The Triple Connection:

ΔrG° = −nFE°(cell) = −RT ln Kc = −2.303RT log Kc Therefore: E°(cell) = (RT/nF) ln Kc = (0.0592/n) log Kc (at 298K)
📐 Solved Example 2.3 (NCERT)
Q: Calculate standard Gibbs energy for: Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s). E°cell = 1.1V
ΔrG° = −nFE°(cell) n = 2 (2 electrons transferred) F = 96487 C mol⁻¹ E° = 1.1 V ΔrG° = −2 × 96487 × 1.1 = −212271 J mol⁻¹ = −212.27 kJ mol⁻¹ Negative ΔrG° confirms: spontaneous reaction ✅
⚠️ E(cell) is INTENSIVE — ΔrG is EXTENSIVE!
If you double the equation: 2Zn + 2Cu²⁺ → 2Zn²⁺ + 2Cu
E°cell stays 1.1V (unchanged), but ΔrG° doubles to −424.54 kJ mol⁻¹ (n becomes 4).
📝 Board Question (2023)
Q: State the relationship between Gibbs free energy and EMF of the cell. For the reaction: Cu + 2Ag⁺ → Cu²⁺ + 2Ag, E°cell = 0.46V. Calculate ΔrG°.

Answer: ΔrG° = −nFE°cell = −2 × 96487 × 0.46 = −88,768 J mol⁻¹ ≈ −88.8 kJ mol⁻¹
💡Conductance of Electrolytic Solutions
🚰
Electricity is like water through a pipe! Resistance = how hard it is for electricity to flow (narrow pipe). Conductance = how easy it is (wide pipe). Resistivity = property of the material. Conductivity = opposite of resistivity. Easy!

Types of Conductors

Electronic (Metallic) Conductors

  • Charge carriers = free electrons
  • Examples: metals, graphite
  • Conductivity decreases with temperature (electrons scatter more)
  • NO chemical reaction during conduction

Electrolytic (Ionic) Conductors

  • Charge carriers = ions (cations + anions)
  • Examples: molten salts, aqueous solutions, acids
  • Conductivity INCREASES with temperature (more ion movement)
  • Chemical reaction OCCURS at electrodes

Key Quantities and Their Relationships

📐 Resistance (R)
R = ρ × (l/A)

Where ρ (rho) = resistivity (specific resistance) [Ω·m or Ω·cm]
l = length between electrodes, A = cross-sectional area

Units: R in Ohm (Ω)
📐 Conductance (G)
G = 1/R
Units: Siemens (S) or Ω⁻¹ or mho
📐 Conductivity (κ, kappa)
κ = 1/ρ = G × (l/A)

OR κ = G × cell constant (where cell constant = l/A)

Units: S m⁻¹ or S cm⁻¹

Physical meaning: conductance of a 1 m × 1 m × 1 m cube of solution
📐 Molar Conductivity (Λm, Lambda_m)
Λm = κ / c

Where c = molar concentration (mol m⁻³ or mol L⁻¹)

If c is in mol L⁻¹ (= mol/1000 cm³):
Λm = (κ × 1000) / c (when κ in S cm⁻¹, c in mol L⁻¹)

Units: S m² mol⁻¹ or S cm² mol⁻¹
📖 Molar Conductivity (Λm) — Board Definition: Molar conductivity is defined as the conductance of a solution containing one mole of electrolyte placed between two electrodes 1 cm apart with sufficient cross-sectional area to hold the entire solution. It is denoted by Λm and measured in S cm² mol⁻¹.

Worked Examples — Conductivity Calculations

📐 NCERT Example 2.4 — Cell Constant & Molar Conductivity
Resistance of a conductivity cell filled with 0.1 mol L⁻¹ KCl = 100 Ω. When filled with 0.02 mol L⁻¹ KCl the resistance = 520 Ω. Conductivity of 0.1 mol L⁻¹ KCl = 1.29 S m⁻¹. Find conductivity and molar conductivity of 0.02 mol L⁻¹ KCl.
STEP 1: Find Cell Constant Cell constant (G*) = κ × R = 1.29 S m⁻¹ × 100 Ω = 129 m⁻¹ = 1.29 cm⁻¹ STEP 2: Conductivity of 0.02 mol L⁻¹ KCl κ = G*/R = 129 m⁻¹ / 520 Ω = 0.248 S m⁻¹ (OR in cm units: κ = 1.29 cm⁻¹ / 520 = 0.00248 S cm⁻¹) STEP 3: Molar Conductivity c = 0.02 mol L⁻¹ = 0.02 × 1000 mol m⁻³ = 20 mol m⁻³ Λm = κ/c = 0.248 / 20 = 124 × 10⁻⁴ S m² mol⁻¹ OR: Λm = (κ × 1000)/c = (0.00248 × 1000)/0.02 = 124 S cm² mol⁻¹ ✅
📐 NCERT Example 2.5 — From Geometry (Resistivity → Conductivity → Λm)
Resistance of a column of 0.05 mol L⁻¹ NaOH, diameter 1 cm, length 50 cm = 5.55 × 10³ Ω. Find resistivity, conductivity and molar conductivity.
Given: l = 50 cm, radius r = 0.5 cm Area A = πr² = 3.14 × (0.5)² = 0.785 cm² STEP 1: Resistivity ρ = R × A / l = (5550 × 0.785) / 50 = 87.135 Ω cm STEP 2: Conductivity κ = 1/ρ = 1/87.135 = 0.01148 S cm⁻¹ STEP 3: Molar Conductivity Λm = (κ × 1000) / c = (0.01148 × 1000) / 0.05 = 229.6 S cm² mol⁻¹ KEY: Find ρ from geometry → flip to get κ → use Λm = (κ×1000)/c
📐 Cell Constant — Common Board Type (NCERT Exercise 2.9)
The resistance of a conductivity cell containing 0.001 M KCl at 298 K is 1500 Ω. What is the cell constant if conductivity of 0.001 M KCl is 0.146 × 10⁻³ S cm⁻¹?
Cell constant G* = κ × R = 0.146 × 10⁻³ × 1500 = 0.219 cm⁻¹ Remember: G* = κ × R (cell constant calibrates the cell) Then for any unknown: κ(unknown) = G* / R(unknown)

Variation of Conductivity with Concentration

Why conductivity (κ) DECREASES on dilution:

  • Conductivity depends on how many ions are present per unit volume
  • On dilution → more water, fewer ions per unit volume → κ decreases

Why molar conductivity (Λm) INCREASES on dilution:

  • Λm = κ/c → as dilution, c decreases faster than κ
  • Ions are more free to move (less ion-ion interactions)
  • On infinite dilution → Λm reaches maximum: Λ°m (limiting molar conductivity)

Fig 4: Λm vs √c — Strong electrolytes (linear, can extrapolate) vs Weak electrolytes (steep, cannot extrapolate directly)

Measurement of Conductivity

We use a Wheatstone bridge setup (AC current, not DC) because:

  • DC would cause electrolysis and change the solution composition
  • AC current reverses direction rapidly, no net electrolysis
  • Platinum electrodes are used (inert)
Cell Constant
Cell constant = l/A (unit: cm⁻¹)
κ = G × cell constant = (1/R) × (l/A)
📝 Board Question (2022)
Q: The molar conductivity of 0.025 mol L⁻¹ methanoic acid (HCOOH) is 46.1 S cm² mol⁻¹. Calculate its degree of dissociation. Given Λ°m(H⁺) = 349.6, Λ°m(HCOO⁻) = 54.6 S cm² mol⁻¹

Answer: Λ°m = 349.6 + 54.6 = 404.2 S cm² mol⁻¹
α = Λm / Λ°m = 46.1 / 404.2 = 0.114 (11.4%)
⚗️Kohlrausch's Law
📖 Kohlrausch's Law of Independent Migration of Ions: At infinite dilution, each ion makes a definite contribution to the molar conductivity of an electrolyte, irrespective of the nature of the other ion with which it is associated. The limiting molar conductivity (Λ°m) of an electrolyte is equal to the sum of the limiting molar conductivities of the constituent ions.
📐 Kohlrausch's Law
Λ°m = ν₊λ°₊ + ν₋λ°₋

Where:
• ν₊, ν₋ = number of cations and anions per formula unit
• λ°₊, λ°₋ = limiting molar conductivity of cation and anion

Examples:
KCl: Λ°m = λ°(K⁺) + λ°(Cl⁻)
CaCl₂: Λ°m = λ°(Ca²⁺) + 2λ°(Cl⁻)
Al₂(SO₄)₃: Λ°m = 2λ°(Al³⁺) + 3λ°(SO₄²⁻)

Applications of Kohlrausch's Law

  1. Finding Λ°m of weak electrolytes (cannot be directly measured by extrapolation):
    Λ°m(CH₃COOH) = Λ°m(CH₃COONa) + Λ°m(HCl) − Λ°m(NaCl) (EXAMPLE: Using three strong electrolytes to get weak one) Λ°m(NH₄OH) = Λ°m(NH₄Cl) + Λ°m(NaOH) − Λ°m(NaCl)
  2. Calculating degree of dissociation (α) of weak electrolyte:
    α = Λm(c) / Λ°m
    (Λm(c) = molar conductivity at concentration c; Λ°m from Kohlrausch's law)
  3. Calculating Ka of weak acid:
    Ka = cα² / (1−α) ≈ cα² (if α << 1)
  4. Calculating solubility and Ksp of sparingly soluble salts (like AgCl, BaSO₄):
    For a sparingly soluble salt, measure the conductivity of its saturated solution. Subtract the conductivity of pure water. Then:
    Λm(salt) = κ(solution) − κ(water)

    Then use Kohlrausch: Λ°m = λ°(cation) + λ°(anion)

    Solubility (mol L⁻¹) = κ(salt) × 1000 / Λ°m

    Ksp = [cation] × [anion] (from solubility)
    📐 Solubility of AgCl — Board-Level Example

⚡Electrolysis & Faraday's Laws
🏭
Think of charging a phone! When you force electricity INTO a solution, you FORCE non-spontaneous reactions to happen. Faraday figured out exactly HOW MUCH stuff is produced based on how much electricity you pass. This is behind aluminium production, electroplating, and making NaOH!
📖 Electrolysis: The process of using electrical energy to bring about a non-spontaneous chemical reaction in an ionic substance (in molten or dissolved state) is called electrolysis. The device used is called an electrolytic cell.

Products of Electrolysis of NaCl (Aqueous)

Cathode: 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂(g) + 2OH⁻ (water reduced in preference to Na⁺) Anode: 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂(g) + 2e⁻ Overall: 2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O → Cl₂(g) + H₂(g) + 2NaOH(aq) This is the Chlor-alkali process (industrial importance!)

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

📖 First Law: The amount of substance (mass) deposited or liberated at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed through the electrolyte.
m ∝ Q = m = ZQ = ZIt
Where Z = electrochemical equivalent (g C⁻¹)
📖 Second Law: When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of different substances deposited are proportional to their chemical equivalents (equivalent weight = molar mass / valency).
m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂ (E = equivalent weight)

🔋Batteries & Fuel Cells
📱
Your phone battery is a galvanic cell! Primary batteries are like a one-time snack pack — use it, it's gone. Secondary batteries (like your phone's Li-ion) are like a lunchbox — you can recharge and reuse! Fuel cells are like a kitchen — keep feeding it fuel, it keeps making electricity!

Primary Batteries (Non-rechargeable)

1. Dry Cell (Leclanché Cell) — Torches, remotes

Structure: Zinc container (anode) | Ammonium chloride + Zinc chloride paste (electrolyte) | Carbon rod with MnO₂ (cathode)

Anode: Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ Cathode: MnO₂(s) + NH₄⁺(aq) + e⁻ → MnO(OH)(s) + NH₃(g) EMF = ~1.5 V

The MnO₂ oxidizes Mn³⁺ → Mn²⁺? No! It converts MnO₂ (Mn⁴⁺) to MnO(OH) (Mn³⁺). NH₃ produced forms [Zn(NH₃)₄]²⁺ complex with Zn²⁺.

2. Mercury Cell — Hearing aids, medical devices

Structure: Zinc-mercury amalgam (anode) | KOH + ZnO paste | HgO (cathode)

Anode: Zn(s) + 2OH⁻(aq) → ZnO(s) + H₂O(l) + 2e⁻ Cathode: HgO(s) + H₂O(l) + 2e⁻ → Hg(l) + 2OH⁻(aq) Net: Zn + HgO → ZnO + Hg EMF = ~1.35 V (constant! Good for medical devices)

Constant voltage because no change in electrolyte concentration during the reaction!

Secondary Batteries (Rechargeable)

3. Lead-Acid Battery (Car battery) ⚡

6 cells in series → ~12V. Most common rechargeable battery.

During DISCHARGE (galvanic): Anode: Pb(s) + SO₄²⁻(aq) → PbSO₄(s) + 2e⁻ Cathode: PbO₂(s) + 4H⁺(aq) + SO₄²⁻(aq) + 2e⁻ → PbSO₄(s) + 2H₂O(l) Net: Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O During CHARGING (electrolytic, reactions REVERSE): 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ EMF = ~2V per cell
⚠️ During discharge: Both electrodes become PbSO₄ and H₂SO₄ is consumed (density of H₂SO₄ decreases). This is how you check if a car battery is charged!

4. Nickel-Cadmium (NiCd) Battery

Anode: Cd(s) + 2OH⁻(aq) → Cd(OH)₂(s) + 2e⁻ Cathode: NiO(OH)(s) + H₂O(l) + e⁻ → Ni(OH)₂(s) + OH⁻(aq) EMF ≈ 1.2V per cell

Used in cordless phones, cameras. Toxic (cadmium).

Fuel Cells

5. Hydrogen-Oxygen Fuel Cell 🚀

Used in space vehicles, zero-emission cars. Hydrogen fuel continuously fed.

Fig 5: H₂-O₂ Fuel Cell — EMF ≈ 1.23V, 70% efficient, used in NASA spacecraft

Anode (oxidation): 2H₂(g) + 4OH⁻(aq) → 4H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ Cathode (reduction): O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻(aq) Net: 2H₂(g) + O₂(g) → 2H₂O(l) + electrical energy EMF ≈ 1.23 V
📖 Fuel Cell (Board Definition): A fuel cell is a galvanic cell in which the chemical energy of combustion of fuels (like H₂, CH₄, CO) is directly converted into electrical energy. The fuel is continuously supplied to the cell. It has high efficiency (~70%) compared to thermal power plants (~40%). The only product of H₂-O₂ fuel cell is water.

🦀Corrosion – Electrochemical Process
🚗
Rusting is just a galvanic cell gone wrong! When iron is exposed to moist air, different parts of the iron surface act as tiny anode and cathode. Iron dissolves at the anode (oxidizes), and oxygen gets reduced at the cathode. The result? That ugly red rust (Fe₂O₃·xH₂O) on your car!
📖 Corrosion (Board Definition): Corrosion is a process of slow destruction of materials (especially metals) due to action of their environment. In electrochemical corrosion (like rusting of iron), the metal acts as anode and gets oxidized, while another surface region acts as cathode. It is essentially an electrochemical process.

Rusting of Iron – Mechanism

Fig 6: Electrochemical Corrosion — Iron surface acts as mini galvanic cell in the presence of moisture and O₂

Anode (oxidation): Fe(s) → Fe²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ [E° = −(−0.44) = 0.44 V vs SHE] Cathode (reduction): O₂(g) + 2H₂O(l) + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻(aq) Then: Fe²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂ 4Fe(OH)₂ + O₂ + 2H₂O → 4Fe(OH)₃ (brown) 2Fe(OH)₃ → Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (Rust)
⚠️ Why does acid rain accelerate corrosion?
Acid rain lowers pH → increases H⁺ concentration → faster electrochemical reaction → iron corrodes faster. Salt water (near sea) also accelerates corrosion because it improves ion conductivity (better electrolyte).

Prevention of Corrosion

  1. 🎨 Painting / Coating – Barrier from moisture/air (physical protection)
  2. ⚗️ Galvanizing – Coating iron with zinc. Even if zinc coat breaks, Zn acts as sacrificial anode (Zn has more negative E°, so Zn oxidizes instead of Fe)
  3. 🔩 Alloy formation – Stainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni). Chromium forms a protective Cr₂O₃ layer
  4. 🔌 Cathodic protection – Connect iron to a more active metal (like Mg or Zn) which acts as sacrificial anode. Used for underground pipelines, ships
  5. 🛢️ Using inhibitors – Adding chemicals that slow down corrosion reactions
🧠 Galvanizing trick: Galvanizing puts ZINC on IRON. Zinc has lower (more negative) E° than Iron → Zinc is more active → If exposed, Zinc preferentially oxidizes and PROTECTS the iron. This is called cathodic protection or sacrificial protection!
📝 Board Question (2022, 2018)
Q: Explain why rusting of iron is an electrochemical process. How can it be prevented by galvanizing?

Answer: Rusting involves oxidation at anode (Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻) and reduction of O₂ at cathode, forming an electrochemical cell on the iron surface. In galvanizing, zinc coating on iron acts as a sacrificial anode because Zn (E° = −0.76V) is more easily oxidized than Fe (E° = −0.44V). Even if the Zn layer cracks, Zn still preferentially oxidizes, protecting Fe.

🧠HOTS – Higher Order Thinking Questions
🔥 HOTS Level 1
Q: Can copper be stored in a silver nitrate solution? Justify using E° values.

A: E°(Cu²⁺/Cu) = +0.34V and E°(Ag⁺/Ag) = +0.80V. Since E°(Ag⁺/Ag) > E°(Cu²⁺/Cu), Ag⁺ is a stronger oxidizing agent than Cu²⁺. So Ag⁺ can oxidize Cu metal: Cu + 2Ag⁺ → Cu²⁺ + 2Ag. E°cell = 0.80 − 0.34 = +0.46V (positive, spontaneous). Therefore, copper CANNOT be safely stored in silver nitrate solution — it will dissolve and silver will deposit.
🔥 HOTS Level 2
Q: The E° of a cell is 0.34V at 298K. If the temperature is raised to 308K, how does E° change? (Assume ΔS = 50 J mol⁻¹ K⁻¹, n = 2)

A: Using: ΔrG° = −nFE° = ΔH° − TΔS°, so E° = (TΔS° − ΔH°)/nF. As T increases, if ΔS° > 0, E° increases. Since dE°/dT = ΔS°/nF = 50/(2×96487) = 2.59 × 10⁻⁴ V/K. ΔE° = 2.59 × 10⁻⁴ × 10 = +0.00259V. New E° ≈ 0.34 + 0.003 = 0.343V. Temperature coefficient of EMF depends on entropy change of the reaction.
🔥 HOTS Level 3
Q: Why is the mercury cell preferred over the dry cell in hearing aids, even though both have similar voltage?

A: The mercury cell provides a constant voltage throughout its entire discharge period (~1.35V). This is because its cell reaction (Zn + HgO → ZnO + Hg) does not change the electrolyte concentration. In contrast, the dry cell's voltage drops steadily as NH₄Cl is consumed. Hearing aids require precise, steady voltage for accurate audio amplification — any voltage fluctuation would affect sound quality. Additionally, the mercury cell has a much longer shelf life and higher energy density per unit volume, making it ideal for small medical devices.
🔥 HOTS Level 4
Q: Stainless steel does not corrode but aluminium in thermite bombs easily reacts with Fe₂O₃. Explain using electrode potentials.

A: Stainless steel (Fe + Cr) resists corrosion because Cr forms a thin, adherent, self-healing Cr₂O₃ passivating layer that prevents further oxidation. In thermite reaction (2Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe), E°(Al³⁺/Al) = −1.66V and E°(Fe³⁺/Fe³⁺) = much more positive. Al is a stronger reducing agent and reduces Fe₂O₃ thermally (not electrochemically) because the high temperature overcomes kinetic barriers. This is also why Al cans corrode in acid (Al dissolves) but not in air (protective Al₂O₃ layer).
🔥 HOTS Level 5
Q: The molar conductivity of a solution decreases on addition of more electrolyte but increases on dilution. Isn't this contradictory?

A: There's no contradiction. Conductivity (κ) and molar conductivity (Λm = κ/c) are different! When you add more electrolyte: more ions → κ increases BUT c increases even faster (at high concentrations, interionic interactions reduce ion mobility, so κ doesn't increase proportionally). Since Λm = κ/c, when c increases faster than κ, Λm decreases. On dilution: c decreases and ions become more mobile (less interionic attraction) → although κ decreases, the mobility term κ/c increases → Λm increases. At infinite dilution (Λ°m), all interionic forces vanish, and each ion moves independently (Kohlrausch's law).
📝 HOTS Board-style (3M)
Q: A galvanic cell has E°cell = 0. Does this mean no reaction occurs? What if concentrations are non-standard?

A: If E°cell = 0, it means neither species is preferentially reduced — the reaction has equal tendency in both directions, i.e., Kc ≈ 1. It does NOT mean no reaction. At non-standard concentrations, the Nernst equation gives: E = 0 − (0.0592/n) log Q. If Q < 1 (reactants dominate), E > 0 → forward reaction occurs. If Q > 1 (products dominate), E < 0 → reverse reaction occurs. So even when E°cell = 0, the cell can still work at non-standard conditions!

📝 Important Theory Questions

Theory Q1
What is the difference between electronic and electrolytic conductors? How does temperature affect each?
Electronic conductors carry current by movement of free electrons (metals, graphite). Conductivity DECREASES with temperature rise (electrons scatter more at lattice). Electrolytic conductors carry current by movement of ions in solution or melt. Conductivity INCREASES with temperature (ions become more mobile, more dissociation occurs).
Theory Q2
Why can't we use DC current to measure the conductance of electrolytic solutions?
DC current would cause electrolysis of the solution, changing its composition (ions are discharged at electrodes, concentration changes). This would give inaccurate readings. AC current reverses direction so frequently that no net electrolysis occurs and the solution composition remains unchanged. Typically 550-5000 Hz AC is used with platinum electrodes.
Theory Q3
Why does the molar conductivity of a weak electrolyte increase sharply at very low concentrations?
Weak electrolytes (like CH₃COOH) are poorly dissociated at high concentrations. On dilution, the degree of dissociation (α) increases dramatically (by Le Chatelier's principle). More ions are produced per mole of electrolyte, so Λm increases steeply. At infinite dilution, complete dissociation occurs and Λm reaches its maximum (Λ°m). This is why we cannot extrapolate the Λm vs √c curve for weak electrolytes like we do for strong ones.
Theory Q4
What happens to the EMF of a Daniel cell as the cell operates?
As the cell operates, Zn²⁺ concentration increases in the anode compartment and Cu²⁺ concentration decreases in the cathode compartment. From the Nernst equation: E = E° − (0.0592/2)log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺]). As [Zn²⁺] increases and [Cu²⁺] decreases, the ratio [Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺] increases, log term increases, so E decreases. Eventually E = 0 (equilibrium reached, Kc attained) and the battery "dies."
