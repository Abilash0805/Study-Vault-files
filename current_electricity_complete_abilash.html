3.1 Introduction

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

In Chapter 1, charges just sat still — like furniture. Now they start running — like kids in a school corridor. That running of charges is called Electric Current! 🏃

⚡ What is Electric Current?

When electric charges are in motion, they constitute an electric current.
🌩️ Lightning = Charges flowing from clouds to earth — not steady (sudden burst)
🔦 Torch / Clock = Charges flowing steadily. This chapter focuses on STEADY currents.

🌊

River Analogy

Water = Electrons · Flow = Current · Narrow river = High resistance · Wide river = Low resistance

🚶

Crowd in Mall

More people walking faster = More current · Obstacles = Resistance · Battery = Security guard pushing people forward

3.2 Electric Current

📖 Board-Ready Definition

Electric current is defined as the net charge flowing through a cross-section of a conductor per unit time. For steady current: I = Q/t. For varying current: I(t) = lim(ΔQ/Δt) as Δt→0. SI unit: Ampere (A).

Electric Current — Two Forms
I = q/t   (steady)   |   I(t) = dQ/dt   (varying)

If result is negative → current flows in backward direction

📐 About the Unit — Ampere

📌 1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second  |  SI Base Unit (defined via magnetic effects)
🏠 Domestic appliances → order of Amperes  |  🌩️ Lightning → tens of thousands of A
🧠 Nerve signals → microamperes (μA)  |  ⌚ Wristwatch → milliamperes (mA)

🧠 Memory Trick

"PEN" Rule: Positive charge + conventional current → East (right). Electrons go West (left). Conventional current is always opposite to electron flow direction! 🖊️

🔌

3.3 Electric Currents in Conductors

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Imagine a classroom. Usually kids (electrons) run around randomly — no direction. Now the teacher (electric field) says "Everyone walk to the door!" They all drift slowly in one direction = Current flows! 🏫

🧲 Why Do Metals Conduct?

In metals, some electrons become "free electrons" — not attached to any atom. They wander freely.
📌 Ionosphere — free charged particles exist naturally
📌 Metals — free electrons are current carriers
📌 Electrolytic solutions — both + and – ions carry current
📌 Insulators — electrons tightly bound, no current

⚠️ Important

If you only put +Q/−Q at ends of a conductor, current flows briefly then STOPS (charges neutralize). You need a battery/cell to continuously supply charges and maintain steady current!

3.4 Ohm's Law

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

More pressure (voltage) = more water flow (current). Thin clogged pipe (resistance) = less flow even at high pressure. V = IR is exactly this! 💧

📖 Board-Ready Definition

Ohm's Law: The electric current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its ends, provided physical conditions remain constant. V ∝ I → V = IR. R = resistance. SI unit: Ohm (Ω). 1Ω = 1 V/A.

📐 Derivation: R = ρl/A → Vector form of Ohm's Law

1
Double length: Two slabs in series → V_total = 2V, same I → R_C = 2V/I = 2R → R ∝ l
2
Halve area: Slab split lengthwise → each carries I/2, same V → R₁ = 2V/I = 2R → R ∝ 1/A
3
Combining: R ∝ l/A → R = ρl/A where ρ = resistivity (material property only)
4
V = IR = I × ρl/A. Current density j = I/A. Field E = V/l.
→ El = (jA)(ρl/A) = jρl → E = jρ or j = σE where σ = 1/ρ = conductivity
Key Equations
R = ρl/A  |  E = jρ  |  j = σE  |  σ = 1/ρ

🔬 Key Quantities

QuantitySymbolFormulaUnit
ResistanceRV/I = ρl/AΩ (Ohm)
ResistivityρRA/l = m/(ne²τ)Ω·m
Current DensityjI/A = σEA/m²
Conductivityσ1/ρ = ne²τ/mS/m
🧠 Memory Trick

Resistivity = "Character" of a material (lazy person is lazy everywhere — doesn't depend on their size!)
Resistance = How hard it is to push through a specific piece of wire (shape matters!) 🏋️

3.5 Drift of Electrons & Origin of Resistivity

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Walking through a crowded market (electrons in a metal). People keep bumping into you (ions!). Even with someone pushing you (E field), you zigzag slowly. That slow net movement = drift velocity! 🛒

📐 Derivation: Drift Velocity & Origin of Ohm's Law (σ = ne²τ/m)

1
Force on electron: F = −eE. Acceleration: a = −eE/m
2
Velocity of iᵗʰ electron at time t: V_i = v_i + (−eE/m)·t_i. Post-collision velocity v_i is random (average = 0). Average of t_i = τ (relaxation time).
3
Average velocity = drift velocity: v_d = −eEτ/m (constant! independent of time despite acceleration)
4
In time Δt, electrons within |v_d|Δt from area A cross it. Number = n·A·|v_d|·Δt. Charge = neA|v_d|Δt.
I = neAv_d
5
Substitute v_d = eEτ/m: I = (ne²Aτ/m)E. Since j = I/A and j = σE:
σ = ne²τ/m and ρ = m/(ne²τ) — Ohm's law derived from first principles! ✓
Drift Velocity Results
v_d = eEτ/m  |  I = neAv_d  |  σ = ne²τ/m  |  ρ = m/(ne²τ)

3.5.1 Mobility (μ)

📖 Definition

Mobility = magnitude of drift velocity per unit electric field. μ = |v_d|/E = eτ/m. SI unit: m²/Vs. Always positive.

Higher τ → higher μ → electrons drift more freely under same field.

❓ Famous Conceptual Q&A (NCERT Example 3.2)

Q: Drift speed is mm/s — how does current establish instantly? → E field travels at speed of light! Every electron drifts simultaneously — no need for electrons from one end to reach the other.

Q: Force accelerates electrons — why constant drift speed? → Each electron accelerates until it collides with an ion, losing drift speed. Cycle repeats. Average = constant drift.

Q: How can large currents flow if drift is tiny? → Electron density n ≈ 10²⁹/m³ is ENORMOUS!

Q: Do all electrons drift in same direction? → NO! Drift is superposed on large random thermal velocities.

🧠 Memory Trick

τ = Relaxation Time = "Rest time between crashes" (like a racing car that crashes and starts again). Smaller τ = more crashes = more resistance. Higher temp = more crashes = τ↓ = ρ↑ for metals! 🏎️

🔑 Special Alloys

Nichrome, Manganin, Constantan have very weak dependence of resistivity on temperature (α ≈ 0). Used in standard wire-wound resistors and heater coils — their resistance stays stable even when hot.

3.8 Temperature Dependence of Resistivity

📖 Board-Ready Definition

For metallic conductors: ρ_T = ρ₀[1 + α(T − T₀)]. α = temperature coefficient of resistivity. For metals, α > 0 (ρ increases with T). For semiconductors, α < 0 (ρ decreases with T). R_T = R₀[1 + α(T − T₀)] for resistance.

🌡️ Formula Breakdown: ρ_T = ρ₀[1 + α(T − T₀)]

TermMeaningSign for Metals
ρ_TResistivity at temperature T—
ρ₀Resistivity at reference temp T₀Positive
αTemperature coefficient of resistivityα > 0 (metals), α < 0 (semiconductors)
T − T₀Temperature change (in K or °C)Either unit works

📌 Semiconductors: increasing T frees more electrons → n increases → dominates over τ decrease → ρ falls

🧠 Memory Trick — M.S. Dhoni Method

Metals → More temp = More resistance (α > 0)
Semiconductors → Surprisingly opposite! More temp = Less resistance (α < 0) 🏏

3.9 Electrical Energy & Power

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Water hitting a wheel converts pressure-energy to spinning. Similarly, charges flowing through a resistor give up their energy as heat. Rate of energy release = Power! 💧→⚙️→🔥

📖 Board-Ready Definition

Electric power = energy dissipated per unit time. P = IV = I²R = V²/R. SI unit: Watt (W). Energy: W = Pt. Commercial unit: 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.

📐 Derivation of P = IV

1
Conductor AB, current I, potential diff V = V(A)−V(B) > 0.
2
In time Δt, charge ΔQ = I·Δt flows from A to B. Potential energy change: ΔU = −ΔQ·V = −IV·Δt (negative = energy lost by charges)
3
Energy doesn't speed up electrons (constant drift). It goes into collisions with ions → heat. Energy dissipated: ΔW = IV·Δt
4
P = ΔW/Δt = IV. Using V = IR: P = I²R. Using I = V/R: P = V²/R
Power — 3 Forms
P = IV = I²R = V²/R

🏭 Why High Voltage for Power Transmission?

P_loss in cables = I²R_c = P²R_c/V² → P_loss ∝ 1/V²
India uses 220kV–765kV for transmission → tiny I → tiny I²R loss → efficient! Transformer steps down to 230V at homes. That's why high-tension lines have danger signs! ⚡

3.10 Cells, EMF & Internal Resistance

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

A cell is like a pump for electricity. EMF (ε) = how hard it pumps. But the pump has internal friction (r). Actual pressure outside (terminal voltage V) is less than maximum. More current = more energy wasted inside! 🚰

📖 EMF Definition

EMF (ε) = potential difference between positive and negative electrodes when no current flows (open circuit). ε = V₊ + V₋. EMF is a potential difference, NOT a force! Unit: Volt.

📖 Internal Resistance

Internal resistance (r) = resistance offered by the electrolyte of a cell to the flow of current. Unit: Ohm (Ω). Dry cells have MUCH higher r than liquid electrolytic cells.

📐 Derivation: Terminal Voltage V = ε − Ir

1
Open circuit (I = 0): V = ε (full EMF available, no internal drop)
2
With load R: current I flows. Internal drop = Ir. Terminal voltage V = ε − Ir.
3
Since V = IR (Ohm's law for external): IR = ε − Ir → I = ε/(R+r)
EMF and Terminal Voltage
V = ε − Ir  |  I = ε/(R+r)  |  I_max = ε/r  |  V_charge = ε + Ir

📊 Terminal Voltage in Different Conditions

ConditionTerminal VoltageMeaning
Open circuit (I = 0)V = εFull EMF — no internal drop
Under load (I flows)V = ε − IrV < ε — some lost to internal r
Short circuit (R = 0)V = 0, I = ε/rAll voltage lost inside — dangerous!
ChargingV = ε + IrV > ε — external source pushes current in
🧠 Memory Trick

EMF is what the cell PROMISES. Terminal voltage is what it DELIVERS. The difference is "stolen" by internal resistance r — like a shop promising ₹100 discount but giving ₹80 due to "handling charges"! 🛒

3.11 Cells in Series & Parallel

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Series = Stacking batteries in a remote — total push (EMF) adds up!
Parallel = Multiple pumps side-by-side — same pressure, more flow capacity, lower internal friction. 🔋🔋

📐 Series Cells: ε_eq = ε₁+ε₂, r_eq = r₁+r₂

1
V_AB = ε₁ − Ir₁   V_BC = ε₂ − Ir₂
2
V_AC = V_AB + V_BC = (ε₁+ε₂) − I(r₁+r₂)
3
Compare with V = ε_eq − Ir_eq: ε_eq = ε₁+ε₂ and r_eq = r₁+r₂
⚠️ Opposing Cells!

If connected opposing: ε_eq = ε₁ − ε₂. The smaller EMF fights the larger one.

📐 Parallel Cells: 1/r_eq = 1/r₁+1/r₂ and ε_eq/r_eq = ε₁/r₁+ε₂/r₂

1
I = I₁ + I₂ (KCL). Same V across both: I₁ = (ε₁−V)/r₁, I₂ = (ε₂−V)/r₂
2
I = (ε₁/r₁ + ε₂/r₂) − V(1/r₁ + 1/r₂)
3
V = [(ε₁r₂+ε₂r₁)/(r₁+r₂)] − I·[r₁r₂/(r₁+r₂)] → ε_eq = (ε₁r₂+ε₂r₁)/(r₁+r₂), r_eq = r₁r₂/(r₁+r₂)
🧠 Memory Trick

Series = STACK (like books) → everything adds up 📚
Parallel = SPREAD (like highway lanes) → more lanes, less congestion, same speed limit 🛣️

3.12 Kirchhoff's Rules

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Junction Rule = Traffic Rule 🚦 Cars in = Cars out at any junction. No cars appear or vanish!
Loop Rule = Bank Account 🏦 Start at ₹0, spend/earn, return to same account = ₹0 net change. Same with voltage around a loop!

📖 Junction Rule (KCL)

At any junction, sum of currents entering = sum of currents leaving. ΣI_in = ΣI_out. Based on: conservation of charge.

📖 Loop Rule (KVL)

Algebraic sum of changes in potential around any closed loop is zero. ΣΔV = 0. Based on: conservation of energy.

📐 How to Apply Kirchhoff's Rules

1
Label all branch currents with symbols and assumed directions
2
Apply KCL at each junction: ΣI_in = ΣI_out (reduces unknowns)
3
Apply KVL to independent closed loops: ΣΔV = 0
4
Solve simultaneous equations. Negative current → actual direction is opposite to assumed

🎲 Cube of 12 Resistors (Example 3.5)

12 equal resistors (R each) form a cube. Battery across body diagonal. By symmetry, current I enters at one corner, splits into 3 equal branches (I/3 each at first level, I/6 middle, I/3 last).
KVL loop ABCC'EA: −IR − (1/2)IR − IR + ε = 0 → ε = (5/2)IR
Total current = 3I. R_eq = ε/(3I) = 5R/6
For R = 1Ω, R_eq = 5/6 Ω. For ε = 10V, total I = 12A.

🧠 Memory Trick

KCL = Traffic Junction 🚦 — cars can't pile up at intersections!
KVL = Round-trip ✈️ — Delhi→Mumbai→Delhi: altitude change = 0 net!

3.13 Wheatstone Bridge

🧒 Explain Like I'm 10

Like a balance scale ⚖️ — when both sides equal, middle pointer shows zero. The Wheatstone Bridge: when 4 resistors are in the right ratio, NO current flows through the middle wire (galvanometer = 0). Bridge is balanced! Used to find unknown resistance precisely.

📖 Board-Ready Definition

A Wheatstone bridge measures an unknown resistance precisely using four resistors R₁, R₂, R₃, R₄ in a diamond arrangement. Battery across diagonal AC, galvanometer across BD. At balance (I_g = 0): R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄. Unknown R₄ = R₃(R₂/R₁).

📐 Derivation: Balance Condition R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄

1
At balance: I_g = 0 → I₁ = I₃ and I₂ = I₄ (KCL)
2
Loop ADBA: −I₁R₁ + I₂R₂ = 0 → I₁/I₂ = R₂/R₁ ...(i)
3
Loop CBDC: I₁R₃ = I₂R₄ → I₁/I₂ = R₄/R₃ ...(ii)
4
From (i) and (ii): R₂/R₁ = R₄/R₃ → R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄
Balance Condition
R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄   ⟺   R₁·R₄ = R₂·R₃   |   R₄ = R₃·(R₂/R₁)

⚡ Unbalanced Bridge (Example 3.7)

AB=100Ω, BC=10Ω, CD=5Ω, DA=60Ω. Galvanometer G=15Ω across BD. Battery=10V.
Check: R₁/R₂ = 100/60 ≠ 10/5 → Unbalanced → I_g ≠ 0. Apply KVL to 3 meshes (BADB, BCDB, ADCEA):
20I₁ + 3I_g − 12I₂ = 0 ...(a) | 2I₁ − 6I_g − I₂ = 0 ...(b) | 13I₂ + I_g = 2 ...(c)
Solving: I₂ = 31.5·I_g → 410.5·I_g = 2 → I_g = 4.87 mA

🧠 Memory Trick

"Products of Opposites Equal": R₁×R₄ = R₂×R₃ at balance. Cross-multiply the ratio! Like a see-saw — opposite corners balance each other. ⚖️

Points to Ponder — 6 Deep Insights

Point 1
Current is a SCALAR — Not a Vector!

Even with direction arrows, current does NOT obey vector addition. I = j · ΔS (dot product of two vectors = scalar). Board trap: "Is current scalar or vector?" → SCALAR! 📌

Point 2
V = IR Defines R — NOT Ohm's Law!

V = IR holds for ALL conductors — it defines R = V/I. Ohm's law says R is CONSTANT (I-V is linear). A diode has R = V/I at every point, but R changes with V → Ohm's law fails, but V = IR is still valid. E = ρj is another statement: material obeys Ohm's law when ρ doesn't depend on |E| or direction.

Point 3
All Homogeneous Conductors Obey Ohm's Law — Up to a Limit!

Silver and pure Ge both obey Ohm's law within some field range. But at very strong fields, ALL materials deviate — τ itself becomes field-dependent, breaking linearity.

Point 4
Electron Motion = Random Thermal Motion + Drift Due to E

Random thermal motion averages to ZERO. Only E causes drift. v_d is purely due to applied field. Thermal speeds (~10⁵ m/s) >> drift speeds (~10⁻³ m/s).

Point 5
j = ρv Does NOT Apply to Total Current Density!

In neutral wire: ρ₊ = −ρ₋ → ρ_total = 0. But j = ρ₊v₊ + ρ₋v₋. Ions barely move (v₊ ≈ 0), so j ≈ ρ₋v₋ ≠ 0. Apply j = ρv to EACH carrier type separately, not to total charge density.

Point 6
KCL Works Regardless of Wire Geometry!

Bending or reshaping a wire does NOT change KCL. It's based on charge conservation — a fundamental law, independent of geometry. Works in any shape, dimension, or orientation.

🧠 All 6 in One Line Each

1️⃣ Current=SCALAR (j·ΔS dot product)
2️⃣ V=IR defines R; Ohm's law says R=constant
3️⃣ All materials obey Ohm's law — up to field limit
4️⃣ Drift only from E; random motion cancels
5️⃣ j=ρv for each carrier separately, not total
6️⃣ KCL works regardless of wire shape

  4. V = ε − Ir (discharge); V = ε + Ir (charging)
  5. I = ε/(R+r); I_max = ε/r when R=0
  6. Series cells: ε_eq=Σε, r_eq=Σr
  7. Parallel cells: 1/r_eq=Σ(1/r), ε_eq/r_eq=Σ(ε/r)
  8. KCL: ΣI_in = ΣI_out (charge conservation)
  9. KVL: ΣΔV = 0 (energy conservation)
  10. Wheatstone balance: R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄ when I_g=0
  11. Cube: R_eq = 5R/6 (12 resistors, body diagonal)
  12. Max power at R_ext = r_int → P_max = ε²/4r

NCERT Exercises — All 9 Solved

Exercise 3.1
Car battery: EMF = 12V, internal resistance = 0.4Ω. Find maximum current that can be drawn.

Concept: Max current when R = 0 (short circuit). I_max = ε/r = 12/0.4 = 30 A

📌 This is why short-circuiting a car battery is dangerous — 30A causes fire/explosion!

Exercise 3.2
Battery: EMF 10V, r = 3Ω, current = 0.5A. Find (a) external resistance R (b) terminal voltage.

(a) I = ε/(R+r) → R+r = 10/0.5 = 20Ω → R = 20−3 = 17 Ω

(b) V = ε−Ir = 10−(0.5)(3) = 10−1.5 = 8.5 V  (= IR = 0.5×17 ✓)

Exercise 3.3
Heating element: R₀=100Ω at 27°C, R_T=117Ω. α = 1.70×10⁻⁴ °C⁻¹. Find temperature T.

R_T = R₀[1+α(T−T₀)] → 117 = 100[1 + 1.70×10⁻⁴×(T−27)]

0.17 = 1.70×10⁻⁴×(T−27) → T−27 = 1000 → T = 1027°C

Exercise 3.4
Wire: length 15m, A = 6.0×10⁻⁷ m², resistance = 5.0Ω. Find resistivity.

ρ = RA/l = (5.0 × 6.0×10⁻⁷)/15 = 30×10⁻⁷/15 = 2.0 × 10⁻⁷ Ω·m

Exercise 3.5
Silver wire: R = 2.1Ω at 27.5°C and R = 2.7Ω at 100°C. Find temperature coefficient α of silver.

2.7 = 2.1[1+α(100−27.5)] → 2.7/2.1 = 1 + 72.5α → 0.2857 = 72.5α → α = 3.9 × 10⁻³ °C⁻¹

Exercise 3.6
Nichrome element on 230V: initial I = 3.2A, steady I = 2.8A. Room temp = 27°C, α = 1.70×10⁻⁴ °C⁻¹. Find steady temperature.

R₁ = 230/3.2 = 71.9Ω   R₂ = 230/2.8 = 82.1Ω

T₂−T₁ = (R₂−R₁)/(R₁×α) = (82.1−71.9)/(71.9×1.70×10⁻⁴) = 10.2/0.01223 ≈ 834°C

T₂ = 834 + 27 = 861°C

Exercise 3.7
Find current in each branch of the bridge network (Fig 3.20): AB=10Ω, BC=5Ω, CD=10Ω, DA=5Ω, AC-middle=5Ω, battery=10V.

Check balance: AB/DA = 10/5 = 2; BC/DC = 5/10 = 0.5 → NOT balanced → full Kirchhoff needed.

NCERT results: Branch AB: 1A | BC: 0.5A | AD: 1A | DC: 1A | Middle AC: 0.5A

Method: KCL at junctions, KVL for 3 independent loops, solve 3 simultaneous equations.

Exercise 3.8
Battery (8V, 0.5Ω) charged by 120V DC using series resistor 15.5Ω. Find terminal voltage. Why use series resistor?

Charging current I = (120−8)/(0.5+15.5) = 112/16 = 7 A

Terminal voltage during charging: V = ε + Ir = 8 + (7)(0.5) = 8 + 3.5 = 11.5 V

Series resistor: limits charging current to safe value. Without it, I = 112/0.5 = 224A → battery destroyed! 🔋

Exercise 3.9
Copper: n = 8.5×10²⁸ m⁻³, wire length = 3.0m, A = 2.0×10⁻⁶ m², I = 3.0A. How long for electron to drift from one end to other?

v_d = I/(neA) = 3.0/(8.5×10²⁸ × 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ × 2.0×10⁻⁶) = 3.0/27200 ≈ 1.1 × 10⁻⁴ m/s

t = l/v_d = 3.0/(1.1×10⁻⁴) ≈ 2.7 × 10⁴ s ≈ 7.5 hours!

📌 A single electron takes 7.5 HOURS to drift 3m! Yet light turns on instantly — E field propagates at speed of light, all electrons drift simultaneously! ✨

Solved Numerical Problems

📊 Numerical 1 — Drift Velocity (NCERT Eg 3.1)
Copper wire A = 1.0×10⁻⁷ m², I = 1.5A. Density = 9.0×10³ kg/m³, atomic mass = 63.5u. Find drift speed.

n = (6.022×10²³/63.5)×9.0×10⁶ = 8.5×10²⁸ m⁻³

v_d = I/(neA) = 1.5/(8.5×10²⁸×1.6×10⁻¹⁹×1.0×10⁻⁷) = 1.5/(1.36×10³) = 1.1×10⁻³ m/s = 1.1 mm/s

📌 Drift is incredibly slow — yet current is instantaneous because E field travels at ~3×10⁸ m/s!

📊 Numerical 2 — Resistance when Stretched
Wire of R=10Ω, length 2m, A=2×10⁻⁶ m². Find: (a) resistivity (b) resistance when stretched to double length.

(a) ρ = RA/l = 10×2×10⁻⁶/2 = 1×10⁻⁵ Ω·m

(b) Stretched: l' = 4m, volume constant → A' = A/2 = 10⁻⁶ m²

R' = ρl'/A' = 10⁻⁵×4/10⁻⁶ = 40 Ω → (n times length: R increases n² times)

📊 Numerical 3 — EMF and Terminal Voltage
Battery: EMF 12V, r = 0.5Ω, R = 5.5Ω. Find: (a) current (b) terminal voltage (c) power wasted internally.

(a) I = 12/(5.5+0.5) = 12/6 = 2 A

(b) V = ε−Ir = 12−(2)(0.5) = 11 V

(c) P_internal = I²r = 4×0.5 = 2 W (Check: P_ext = 4×5.5=22W. Total = 24W = εI = 12×2 ✓)

📊 Numerical 4 — Cells in Series vs Parallel
Three cells, each 2V, 1Ω, connected to R=3Ω. Compare current when (a) series (b) parallel.

(a) Series: ε_eq=6V, r_eq=3Ω. I = 6/(3+3) = 1 A

(b) Parallel: ε_eq=2V, r_eq=r/n=1/3 Ω. I = 2/(3+1/3) = 2/(10/3) = 0.6 A

📌 Series gives more current here (external R large). Parallel better when R is very small (heavy loads).

📊 Numerical 5 — Wheatstone Bridge
Wheatstone bridge: P=10Ω, Q=15Ω, R=20Ω. Bridge balanced. Find unknown S. With 5V battery (no r), find battery current.

P/Q = R/S → S = Q×R/P = 15×20/10 = 30 Ω

At balance: Branch 1 (P+R): 10+20=30Ω; Branch 2 (Q+S): 15+30=45Ω

R_eq = 30×45/(30+45) = 1350/75 = 18Ω. I_battery = 5/18 ≈ 0.278 A

📊 Numerical 6 — Power Transmission
Power station generates 10MW. Transmission cable R_c = 10Ω. Compare power loss at V=10kV vs V=100kV.

At 10kV: I = 10×10⁶/10⁴ = 1000A. P_loss = I²R = (1000)²×10 = 10MW = 100% wasted! 😱

At 100kV: I = 10×10⁶/10⁵ = 100A. P_loss = (100)²×10 = 0.1MW = only 1%! 🎉

Stepping up by 10× reduces loss by 100×! THIS is why high-tension lines exist!

📊 Numerical 7 — Relaxation Time
Copper: n=8.5×10²⁸ m⁻³, ρ=1.7×10⁻⁸ Ω·m. Find relaxation time τ.

ρ = m/(ne²τ) → τ = m/(ne²ρ)

τ = (9.1×10⁻³¹)/(8.5×10²⁸ × (1.6×10⁻¹⁹)² × 1.7×10⁻⁸)

= (9.1×10⁻³¹)/(3.7×10⁻¹⁷) = 2.46 × 10⁻¹⁴ s

📌 Relaxation time ~10⁻¹⁴ s — extremely short!

📜 CBSE 2024
2 Marks
Q: Is current a scalar or a vector? Justify with the definition I = j·ΔS.

Current is a scalar. I = j·ΔS is a dot product of two vectors — result is scalar. Current does NOT obey vector addition law. Even though we assign a direction, it's just to indicate sense of flow, not a vector property.

📜 CBSE 2023
5 Marks
Q: Derive the expression for drift velocity. Hence derive the relation σ = ne²τ/m and prove Ohm's law from first principles.

v_d = eEτ/m: a=eE/m; V_i = v_i + a·t_i; average: v_d = 0 − eEτ/m = −eEτ/m (drift opposite to E)
I = neAv_d (charge crossing area A per second)
Substitute: I = neA·(eEτ/m) = (ne²Aτ/m)·E. Since j = I/A = σE: σ = ne²τ/m
Since σ = 1/ρ and V = El, I = V/R: this is exactly Ohm's law V = IR with R = ρl/A ✓

📜 CBSE 2023
3 Marks
Q: Define resistivity. Write its SI unit. Derive R = ρl/A. On what factors does resistivity depend?

Resistivity ρ = inherent property of material = RA/l. Unit: Ω·m. Derivation: R∝l (doubling l doubles R); R∝1/A (halving A doubles R) → R=ρl/A where ρ is material-specific. Depends on: (1) Material (2) Temperature (3) Pressure. Does NOT depend on shape or size.

📜 CBSE 2022
3 Marks
Q: State Kirchhoff's rules. Using them, derive the balanced condition for the Wheatstone bridge.

KCL: ΣI_in=ΣI_out (charge conservation). KVL: ΣΔV=0 (energy conservation).
At balance I_g=0: KCL gives I₁=I₃, I₂=I₄. KVL on ADBA: I₁R₁=I₂R₂ ...(i). KVL on CBDC: I₁R₃=I₂R₄ ...(ii). Dividing: R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄

📜 CBSE 2022
2 Marks
Q: Two wires A and B: same material, A is twice as long and twice the diameter. Find R_A/R_B.

l_A=2l, d_A=2d → A_A=π(2r)²=4A. R_A=ρ(2l)/(4A)=ρl/(2A). R_B=ρl/A. R_A/R_B = (ρl/2A)/(ρl/A) = 1:2

📜 CBSE 2021
2 Marks
Q: How does resistance of metallic conductor change with temperature? Give reason.

Resistance INCREASES with temperature. Reason: Higher T → ions vibrate more → more frequent electron-ion collisions → τ decreases → ρ=m/(ne²τ) increases → R increases. Formula: R_T=R₀[1+α(T−T₀)], α>0 for metals.

📜 CBSE 2021
3 Marks
Q: (a) Define mobility. (b) Derive expression for resistivity in terms of n and τ. (c) How does mobility change with temperature?

(a) Mobility μ = magnitude of drift velocity per unit electric field = v_d/E = eτ/m. Unit: m²/Vs. Always positive.
(b) From v_d=eEτ/m and I=neAv_d: j=σE where σ=ne²τ/m → ρ=m/(ne²τ)
(c) Temperature↑ → τ↓ → μ=eτ/m decreases for metals

I = (120−8)/(0.5+15.5) = 112/16 = 7A. Terminal voltage = ε+Ir = 8+3.5 = 11.5V. Purpose: limits charging current to safe value. Without it, I=224A → battery destroyed.

📜 CBSE 2019
2 Marks
Q: Why is KCL valid regardless of wire shape or orientation?

KCL is based on conservation of charge — a fundamental law of nature. Charge cannot accumulate at any junction in steady state. Since charge conservation holds in any geometry, KCL is valid regardless of wire shape, orientation, or material. It is topological, not geometric.

📜 CBSE 2017
2 Marks
Q: When electrons drift from lower to higher potential, do all free electrons move in the same direction?

NO. Drift velocity (~10⁻³ m/s) is superposed on large random thermal velocities (~10⁵ m/s). Each electron moves randomly — but the average has a tiny net drift. So not all electrons move in the same direction at any instant.

HOTS — Higher Order Thinking

🔥 HOTS 1
Two bulbs rated 60W/220V and 100W/220V connected in series across 220V. Which glows brighter?

R₁(60W) = V²/P = (220)²/60 = 807Ω; R₂(100W) = (220)²/100 = 484Ω. Series: same I, P=I²R → more R = more power. 60W bulb glows brighter in series (higher R)! Counter-intuitive — in parallel, 100W is brighter.

🔥 HOTS 2
For what value of external R is maximum power delivered to it from a cell (ε, r)?

P_ext = I²R = ε²R/(R+r)². dP/dR = 0 → r−R = 0 → R = r. P_max = ε²/(4r). This is the Maximum Power Transfer Theorem — used in electronics and communication! 📡

🔥 HOTS 3
A wire is stretched to n times its original length. Find new resistance in terms of original.

Volume constant: l×A = l'×A' → l' = nl → A' = A/n. R' = ρl'/A' = ρ(nl)/(A/n) = ρn²l/A = n²R. New resistance = n²R. Stretching to n times → resistance becomes n² times! 📏

🔥 HOTS 4
If Wheatstone bridge battery and galvanometer are interchanged, does the balance condition change?

No. Balance condition R₁/R₂ = R₃/R₄ does NOT change. The bridge is symmetric w.r.t. battery and galvanometer arms. This is the reciprocity theorem — sensitivity may change but balance point stays same.

🔥 HOTS 5
In a neutral copper wire carrying current, total charge density ρ_total = 0. Yet current flows (j ≠ 0). How?

ρ₊ = −ρ₋ → ρ_total = 0. But j = ρ₊v₊ + ρ₋v₋. Positive ions barely move (v₊ ≈ 0). Only electrons drift: j ≈ ρ₋v₋ ≠ 0. The formula j = ρv applies to EACH carrier type separately, not to total charge density. (Points to Ponder #5!)

🔥 HOTS 6
Two wires of same material: lengths ratio 1:2, areas ratio 2:1. Connected in parallel across same battery. Find ratio of heat produced.

R₁ = ρl/(2A) = ρl/2A. R₂ = ρ(2l)/A = 2ρl/A → R₂/R₁ = 4. Parallel: same V, H = V²t/R ∝ 1/R. H₁/H₂ = R₂/R₁ = 4:1

🔥 HOTS 7
Why is tungsten used for bulb filament but not copper, even though copper has lower resistivity?

Bulb filament needs HIGH resistance (heat/light) AND HIGH melting point (withstand ~2500°C). Tungsten: MP=3422°C, high ρ → glows white-hot ✓. Copper: MP=1085°C, very low ρ → melts at bulb temperature ✗. Tungsten's extreme melting point makes it the only practical choice!

🔥 HOTS 8
Why is semiconductor resistance temperature-dependent OPPOSITE to metals? Explain using electron theory.

Metal: n is constant. T↑ → more ion vibration → τ↓ → ρ=m/(ne²τ) increases → R increases. Semiconductor: very few free electrons at room T. T↑ → more electrons gain energy to break free → n increases dramatically. The n-increase DOMINATES over τ-decrease → net ρ decreases → R decreases. Two competing effects; in SC, the carrier release wins!

Theory Questions Bank

1 MARK
Is EMF a force? What is its SI unit?
No. EMF = potential difference (work done per unit charge by source). SI unit: Volt (V). Name is historical and misleading — given when phenomenon wasn't understood.
1 MARK
What are dimensions of electrical conductivity σ?
σ = 1/ρ. Dimensions: [M⁻¹ L⁻³ T³ A²]. Unit: S/m (Siemens/metre) or (Ω·m)⁻¹.
1 MARK
Name the physical quantity whose SI unit is m²V⁻¹s⁻¹.
Mobility (μ = v_d/E). It measures how easily charge carriers drift in an electric field.
2 MARKS
Distinguish between resistance and resistivity.
Resistance R: depends on material AND dimensions (l, A). R = ρl/A. Unit: Ω. Extrinsic property. Resistivity ρ: depends ONLY on material (not shape/size). ρ = m/(ne²τ). Unit: Ω·m. Intrinsic property of material.
2 MARKS
Why are household appliances connected in parallel?
(1) Each appliance gets full supply voltage (2) Can be switched independently (3) If one fails, others work (4) Each draws its rated current (flexibility). Series connection would give each appliance less than full voltage and switching one off would break all!
2 MARKS
What is the condition for maximum current from a cell? Why is it rarely used in practice?
I_max = ε/r (when R=0, short circuit). Not used in practice: (1) Causes excessive heating inside cell (2) Can permanently damage/destroy the cell (3) No useful work done in external circuit. Real cells have a maximum rated current much lower than I_max.
3 MARKS
State the "V=IR is not Ohm's law" insight. Give example where V=IR applies but Ohm's law fails.
V=IR defines resistance R=V/I at any operating point — applies to ALL conductors. Ohm's law additionally says R is CONSTANT (I-V graph is linear). A diode: at every point V=IR gives a value of R, but R changes dramatically with voltage → V=IR applicable but Ohm's law NOT obeyed. Ohm's law also stated as: material obeys it when ρ doesn't depend on |E| or direction of E.
3 MARKS
Explain why electrical power is transmitted at high voltages over long distances.
P_loss = I²R_c = P²R_c/V². Power loss ∝ 1/V². For same power P delivered, high V → small I → I²R loss is tiny. Step-up transformer increases V at power station (e.g. 220kV). Step-down transformer reduces to 230V at consumer end. Example: At 10× higher V, current is 10× less, power loss is 100× less!
3 MARKS
What is a Wheatstone bridge? Describe how it is used to find an unknown resistance.
Wheatstone bridge: four resistors R₁,R₂,R₃,R₄ in diamond shape. Battery across one diagonal, galvanometer across other. Method: Insert unknown R₄ in one arm. Keep known R₁,R₂ in arms 1 and 2. Vary R₃ until galvanometer reads zero (I_g=0 = null deflection = balanced). Then R₄ = R₃·R₂/R₁. Advantage over ammeter-voltmeter: null-point method eliminates instrument errors.
