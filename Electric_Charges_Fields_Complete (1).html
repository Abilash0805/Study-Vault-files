Section 1.1

Introduction — What IS Static Electricity?

Let's build your intuition from real-life examples first

👕
The Winter Sweater Mystery

Ever taken off your woollen sweater in the dark and seen tiny sparks? That's static electricity! When you rub wool against your body, electrons (tiny negative particles) jump from one material to another. One side gets extra electrons (negative), the other loses them (positive). When those charges jump back through air — SPARK! ⚡

⛈️
Lightning — Same Thing, Just 300 Million Times Bigger!

Clouds rub against each other in a thunderstorm. Charges build up massively. When the difference becomes huge, a massive spark jumps — that's lightning! The same physics as your sweater. Nature doesn't waste good physics.

🚗
That Electric Shock from a Car Door

When you slide out of a car seat (especially with synthetic clothes), electrons pile up on your body. The moment you touch the metal door handle — ZAP! The charges discharge through your fingers. This is exactly the same principle!

📋 Exam-Ready Definition — Electrostatics

Electrostatics is the branch of physics that deals with the study of forces, fields, and potentials arising from charges that are at rest (static = not moving).

💡 Exam Tip
"Static" = not moving. That's the KEY word! If charges move, it becomes current electricity. This distinction appears in 1-mark MCQs every year.
History

How Humans Discovered Electricity

600 BC

🏺 Thales of Miletus, Greece

Discovered that amber (Greek: elektron) rubbed with wool attracts light objects. The word "electricity" literally comes from elektron (amber). He had NO idea he was discovering one of the universe's fundamental forces!

1600 AD

🔬 William Gilbert

Coined the term "electricus" and showed many materials (not just amber) can be electrified. First systematic study of electricity.

1750 AD

🇺🇸 Benjamin Franklin

Named the two types of charges positive (+) and negative (−). By convention, glass rod rubbed with silk = positive. Plastic rod rubbed with fur = negative. He also proved lightning = electricity.

1785 AD

⚖️ Charles Coulomb

Used a torsion balance to measure electric force precisely. Discovered the inverse-square law — now called Coulomb's Law. Exact same math form as Newton's gravity!

1912 AD

🔬 Robert Millikan

Oil drop experiment — experimentally proved that charge is quantised (comes in packets of e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). Won Nobel Prize for this.

Electric Charge

The fundamental property of matter that causes electromagnetic phenomena

🧠 Memory Trick

Think of it like personality: Two bossy people (same type) clash and push each other away. But an introvert and extrovert attract each other! Same charges = same personality = REPEL. Opposite charges = complementary = ATTRACT.

📋 What is Electric Charge?

Electric charge is a fundamental intrinsic property of matter due to which it experiences electromagnetic force. It exists in two types: positive (+) and negative (−). The property that differentiates the two kinds of charges is called the polarity of charge.

How Charging Actually Works — At the Atomic Level

Fig — Electron transfer makes glass rod positively charged

🔴 Positive Charge

A body gets positively charged when it loses electrons. More protons than electrons. Glass rod rubbed with silk becomes positive.

🔵 Negative Charge

A body gets negatively charged when it gains electrons. More electrons than protons. Silk cloth becomes negative (gained electrons from glass).

⚠️ Critical Concept
No new charge is ever created! Electrons are just transferred from one object to another. Total charge before rubbing = Total charge after rubbing. This is the Law of Conservation of Charge!
Section 1.3

Conductors & Insulators

Why does a metal spoon NOT get charged but a plastic comb does?

🧲
The Comb and Hair Experiment

Run a plastic comb through dry hair rapidly. Now bring it near tiny bits of paper — it attracts them! The comb gets charged because plastic is an insulator. But try the same with a metal spoon — it won't work! Why? Because metal is a conductor — charges immediately flow through your hand to the ground.

Fig — Conductors have free electrons; insulators have bound electrons
📋 Conductor — Definition

Conductors are substances that allow easy passage of electricity (charge) through them. They have comparatively free electrons that can move inside the material. Examples: metals (copper, silver), human body, earth, animal bodies, tap water.

📋 Insulator — Definition

Insulators are substances that do not allow easy passage of electricity. Electrons are tightly bound. Examples: glass, porcelain, plastic, nylon, wood, rubber, dry air.

⚡ Semiconductor — The Third Type

There's a third category: Semiconductors (Silicon, Germanium). Their resistivity to charge movement is intermediate between conductors and insulators. They're the backbone of all modern electronics — your phone's chips are made of semiconductors!

Not directly in Class 12 Ch1 Important for future chapters
💡 Why Can't You Charge a Metal with Your Hand?
When you hold a metal rod and rub it, charges flow through the metal → through your body (conductor) → to the ground (earth). So it can't accumulate charge. But if you use a wooden/plastic handle, the charges stay on the metal! That's why lab equipment has insulating handles.
Section 1.4

Basic Properties of Electric Charge

Three fundamental laws that govern ALL electric charge in the universe

➕

1. Additivity

Charges add like real numbers (scalars). Total charge = algebraic sum of all individual charges.

⚖️

2. Conservation

Total charge of an isolated system is always conserved. Charge cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred.

🔢

3. Quantisation

Charge comes in discrete packets. q = ne where n is an integer and e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C is the elementary charge.

1.4.1 Additivity of Charges

➕ What does "Additive" mean?

Charges add up like regular numbers — you just do simple algebra! A charge of +3C and −5C together = +3 + (−5) = −2C. Unlike, say, velocity (which is a vector), charge has magnitude but no direction — it's a scalar quantity.

Additivity Formula
Q_total = q₁ + q₂ + q₃ + ... + qₙ
Where each qᵢ can be positive or negative. Proper algebraic signs must be used!
🧮
Example: System with 5 charges

Charges: +1, +2, −3, +4, −5 (in arbitrary units)
Total charge = (+1) + (+2) + (−3) + (+4) + (−5) = −1 unit
Simple arithmetic! No vectors, no angles needed.

📌 Key difference: Charge vs Mass
Mass is always positive. Charge can be positive OR negative. Both are scalars, but this is a key distinction examiners love to test!

1.4.2 Conservation of Charge

📋 Law of Conservation of Charge

The total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant. Charge can neither be created nor destroyed. It can only be transferred from one body to another. This is one of the most fundamental laws of physics — never violated in any known experiment.

Fig — Conservation of charge: charges separate but total remains zero
⚛️
Cool Fact: Even Particles Obey This!

In nuclear physics, a neutron (charge = 0) can decay into a proton (+e) + an electron (−e). Total charge before = 0. Total charge after = +e + (−e) = 0. Conservation holds even at the subatomic level!

1.4.3 Quantisation of Charge

📋 Quantisation of Charge — Exam-Ready Definition

Electric charge is quantised, meaning it exists only in integral multiples of a basic unit of charge 'e'.

q = ne

where n is any integer (±1, ±2, ±3, ...) and e = 1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (charge of one proton or electron). This was first suggested by Faraday's laws of electrolysis and experimentally proved by Millikan (1912).

🧠 Memory Trick — Quantisation

Think of charge like coins. You can have ₹1, ₹2, ₹5, ₹10 coins — but NOT ₹1.73 coin. Similarly, charge can be 1e, 2e, 3e... but NEVER 1.5e or π·e. Charge comes in coins, not as a liquid!

Section 1.5

Coulomb's Law

The quantitative law describing force between two point charges

🧲
The Magic of Inverse Square Laws

Coulomb discovered something beautiful: the electric force between two charges behaves EXACTLY like gravity between two masses. Double the distance → force becomes ¼. Triple the distance → force becomes 1/9. This is the Inverse Square Law!

📋 Coulomb's Law — Exam-Ready Statement

The electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. The force acts along the line joining the two charges.

Coulomb's Law — Scalar Form
F = k |q₁q₂| / r²
= (1/4πε₀) × |q₁q₂| / r²
k = 9 × 10⁹ N·m²/C²  |  ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻²  |  r = distance between charges
k = 1/(4πε₀) where ε₀ = permittivity of free space
Fig 1.3 — Coulomb's force as vectors (repulsion case shown)

Vector Form of Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law — Vector Form
F₂₁ = (1/4πε₀) × (q₁q₂/r²₂₁) × r̂₂₁
r₂₁ = position vector from charge 1 to charge 2
r̂₂₁ = unit vector from 1 to 2 (direction of force on q₂ due to q₁)
F₁₂ = −F₂₁ (Newton's Third Law satisfied ✓)

✅ What vector form handles automatically:

• If q₁, q₂ same sign → F₂₁ along r̂₂₁ → Repulsion
• If q₁, q₂ opposite sign → F₂₁ along −r̂₂₁ → Attraction
No need for separate equations!

⚠️ Important Limitations:

• Valid only for point charges (or when size ≪ separation)
• Valid in vacuum/free space
• For charges in a medium, multiply by 1/εᵣ where εᵣ is relative permittivity

How Coulomb Discovered His Law (Historical)

📜 Coulomb's Clever Experimental Method ▼

  1. The Torsion Balance

    Coulomb invented a torsion balance — a very sensitive device where a charged sphere is suspended by a thin wire. The twist of the wire measures the force. (Same device Cavendish used for gravity!)

  • Creating Known Charge Ratios

    Problem: He didn't know the exact charge! Solution: If sphere has charge q and you touch it with an identical uncharged sphere, each gets q/2. Repeat: each gets q/4. This gives known ratios!

  • Varying Distance

    He fixed charges and varied distance r. Found F ∝ 1/r². Then fixed distance and varied charges. Found F ∝ q₁q₂. Combined: F = kq₁q₂/r²

    Forces Between Multiple Charges

    The Principle of Superposition — handling more than 2 charges

    📋 Principle of Superposition — Exam-Ready

    The total electrostatic force on a charge due to a number of other charges is the vector sum of all the individual forces on that charge due to each other charge, taken one at a time. The individual forces are unaffected by the presence of other charges.

    Superposition Principle — General Formula
    F₁ = F₁₂ + F₁₃ + F₁₄ + ... + F₁ₙ
    = (q₁/4πε₀) × Σᵢ (qᵢ/r²₁ᵢ) × r̂₁ᵢ  [i = 2 to n]
    Vector addition using parallelogram law
    Fig 1.5 — Superposition: vector sum of all individual forces
    🎸
    Real-World Analogy — Multiple Speakers

    Imagine 5 speakers playing different notes. At any point in the room, the total sound = sum of sound from each speaker independently. Each speaker doesn't "know" about other speakers. Same with charges — each force acts independently, you just add them up as vectors!

    📘 NCERT Example — Equilateral Triangle

    Three charges q₁ = q₂ = q₃ = q at vertices of equilateral triangle of side l. A charge Q (same sign as q) at centroid. Find force on Q.

    Distance from each vertex to centroid = l/√3 F₁ = F₂ = F₃ = (1/4πε₀) × 3Qq/l² (each directed from vertex toward centroid) By symmetry, F₁, F₂, F₃ are equally spaced at 120° apart. Vector sum: F₁ + F₂ + F₃ = 0 ∴ Net force on Q = ZERO ✓ Intuition: Q is at exact center, pulled equally in all directions → cancels out!
    Section 1.7

    Electric Field

    The invisible force field surrounding every charge

    🌊
    Think of it Like Gravity Around Earth

    Earth creates a "gravity field" around it — even in empty space. Drop a ball 1 meter away from Earth's center and it gets pulled. The field exists whether or not the ball is there. Similarly, a charge Q creates an electric field all around it. Place another charge q in that field → force appears!

    📋 Electric Field — Exam-Ready Definition

    The electric field at a point in space due to a source charge Q is defined as the force experienced by a unit positive test charge placed at that point, without disturbing the original position of the source charge Q.

    E = F/q₀  (where q₀ → 0, the test charge)

    Unit: N/C or V/m  |  It is a vector quantity

    Electric Field Due to a Point Charge
    E = (1/4πε₀) × Q/r² × r̂
    Q = source charge  |  r = distance from charge  |  r̂ = unit vector pointing away from +Q
    For +Q: E points radially OUTWARD  |  For −Q: E points radially INWARD
    Fig 1.8 — Electric field direction for positive and negative charges

    Key Points About Electric Field

    🎯

    Source vs Test Charge

    Q (source charge) creates the field. q₀ (test charge, vanishingly small) detects it. Test charge must be small enough not to disturb Q!

    🌐

    Exists Everywhere

    Electric field exists at every point in 3D space around a charge. Even in vacuum. Even if no test charge is there to feel it!

    ⚡

    E is Independent of q₀

    The field E depends only on Q (source) and position. F depends on both Q and q₀. But E = F/q₀ is always the same at a point.

    📐

    Spherical Symmetry

    At equal distances from a point charge, |E| is the same. So E is constant on a sphere centered at Q.

    📋 Electric Field Due to a System of Charges

    By the superposition principle:
    E(r) = E₁(r) + E₂(r) + ... + Eₙ(r)
    = (1/4πε₀) × Σᵢ (qᵢ/r²ᵢₚ) × r̂ᵢₚ

    Each charge contributes independently to the total field at any point.

    💡 Why Is Electric Field Important?
    In electrostatics it's convenient but not essential. But when charges ACCELERATE, they create time-varying electromagnetic fields that travel as waves (light, radio waves, X-rays). The field concept is essential to explain WHY there's a time delay when one charge moves and another responds. This is where it becomes physically crucial!
    Section 1.8

    Electric Field Lines

    Faraday's brilliant visual tool for understanding fields

    🎨
    Faraday's Brilliant Idea

    Michael Faraday (no formal education, just a genius!) invented field lines to visualize the electric field. Instead of just numbers, draw curves such that the tangent at any point gives the direction of E. Density of lines = strength of field. Simple, elegant, powerful.

    📋 Electric Field Line — Definition

    An electric field line is a curve drawn in the electric field such that the tangent to it at each point gives the direction of the net electric field at that point. The relative density (closeness) of field lines at a point represents the relative strength of the electric field at that point.

    Fig 1.14 — Electric field lines for point charges and dipole

    Properties of Electric Field Lines

    📍

    Start & End

    Field lines start from positive charges and end at negative charges. If only one charge, they extend to/from infinity.

    🔄

    Continuous

    In a charge-free region, field lines are continuous curves without breaks.

    ❌

    Never Cross!

    Two field lines can NEVER intersect. If they did, the field would have two directions at one point — impossible!

    🔁

    No Closed Loops

    Electrostatic field lines never form closed loops. (Magnetic field lines do — that's the difference!)

    🧠 Memory Trick — Field Line Properties

    Remember: "Start Positive, End Negative, Cross Never, Loop Never"
    S.P.E.N.C.N.L.N — "Spenching Nothing Lost" (make your own silly sentence!)

    💡 Density of Lines = Strength of Field
    Where field lines are close together (dense) → strong field. Where they're spread apart → weak field. This is why field is strongest near a charge and weakens as you move away.
    Section 1.9

    Electric Flux

    How many field lines "pierce" through a surface?

    💧
    Think of Water Flow

    Imagine holding a ring in a river current. If you hold the ring perpendicular to the flow, maximum water flows through. If you tilt the ring, less water flows. If parallel, zero water flows. Electric flux is exactly like this — it measures how many electric field lines pass through a surface!

    📋 Electric Flux — Exam-Ready Definition

    Electric flux through a surface is defined as the dot product of the electric field and the area vector. It represents the number of electric field lines passing through a given surface area.

    Δφ = E · ΔS = E ΔS cos θ

    where θ is the angle between E and the outward normal to the surface. Unit: N·m²/C or V·m. It is a scalar quantity.

    Electric Flux Formula
    Δφ = E · ΔS = E ΔS cos θ
    For total surface: φ = ∮ E · dS (closed surface integral)
    θ = 0° → φ = maximum (E ⊥ surface)  |  θ = 90° → φ = 0 (E ∥ surface)
    Fig 1.15 — Electric flux depends on the angle between E and surface normal

    📐 Area as a Vector

    Area element ΔS is a vector quantity. Its magnitude = the area value. Its direction = along the outward normal to the surface. For closed surfaces, outward normal is used by convention.

    ⚡ Positive vs Negative Flux

    • θ < 90°: cos θ > 0 → Positive flux (lines exit)
    • θ > 90°: cos θ < 0 → Negative flux (lines enter)
    • θ = 90°: φ = 0 (lines parallel to surface)

    💡 Flux Through a Closed Sphere Around +Q
    Gauss's Law (coming in Ch2) states: φ = Q_enclosed/ε₀. For a sphere around +Q, all field lines exit the sphere → positive flux. This is one of the most powerful tools in electrostatics!
    Section 1.10

    Electric Dipole

    Two equal and opposite charges — the building block of molecular physics

    🧲
    Water — The Natural Dipole!

    A water molecule (H₂O) is a natural electric dipole! The oxygen atom pulls electrons toward itself, making it slightly negative, while the hydrogen atoms become slightly positive. This dipole nature makes water a universal solvent and is why life as we know it is possible. Physics is literally in every drop of water!

    📋 Electric Dipole — Board-Ready Definition

    An electric dipole is a system of two equal and opposite point charges (+q and −q) separated by a small distance 2a. The line joining −q to +q defines the dipole axis. The dipole moment p⃗ is directed from −q to +q.

    Dipole Moment
    p = q × 2a
    p = dipole moment (vector) — directed from −q to +q (negative to positive!)
    Magnitude: p = q × 2a  |  Unit: C·m (coulomb-metre)
    ⚠️ Critical: Direction of Dipole Moment
    Direction is from −q to +q (negative to positive). Many students get this wrong! It's counter-intuitive — remember it by: "dipole points toward the positive charge."
    Fig 1.10 — Electric dipole: charges separated by 2a, dipole moment p from −q to +q

    Electric Field on the Axial Line

    📋 Axial Position (End-on position)

    A point P on the axis of the dipole at distance r from the centre (where r >> a).

    E-Field on Axial Line (Short Dipole: r >> a)
    E_axial = 2p / (4πε₀r³)
    Direction: Same as dipole moment p⃗ (from −q to +q direction)
    For exact formula: E = (2pr) / [4πε₀(r²−a²)²]

    Electric Field on the Equatorial Line

    📋 Equatorial Position (Broadside-on position)

    A point P perpendicular to the dipole axis, at distance r from the centre (where r >> a).

    E-Field on Equatorial Line (Short Dipole: r >> a)
    E_equatorial = p / (4πε₀r³)
    Direction: OPPOSITE to dipole moment p⃗ (anti-parallel to p⃗)
    For exact formula: E = p / [4πε₀(r²+a²)^(3/2)]

    ⚡ Axial vs Equatorial: Key Comparison

    • E_axial = 2 × E_equatorial (axial is TWICE as strong)
    • Axial E is along p⃗; Equatorial E is opposite to p⃗
    • Both fall as 1/r³ (faster than point charge's 1/r²!)
    • Ratio: E_axial / E_equatorial = 2 (always, for r >> a)

    🧠 Memory Trick — Directions

    Axial: Standing at the "end" of the dipole — you're looking at it head-on. The field agrees with the dipole's direction.
    Equatorial: Standing at the "side" — you see the dipole sideways. The field reverses!

    Torque on Dipole in Uniform Electric Field

    Torque on an Electric Dipole
    τ = pE sinθ
    τ = torque (vector: τ⃗ = p⃗ × E⃗)
    p = dipole moment, E = electric field, θ = angle between p⃗ and E⃗
    Maximum torque when θ = 90°: τ_max = pE
    Zero torque when θ = 0° or 180° (stable/unstable equilibrium)
    🌀
    Analogy: Compass in Magnetic Field

    Just as a compass needle aligns with Earth's magnetic field (torque from the field rotates it to alignment), an electric dipole in a uniform electric field experiences a torque that tries to align p⃗ with E⃗. This is how microwave ovens work — they use an alternating E field to rotate water dipoles millions of times per second, generating heat through friction!

    Fig 1.11 — Dipole in uniform electric field: torque τ = pE sinθ aligns the dipole with E
    🧠 Memory Trick — Dipole Formulas

    "Axial is Double, Equatorial is Opposite"
    E_axial = 2E_equatorial (double the value), and equatorial field points opposite to p. For torque: τ = pE sinθ (uses SINE, not cosine!). Confusing torque with potential energy? PE = −pEcosθ (cosine). Remember: Torque = Sine, PE = cosine.

    Section 1.11

    Gauss's Law

    The most powerful tool in electrostatics — one law to rule them all

    💡
    Why Gauss's Law is Beautiful

    Coulomb's Law lets you calculate force. But Gauss's Law does something more elegant: it relates the total electric flux through any closed surface to the charge enclosed inside. It doesn't care about the shape of the surface, the position of the charge inside, or what's outside. Just the charge inside. Simple. Elegant. Powerful. This is physics at its best!

    📋 Gauss's Law — Statement

    The total electric flux through any closed surface is equal to 1/ε₀ times the total charge enclosed by that surface.

    Gauss's Law
    φ = ∮ E⃗ · dS⃗ = q_enclosed / ε₀
    φ = total electric flux through any closed surface (called Gaussian surface)
    q_enclosed = total charge inside the closed surface (algebraic sum)
    ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻² (permittivity of free space)
    The surface can be ANY shape — sphere, cube, irregular blob!
    Fig 1.12 — Gauss's Law: total flux through any closed surface = Q_enclosed/ε₀

    ✅ What Makes Gauss's Law Powerful?

    • Works for ANY closed surface (sphere, cube, irregular)
    • Charge OUTSIDE the surface contributes ZERO net flux
    • Flux depends ONLY on enclosed charge
    • Doesn't need the charge to be at center
    • If q_enc = 0, net flux = 0 (but E may not be zero!)

    ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

    • Gauss's law works for ANY closed surface, not just spheres!
    • φ = 0 does NOT mean E = 0 everywhere on the surface
    • External charges don't contribute to NET flux but DO affect local E
    • Must be an isolated system — closed surface required

    💡 Gauss's Law vs Coulomb's Law
    Gauss's law is actually a more general statement than Coulomb's law — you can derive Coulomb's law FROM Gauss's law (using a spherical Gaussian surface around a point charge). That's why it's so fundamental!

    Proof Concept: Gauss's Law from Coulomb's Law

    📐 Derivation Concept (For Board Exams)

    Consider a point charge +q at centre of a sphere of radius r.
    E at surface = q/(4πε₀r²) (outward, constant on sphere)
    Area of sphere = 4πr²
    φ = E × A = [q/(4πε₀r²)] × [4πr²] = q/ε₀
    The r² cancels! This is why Gauss's law is distance-independent.
    It works because Coulomb's law is an inverse-square law — exactly matching the area growth of spheres!

    Section 1.12

    Applications of Gauss's Law

    Using Gauss's Law to find electric fields of continuous charge distributions

    ⚡
    The Strategy: Choose Your Gaussian Surface Wisely!

    The trick with Gauss's law is choosing a Gaussian surface where E is constant in magnitude and either parallel or perpendicular to the surface everywhere. When you can factor E out of the integral, the math becomes trivial. This is the art of applying Gauss's law!

    1. Field Due to Infinite Line Charge

    📋 Setup

    An infinitely long line charge with uniform linear charge density λ (C/m). Gaussian surface: coaxial cylinder of radius r and length l.

    Electric Field Due to Infinite Line Charge
    E = λ / (2πε₀r)
    λ = linear charge density (C/m)  |  r = perpendicular distance from line
    Direction: radially outward (for +λ), radially inward (for −λ)
    Note: E ∝ 1/r (falls slower than point charge's 1/r²!)
    💡 Derivation Steps — Infinite Line Charge
    1. Cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r, length l
    2. Flux through two flat ends = 0 (E perpendicular to normal)
    3. Flux through curved surface = E × 2πrl (E is constant, parallel to outward normal)
    4. q_enclosed = λl
    5. Gauss's Law: E × 2πrl = λl/ε₀ → E = λ/(2πε₀r)

    2. Field Due to Infinite Plane Sheet of Charge

    Electric Field Due to Infinite Plane Sheet
    E = σ / (2ε₀)
    σ = surface charge density (C/m²)
    Direction: perpendicular to the plane (outward for +σ)
    Key: E is UNIFORM and INDEPENDENT of distance from the sheet!
    💡 Derivation Steps — Infinite Plane Sheet
    1. Gaussian surface: cylinder (pillbox) with cross-sectional area A, straddling the sheet
    2. Flux through curved surface = 0 (E parallel to surface)
    3. Flux through two flat faces = 2 × E × A (E exits both sides)
    4. q_enclosed = σA
    5. Gauss's Law: 2EA = σA/ε₀ → E = σ/(2ε₀)

    3. Field Due to Uniformly Charged Spherical Shell

    Outside the Shell (r > R)
    E = Q / (4πε₀r²)
    Same as a point charge Q at the centre! The shell "looks like" a point charge from outside.
    Inside the Shell (r < R)
    E = 0
    No enclosed charge → Gauss's Law gives E = 0. This is the basis of Faraday cage shielding!
    Fig 1.13 — E vs r for uniformly charged spherical shell: zero inside, peak at surface, 1/r² outside

    🛡️ Faraday Cage — Real World Application

    Inside a conducting shell, E = 0. This is used in:
    • Car bodies protect passengers from lightning
    • Shielded cables (coaxial cables) for signal integrity
    • Microwave oven mesh prevents radiation escape
    • Aircraft and spacecraft electronics shielding
    Named after Michael Faraday (1836)!

    📋 Summary: All Three Applications

    • Line charge: E = λ/(2πε₀r) — falls as 1/r
    • Plane sheet: E = σ/(2ε₀) — CONSTANT (no r!)
    • Spherical shell: E = 0 (inside), Q/(4πε₀r²) (outside)

    Each uses a cleverly chosen Gaussian surface that exploits the symmetry of the charge distribution!

    Answer:
    Definition: Curve drawn such that tangent at each point gives direction of net E field.
    Properties:
    (1) Start from positive charges, end at negative charges

    Why does a plastic comb rubbed against hair attract small bits of paper? Why does a metal comb not show this property?

    Answer: Plastic is an insulator — when rubbed, charges stay at rubbed spot (not redistributed). Metal is a conductor — charges immediately flow through hand to ground. So plastic retains charge and attracts paper; metal does not.
    📄 CBSE 2022 · 3 Marks

    State the principle of superposition of electrostatic forces. Three point charges +1μC, −2μC, and +3μC are placed at vertices A, B, C of an equilateral triangle. Find the direction of net electric field at centroid.

    Superposition: The force on a charge due to a system of charges is the vector sum of individual forces.
    Net E direction: E due to +1μC points away from A, +3μC points away from C, −2μC points toward B. Net E will be along the direction from centroid toward B (toward −2μC side, since +1+3=4μC net repulsion in opposite hemisphere dominates).

    Answer: Nm²C⁻¹ or Vm (volt-metre)
    📄 CBSE 2019 · 2 Marks

    Two equal balls having equal positive charge 'q' coulombs are suspended by two insulating strings of equal length. What would be the effect on the force between them if a sheet of metal is placed between them?

    Answer: The electric force between the charges becomes zero. The metal sheet being a conductor has charges induced on its surfaces. The field inside a conductor is zero, effectively shielding the charges from each other (Faraday cage effect).
    📄 CBSE 2018 · 1 Mark

    Answer: If two field lines intersect, there would be two directions of electric field at the point of intersection, which is physically impossible as the electric field has a unique direction at any point in space.

    HOTS Questions

    🔥 HOTS 1 — Conceptual Reasoning

    Q: Why is the electric field inside a conductor always zero? What happens to charges when placed on a conductor?

    Answer: When charges are placed on a conductor, free electrons redistribute until the internal electric field becomes zero. If there were an internal field, free electrons would keep moving (which contradicts static equilibrium). At equilibrium, charges reside only on the surface, and E inside = 0. This is the basis of electromagnetic shielding (Faraday cage).
    🔥 HOTS 2 — Paradox Analysis

    Q: If the electric force is 10³⁶ times stronger than gravitational force, why does gravity seem to dominate at large scales?

    Answer: Gravity is always attractive. But electric force can be both attractive and repulsive. Large objects (planets, stars) are electrically neutral — positive and negative charges cancel. So net electric force between large neutral bodies is essentially zero, while gravity (always attractive) accumulates. That's why gravity wins at cosmic scales despite being intrinsically much weaker!
    🔥 HOTS 3 — Application

    Q: Two identical metallic spheres A and B have charges +3Q and −Q. They are brought into contact and then separated. Find the final charge on each sphere and explain the process.

    Answer: When touching: Total charge = +3Q + (−Q) = +2Q. By symmetry (identical spheres), each gets +2Q/2 = +Q. After separation, each sphere has charge +Q. Principle used: Additivity of charges + equal distribution in identical conductors in contact.
    🔥 HOTS 4 — Derive and Analyze

    Q: Four equal charges +q are placed at the four corners of a square of side 'a'. Find the net force on one corner charge.

    Answer:
    Forces from two adjacent corners: F₁ = kq²/a² (along diagonal, each)
    Force from diagonally opposite corner: F₂ = kq²/(a√2)² = kq²/2a² (along diagonal)
    The two adjacent forces have equal magnitude and 90° between them.
    Resultant of adjacent forces = √2 × kq²/a² (along diagonal toward center)
    Plus F₂ along the same diagonal: Total = (1/4πε₀)(q²/a²)(2√2 + 1/2) — directed away from center.
    🔥 HOTS 5 — Critical Thinking

    Q: The test charge used to measure electric field must be infinitesimally small. Why? What happens if a large test charge is used?

    Answer: A large test charge disturbs the distribution of the source charge Q (since charges exert forces on each other). This changes the field being measured. As q₀ → 0, its effect on Q's distribution becomes negligible, giving the "true" field due to Q alone. Hence E = lim(q₀→0) F/q₀.
    🔥 HOTS 6 — Real Life

    Q: Why is it safer to be inside a car during a thunderstorm than standing in an open field?

    Answer: A metal car body acts as a Faraday cage. According to Gauss's law, the electric field inside a closed conducting surface is zero. Even if lightning strikes the car, the charge distributes on the outer surface and the interior field remains zero. The person inside is safe from electrocution. Standing in an open field makes you the tallest point — a potential lightning rod!
    🔥 HOTS 7 — Net Flux Analysis

    Q: What is the net flux through a closed surface if there is no charge inside? Can the electric field at the surface be zero?

    Answer: By Gauss's law: φ = q_enc/ε₀ = 0/ε₀ = zero. The net flux is zero. However, E at the surface need NOT be zero — external charges create field at the surface but their flux contribution cancels (equal in and out). It's the NET flux that's zero, not the field itself!
    🔥 HOTS 8 — Superposition Deeper

    Q: State the superposition principle and explain why it is not "obvious" or mathematically guaranteed.

    Answer: The superposition principle states the total force on a charge equals the vector sum of individual forces from each charge acting independently. It is NOT obvious because: (1) it assumes the presence of extra charges does NOT modify the force between any two charges; (2) there are no 3-body forces that emerge only when multiple charges are present. These are experimental facts, not mathematical necessities — they could have been otherwise!

    Theory Questions (Board-Style)

    📝 1-Mark Theory Questions — 20 Questions ▼

    Q1. What is the net charge of a hydrogen atom?

    ✅ Zero. It has 1 proton (+e) and 1 electron (−e); they cancel out.

    Q2. Can a body have a charge of 1.5 × 10⁻¹⁹ C?

    ✅ No. Charge is quantised; only integer multiples of e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C are allowed. 1.5×10⁻¹⁹ is not an integer multiple.

    Q3. State the SI unit of electric field intensity.

    ✅ Newton per coulomb (N/C) or equivalently Volt per metre (V/m).

    Q4. What is the SI unit of electric flux?

    ✅ N·m²/C (Newton metre² per coulomb), also written as V·m (volt-metre).

    Q5. At what angle between E and the area vector is electric flux maximum? Minimum?

    ✅ Maximum: θ = 0° (φ = EΔS). Minimum/Zero: θ = 90° (φ = 0).

    Q6. Is electric charge a scalar or vector quantity?

    ✅ Scalar. It has magnitude only, no direction. (But it can be positive or negative — unlike mass.)

    Q7. What is meant by "point charge"?

    ✅ When the size of a charged body is very small compared to the distance between the charges, the body is treated as a point charge — its entire charge is assumed at one point.

    Q8. Name the device used to detect charge on a body.

    ✅ Gold Leaf Electroscope.

    Q9. Who coined the terms "positive" and "negative" charge?

    ✅ Benjamin Franklin (American scientist, 18th century).

    Q10. Why do electric field lines never cross each other?

    ✅ If two lines crossed, the field would have two directions at the point of intersection — which is physically impossible.

    Q11. What is the value of permittivity of free space (ε₀)?

    ✅ ε₀ = 8.854 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻² (or F/m — farads per metre).

    Q12. Do electrostatic field lines form closed loops?

    ✅ No. Electrostatic field lines never form closed loops because the electrostatic field is a conservative field. (Magnetic field lines do form closed loops.)

    Q13. What happens to the electric force between two charges when the distance is doubled?

    ✅ Force becomes 1/4th. (F ∝ 1/r² → when r doubles, F decreases 4 times.)

    Q14. Define "electrostatics".

    ✅ Electrostatics is the branch of physics that deals with the study of forces, fields, and potentials arising from charges that are at rest (static charges).

    Q15. Is electric flux a scalar or vector?

    ✅ Scalar. It is a dot product of two vectors (E and ΔS), which gives a scalar result.

    Q16. Which particle carries the basic unit of negative charge?

    ✅ The electron, with charge −e = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

    Q17. What is the force between two charges of +1 C each, placed 1 m apart in vacuum?

    ✅ F = k(1)(1)/1² = 9×10⁹ N. This is enormous — showing 1 C is a very large unit.

    Q18. What is the direction of electric field due to a positive point charge?

    ✅ Radially outward (away from the charge) in all directions.

    Q19. In Coulomb's law, what does k = 1/4πε₀ represent?

    ✅ k is the proportionality constant in Coulomb's law. k = 9×10⁹ N·m²·C⁻². It is written as 1/(4πε₀) for mathematical convenience in later derivations.

    Q20. Where do electric field lines start and where do they end?

    ✅ They start from positive charges and end at negative charges. If only one charge is present, they start (or end) at infinity.

    📝 2-Mark Theory Questions — 15 Questions ▼

    Q1. Distinguish between conductors and insulators with examples.

    Conductors: Allow easy passage of charge. Have free electrons. Charge distributes over entire surface. Examples: metals (Cu, Ag), human body, earth.

    Insulators: Do not allow passage of charge. Electrons are tightly bound. Charge stays at the point of deposition. Examples: glass, plastic, rubber, wood.

    Q2. Why is electric force called a "central force"? Does it obey Newton's third law?

    It acts along the line joining the two charges — the "central" direction. Its magnitude depends only on distance r between charges.

    Yes, it obeys Newton's 3rd law: F₁₂ = −F₂₁. Force on q₁ due to q₂ is equal and opposite to force on q₂ due to q₁.

    Q3. How does Coulomb's force between two charges change when placed in a medium of relative permittivity εᵣ?

    F_medium = F_vacuum / εᵣ = (1/4πε₀εᵣ) × q₁q₂/r²

    Since εᵣ > 1 for all media (e.g., water εᵣ ≈ 80), the force decreases in a medium. For water, Coulomb force is only 1/80th of its vacuum value!

    Q4. State and explain the law of conservation of electric charge.

    The total electric charge of an isolated system remains constant. Charge cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transferred.

    Example: When glass rod is rubbed with silk, glass gains +Q and silk gains −Q. Net charge = 0 before and after rubbing.

    Q5. Explain the quantisation of electric charge. Who proved it experimentally?

    Electric charge is always an integer multiple of e: q = ne (n = ±1, ±2, ...).
    e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C is the elementary charge (charge of proton/electron).

    First suggested by Faraday's electrolysis laws. Experimentally proved by Robert Millikan (1912) in his famous oil-drop experiment.

    Q6. What is meant by the "principle of superposition" in electrostatics?

    The total electrostatic force on any charge due to a system of other charges is the vector sum of all individual forces on that charge, taken one at a time.

    Key: Individual forces are unaffected by the presence of other charges. F₁ = F₁₂ + F₁₃ + F₁₄ + ...

    Q7. Define electric field. Why must the test charge be vanishingly small?

    Electric field E at a point = force per unit positive test charge: E = lim(q₀→0) F/q₀.

    Test charge must be vanishingly small (q₀ → 0) so it does not disturb the distribution of the source charge Q, ensuring the measured field is due to Q alone.

    Q8. How does the electric field due to a point charge vary with distance?

    E = (1/4πε₀) × Q/r²
    E ∝ 1/r² — electric field decreases as the square of the distance increases (inverse square law).
    Double the distance → E becomes 1/4. Triple the distance → E becomes 1/9.

    Q9. Two charges +3μC and −3μC are placed 10 cm apart. What is the direction of electric field at the midpoint between them?

    At midpoint: E due to +3μC points away from it (toward −3μC). E due to −3μC also points toward −3μC. Both fields are in the same direction (from +q toward −q). So net E is directed from the positive charge toward the negative charge.

    Q10. What is the physical significance of electric field lines?

    (1) Tangent at any point on a field line gives the direction of E at that point.
    (2) Density (closeness) of field lines indicates the relative strength of E — crowded lines = strong field, sparse lines = weak field.
    (3) Introduced by Faraday to visually represent the field.

    Q11. State two differences between gravitational force and Coulomb's electrostatic force.

    1. Nature: Gravitational force is always attractive. Coulomb force can be attractive or repulsive.
    2. Magnitude: Electric force is ~10³⁶ times stronger than gravitational force between same particles.
    3. Medium: Gravity is unaffected by medium. Electric force reduces in a dielectric medium.

    Q12. What is the difference between "charging by friction" and "charging by induction"?

    By friction: Requires actual contact and rubbing. Electrons transfer by rubbing. Both objects get charged with opposite signs.

    By induction: No contact needed. Charged body is brought nearby; charges in the neutral body rearrange. Net charge of neutral body still zero unless grounded.

    Q13. Define electric flux. Is it possible for net flux through a surface to be zero even when E ≠ 0?

    Electric flux φ = E·ΔS = EΔScosθ. It measures the number of field lines passing through a surface.

    Yes! If the surface is oriented parallel to E (θ = 90°), or if equal field lines enter and exit a closed surface (equal positive and negative contributions), net flux = 0 even though E ≠ 0.

    Q14. Why is electric field a vector quantity while electric flux is a scalar?

    Electric field E has both magnitude and direction at every point → vector.
    Electric flux φ = E · ΔS is a dot product of two vectors → result is a scalar (no direction). Flux only tells "how much" passes through — not in which direction.

    Q15. A neutral conducting sphere is placed near a positive charge. What happens to the charge distribution on the sphere?

    By induction: free electrons in the sphere are attracted toward the positive charge — negative charge accumulates on the near side. The far side becomes positively charged (deficit of electrons). Net charge of sphere remains zero, but charges are redistributed. The sphere experiences a net attractive force toward the positive charge.

    📝 3-Mark Theory Questions — 8 Questions ▼

    Q1. State Coulomb's law. Write its vector form and explain each term.

    Statement: The force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their magnitudes and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, acting along the line joining them.

    Vector form: F₂₁ = (1/4πε₀) × (q₁q₂/r²₂₁) × r̂₂₁
    where r̂₂₁ = unit vector from q₁ to q₂, r₂₁ = distance between them.
    If charges same sign → F along r̂₂₁ → repulsion. If opposite sign → F along −r̂₂₁ → attraction. Newton's 3rd law: F₁₂ = −F₂₁ ✓

    Q2. State and explain the principle of superposition of forces. Write the mathematical expression for force on charge q₁ due to n other charges.

    Principle: The total force on any charge due to a system of other charges is the vector sum of forces due to each individual charge, independent of the presence of others.

    F₁ = F₁₂ + F₁₃ + ... + F₁ₙ
    = (q₁/4πε₀) Σᵢ₌₂ⁿ (qᵢ/r²₁ᵢ) r̂₁ᵢ

    Significance: Coulomb's law + superposition → all electrostatics can be calculated.

    Q3. Explain the additivity, conservation, and quantisation of electric charge with one example each.

    Additivity: Charges add algebraically. System with +1, +2, −3 = +1+2−3 = 0. No direction; scalar addition.

    Conservation: In glass-silk rubbing: glass gets +Q, silk gets −Q. Total = 0 before and after.

    Quantisation: q = ne. A body with 3 electrons removed has charge = +3e = +4.8×10⁻¹⁹ C. Non-integer values impossible.

    Q4. What are electric field lines? Draw field lines for (a) isolated positive charge (b) two equal positive charges.

    Field lines are imaginary curves where the tangent at each point gives the direction of E. Density represents strength of field.

    (a) Isolated +Q: Radially outward lines in all directions (like a sunburst), extending to infinity.
    (b) Two +q charges: Lines start from each charge, repel each other. A neutral point exists between them where field is zero. No lines cross. Lines curve away from each other midway.

    Q5. Define electric flux. Derive the expression φ = E·S cosθ and explain the direction convention for a closed surface.

    Electric flux through area ΔS = number of field lines crossing it = E·ΔS.
    If E makes angle θ with area normal n̂: component of E along n̂ = Ecosθ.
    ∴ Δφ = (Ecosθ) × ΔS = EΔScosθ = E⃗ · ΔS⃗

    Convention for closed surface: Area vector (n̂) always points outward (away from enclosed region). Flux leaving surface = positive; entering = negative.

    Q6. Compare Coulomb's electrostatic force with gravitational force. State any three similarities and three differences.

    Similarities: (1) Both follow inverse-square law. (2) Both are central forces. (3) Both have unlimited range.

    Differences: (1) Gravity always attractive; Coulomb can be repulsive. (2) Electric force ~10³⁶ times stronger. (3) Coulomb force depends on medium; gravity does not. (4) Source: charge vs mass.

    Q7. Describe the construction and working of a gold leaf electroscope. How can it detect the sign of charge?

    Construction: Glass-enclosed box. Metal rod with knob at top. Two thin gold leaves at bottom. Rubber insulation at the neck.

    Working: Charge → knob → rod → leaves. Both leaves get same charge → repel → diverge. More charge = wider divergence.

    Sign detection: First charge the electroscope with known charge (say +). Then bring unknown charge near knob. If leaves diverge more → same sign (+). If leaves converge → opposite sign (−).

    Q8. Explain why quantisation of charge is not observable at macroscopic level but is important at microscopic level.

    At the macroscopic level, we deal with charges like 1 μC = 10⁻⁶ C. This contains 10⁻⁶/1.6×10⁻¹⁹ ≈ 6.25×10¹² electrons. The step size 'e' is imperceptibly tiny compared to the total — like how individual pixels are invisible when viewing a full HD photo from far.

    At the microscopic/atomic level (e.g., nuclear reactions, electron transitions), charges of order a few 'e' are involved. Here quantisation is critical — e.g., a neutron decaying into proton+electron shows charge conservation at the single-particle level.

    📝 5-Mark Theory Questions — 6 Questions ▼

    Q1. State and explain the three basic properties of electric charge in detail with examples and formulae.

    1. Additivity (2 marks): Total charge of a system = algebraic sum of all individual charges. Q = q₁+q₂+...+qₙ. Charge is a scalar. Proper algebraic signs must be used. Example: system with +1, +2, −3, +4, −5 = −1 unit. Unlike mass (always +), charge can be positive or negative. This makes conservation testable.

    2. Conservation (1.5 marks): Net charge of an isolated system is always constant. Cannot create or destroy charge. Only transfer. Example: Rubbing glass with silk → glass gets +Q, silk gets −Q, total = 0. Even at subatomic level: neutron (0) → proton (+e) + electron (−e). Total before = 0, after = +e−e = 0. Never violated in any known experiment.

    3. Quantisation (1.5 marks): q = ne, where n is an integer and e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C. Charge exists in discrete packets. First suggested by Faraday (electrolysis laws). Proved by Millikan in 1912 (oil drop experiment). Macroscopic level: not observable (charge of 1μC = 10¹³ electrons, step size too tiny). Atomic level: essential (electrons are countable).

    Q2. Derive the expression for electric field intensity at a point due to a system of n point charges using the superposition principle.

    Definition: E at a point P = force experienced by unit positive test charge at P = lim(q₀→0) F/q₀. Direction = direction of force on positive test charge.

    Single charge: E = (1/4πε₀) × Q/r² × r̂ (vector, pointing away from +Q)

    System of n charges: Using superposition principle, the field at P due to system of charges q₁, q₂, ..., qₙ at positions r₁, r₂, ..., rₙ:
    E₁ = (1/4πε₀) × q₁/r²₁ₚ × r̂₁ₚ (due to q₁)
    E₂ = (1/4πε₀) × q₂/r²₂ₚ × r̂₂ₚ (due to q₂) ... and so on.
    Total E(P) = E₁+E₂+...+Eₙ = (1/4πε₀) Σᵢ (qᵢ/r²ᵢₚ) r̂ᵢₚ

    Key points: E is a vector field — varies with position. Independent of test charge. Has spherical symmetry for a single charge.

    Q3. What are electric field lines? State all their properties. Draw field lines for: (a) single +q (b) single −q (c) +q and −q (dipole) (d) two equal +q charges.

    Definition: Curves drawn in an electric field such that tangent at each point gives the direction of net field E there.

    Properties (all 4 must be written):
    (1) Start from positive, end at negative charges (or at infinity for isolated charges)
    (2) Continuous curves in charge-free regions (no breaks)
    (3) Never cross each other (unique direction at each point)
    (4) Do NOT form closed loops (conservative nature of electrostatic field)

    Density note: Dense lines = strong field; sparse lines = weak field. Relative density carries the magnitude information.

    (a) Single +q: Radially outward, uniform in all directions
    (b) Single −q: Radially inward, uniform in all directions
    (c) Dipole (+q, −q): Start from +q, curve through space, end at −q. Beautiful butterfly pattern
    (d) Two +q: Start from both, repel midway; neutral point between them where E = 0

    Q4. State Coulomb's law. Explain how Coulomb arrived at this law experimentally. Write the law in vector form and verify it satisfies Newton's third law.

    Statement: Force ∝ q₁q₂ and ∝ 1/r². Acts along the line joining them. F = kq₁q₂/r².

    Experimental method: Used torsion balance. Problem: unknown charge. Solution: sphere with charge q + identical uncharged sphere → each gets q/2 (symmetry). Repeat → q/4. Gets known ratios. Varied r with fixed charges: F ∝ 1/r². Varied charges with fixed r: F ∝ q₁q₂. Combined → F = kq₁q₂/r². Verified from macroscopic to subatomic (10⁻¹⁰ m).

    Vector form: F₂₁ = (1/4πε₀)(q₁q₂/r²₂₁)r̂₂₁
    F₁₂ = (1/4πε₀)(q₁q₂/r²₁₂)r̂₁₂ = (1/4πε₀)(q₁q₂/r²₂₁)(−r̂₂₁) = −F₂₁
    ∴ F₁₂ = −F₂₁ → Newton's Third Law satisfied ✓

    Q5. Define electric flux. Explain the area vector concept. Derive φ = E·S·cosθ and state the significance of positive and negative flux.

    Definition: Electric flux through a surface = measure of number of electric field lines passing through it = E·ΔS.

    Area as a vector: Area has magnitude (=area value) and direction (=outward normal for closed surface, or chosen normal for open surface). ΔS⃗ = ΔS × n̂.

    Derivation: For area element ΔS with normal making angle θ with E:
    Projection of ΔS perpendicular to E = ΔS cosθ
    ∴ Δφ = E × (ΔS cosθ) = EΔScosθ = E⃗·ΔS⃗
    For total surface: φ = ∮E⃗·dS⃗ (surface integral)

    Signs: θ < 90°: φ > 0 (lines exiting). θ > 90°: φ < 0 (lines entering). θ = 90°: φ = 0. Unit: Nm²C⁻¹. Scalar quantity.

    Q6. Compare and contrast Coulomb's law and Newton's Law of Gravitation. Also explain why gravity dominates at large scales despite being much weaker than electric force.

    Similarities: Both are inverse-square laws (F ∝ 1/r²). Both are central forces (along the line joining). Both have unlimited range. Both described by similar mathematical forms (k vs G).

    Differences:
    • Nature: Electric can attract/repel; gravity only attracts
    • Strength ratio: Fₑ/Fg ≈ 10³⁶ for proton-proton
    • Medium: Electric force ∝ 1/εᵣ in medium; gravity unchanged
    • Source: charge vs mass
    • Shielding: Electric force can be shielded; gravity cannot

    Why gravity dominates cosmically: Large objects (planets, stars) are electrically neutral — equal +/− charges cancel. Net electric force ≈ 0. But gravity has no "negative mass" to cancel it — it always adds up. For a planet with N atoms: gravity ∝ N (cumulative), electric force ≈ 0 (cancel). Hence gravity dominates at large scales despite being intrinsically far weaker.

    Real-World Applications

    ⚡ Lightning Rods

    Tall buildings use lightning rods (pointed conductors) that create a large electric field at their tip, providing a preferred path for lightning discharge. This protects the building from destructive lightning strikes. Coulomb's law in action!

    🖨️ Laser Printers

    Laser printers use static electricity. The drum is charged, laser selectively discharges it, toner (charged powder) sticks to charged areas, then transfers to paper. Every printout is a Coulomb's law demonstration!

    🧹 Air Purifiers

    Electrostatic precipitators charge dust/smoke particles, which then get attracted to collector plates. Industrial pollution control uses this principle to remove particulates from factory smoke.

    🏭 Spray Painting

    In industrial spray painting, paint droplets are given a negative charge and the object to be painted is given a positive charge. This ensures even coverage with minimal waste — electrostatics at work!

    📱 Touchscreens

    Capacitive touchscreens detect the change in electric field when a conductive object (your finger) touches the screen. The screen measures tiny changes in charge distribution to pinpoint where you touched.

    ⚕️ ECG/EEG

    The heart and brain generate electric fields (from ionic currents). ECG measures the electric field from heart activity. EEG measures brain's electric field. Medical diagnostics = applied electrostatics!

    ⚠️ Top 10 Board Exam Mistakes to Avoid

    Common errors that cost marks — memorise and avoid all 10!

    1. In a medium: Force = kq₁q₂/(εr·d²) — don't forget the εr!
    2. Field inside conductor: Always ZERO in electrostatics (not sometimes, ALWAYS).
    3. Axial vs Equatorial: E_axial = 2p/4πε₀r³ and E_equatorial = p/4πε₀r³. Don't swap them!
    4. Equatorial direction: Equatorial E is OPPOSITE to p (not same direction as axial!).
    5. Torque formula: τ = pE sinθ (uses SINE), not cosine. PE = −pEcosθ (uses cosine).
    6. Gauss's Law surface: Applies to ANY closed surface — not just spheres!
    7. Dipole direction: p goes from −q to +q (negative to positive — counter-intuitive!).
    8. Units: Flux is N·m²·C⁻¹, dipole moment is C·m, field is N/C or V/m.
    9. Simplified dipole formulas: Only valid when r >> a. Always verify this condition!
    10. Charge densities: λ (linear C/m), σ (surface C/m²), ρ (volume C/m³) — don't mix them!
