🌊1. What is a Solution?
🌊 1. What is a Solution?
🎬 IMAGINE THIS!

Imagine you're making lemonade. You pour sugar into water and stir. The sugar disappears! You can't see the sugar anymore, but the water tastes sweet. That's a solution! The sugar is the solute (the thing that dissolves) and water is the solvent (the thing that does the dissolving). 🍋

🔬

Official Definition

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances whose composition can be varied within certain limits.

  • Solute = the component present in smaller amount (dissolves)
  • Solvent = the component present in larger amount (does the dissolving)
  • Particle size < 1 nm (so small you can't even see them!)

Key Property: Homogeneous

"Homogeneous" means same throughout. If you take any part of the solution, it will have the same composition. Unlike a suspension (muddy water) where things settle down, a solution NEVER settles!

🌍 REAL LIFE EXAMPLES
  • 🧂 Salt water (NaCl + H₂O) — ocean water!
  • 🍺 Beer (CO₂ in water + ethanol) — gas in liquid
  • 💍 Gold alloy (gold + copper) — solid in solid
  • 🌬️ Air (O₂, N₂, CO₂, Ar…) — gas in gas
  • ⛽ Petrol in petrol tank — liquid in liquid

The Master Definition

A Solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more components. "Homogeneous" means uniform throughout — if you take any tiny sample from anywhere in a solution, the composition is the same!

  • Solvent = The component present in LARGEST quantity. It decides the physical state of the solution.
  • Solute = Every other component (present in smaller quantity)
  • Binary Solution = Solution with exactly 2 components (what we study in Class 12)
💡
Real Life Connection: When you breathe, you're inhaling a gaseous solution (Air = N₂ + O₂ + other gases). When fish survive in water, they use dissolved O₂ — a gas-in-liquid solution. When your mom makes chai, that's solid (tea, sugar) dissolved in liquid — a solid-in-liquid solution!
🧠 Never-Forget Trick

⚖️3. Concentration Units (All 7!)
⚖️ 3. Concentration Units (THE BIG 5!)
🎬 ANALOGY

Imagine you're measuring how much chilli powder 🌶️ is in a dish. You could say "5 grams per 100g of dish" OR "5 teaspoons per litre" OR "1 spoon per 10 spoons". These are all DIFFERENT ways to say the SAME thing. Concentration units are exactly like this — different ways to express "how much solute is in the solution"!

① Mole Fraction (χ or x)

Moles of one component divided by TOTAL moles of ALL components.

Mole Fraction of solute (x₂)
x₂ = n₂ / (n₁ + n₂)
Mole Fraction of solvent (x₁)
x₁ = n₁ / (n₁ + n₂)

Where n₁ = moles of solvent, n₂ = moles of solute

⚠️ KEY FACT

x₁ + x₂ = 1 (ALWAYS! No units!)

📌 WHEN USED

Used in Raoult's Law calculations. Dimensionless (no units). Doesn't change with temperature!

② Molarity (M)

Moles of solute dissolved per litre of solution.

Molarity Formula
M = n (moles of solute) / V (volume of solution in litres)
From mass
M = (w × 1000) / (M₂ × V_mL)

Unit: mol L⁻¹ or M

⚠️ BOARD TRAP!

Molarity CHANGES with temperature (because volume changes). This is a very common exam question!

🌍 EXAMPLE

1 M NaCl solution = 1 mole of NaCl (58.5g) dissolved in enough water to make 1 litre of solution total.

③ Molality (m)

Moles of solute dissolved per kilogram of solvent (NOT solution!).

Molality Formula
m = n₂ / w₁ (kg of solvent)
Expanded form
m = (w₂ × 1000) / (M₂ × w₁_g)

Unit: mol kg⁻¹

🧠 TRICK to remember

MoLALity = mass of soLvent (L in moLALity → soLvent)

MoLARity = volume of soLution (AR in soLARity → soLAR = big = solution)

⭐ WHY IS MOLALITY SPECIAL?

Molality does NOT change with temperature because it depends on MASS (not volume). That's why it's used in colligative property formulas!

④ Mass Percentage (w/w%)

Mass % of solute
w/w% = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100
🌍 REAL EXAMPLE

When you see "68% HNO₃" on a lab bottle — it means 68 g of HNO₃ in every 100 g of solution!

⑤ Parts Per Million (ppm)

ppm formula
ppm = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 10⁶
🌍 WHERE YOU SEE THIS

Pollutant levels in water (like fluoride in drinking water), medicine dosages in blood, air quality index! If news says "CO₂ is 420 ppm in atmosphere" — now you know what it means! 🌍

📊 Expressing Concentration

Section 1.2 — 7 different ways to answer: "How much solute is dissolved?"

👨‍🍳
Why 7 ways? Why not just one?! 😤

Great question! Different situations need different units. A doctor uses w/v% for medicine doses, a chemist uses molarity for reactions, and an environmental scientist uses ppm for tiny pollutants. Each unit has a job! 🎯

① Mass Percentage (w/w) — The "Recipe" Method

Imagine you're baking: "I used 10g of salt in 100g of dough." That's mass percentage!

Formula
Mass % = (Mass of component / Total mass of solution) × 100

🍺 Real Example: Commercial bleach has 3.62% sodium hypochlorite by mass → 3.62g NaOCl in 100g solution

✅
Board Exam Trick: "10% glucose by mass" = 10g glucose + 90g water = 100g solution. The 90g comes automatically!

② Volume Percentage (v/v) — The "Drink" Method

Formula
Volume % = (Volume of component / Total volume of solution) × 100

🚗 Real Example: A 35% (v/v) ethylene glycol solution = antifreeze in your car engine. It lowers water's freezing point to –17.6°C!

🍷 Alcohol bottles say "40% v/v" = 40mL pure alcohol in 100mL drink

③ Mass by Volume % (w/v) — The Doctor's Method

Formula
w/v % = (Mass of solute in grams / Volume of solution in mL) × 100

💉 Used in medicine & pharmacy: "Normal saline is 0.9% w/v NaCl" = 0.9g NaCl per 100mL solution

④ Parts Per Million (ppm) — The "Trace Amounts" Method

When concentration is TINY, like fluoride in drinking water or CO₂ in air, we use ppm.

Formula
ppm = (Parts of component / Total parts of all components) × 10⁶
  • 🦷 1 ppm fluoride in water → prevents tooth decay
  • 🦷 1.5 ppm fluoride → teeth become mottled (discolored)
  • ☠️ High ppm fluoride → poisonous!
  • 🐟 Sea water: 5.8 ppm dissolved O₂ (6×10⁻³g in 1030g sea water)
🧠 Memory Trick
ppm = "out of 1 million" — Think of 1 grain of salt in a large swimming pool! 🏊

⑤ Mole Fraction (x) — The "Ratio" Method

Formula (Binary Solution)
x_A = n_A / (n_A + n_B)     x_B = n_B / (n_A + n_B)

x_A + x_B = 1 (Always! Sum of all mole fractions = 1)

🎯 Key property: Mole fractions are dimensionless (no units) and always sum to 1

💡 Used in Raoult's Law and vapour pressure calculations (you'll see this in Section 4!)

📌
Example 1.1 (NCERT): 20% C₂H₆O₂ by mass → x(glycol) = 0.068, x(water) = 0.932. Molar mass of C₂H₆O₂ = 62 g/mol

⑥ Molarity (M) — The "Lab Chemist" Method

Formula
Molarity (M) = Moles of solute / Volume of solution in LITRES
  • Unit: mol/L or mol/dm³ or M
  • 0.25M NaOH = 0.25 mol NaOH dissolved in 1 litre solution
  • ⚠️ CHANGES with temperature (because volume changes)
⚠️
Exam Trap! Molarity is calculated using volume of SOLUTION (not solvent). Don't confuse!

⑦ Molality (m) — The "Temperature-Safe" Method

Formula
Molality (m) = Moles of solute / Mass of solvent in KILOGRAMS
  • Unit: mol/kg or m
  • 1m KCl = 1 mol (74.5g) KCl dissolved in 1 kg water
  • ✅ DOES NOT change with temperature (mass is always constant)
  • Used in colligative property calculations
🧠 Molarity vs Molality
MolarITY → solutIon volume (lITres) | MolalITY → solveNt mass (kG)
Easy: Molarity = "rity" → liter | Molality = "lality" → kilo solvent

🫧4. Henry's Law & Gas Solubility
🫧 4. Henry's Law (Gas Solubility)
🎬 CARTOON STORY

Imagine a diver 🤿 going deep underwater. The higher the pressure of N₂ and O₂ above the water, the MORE gas dissolves into their blood. When they come up too fast, the pressure drops suddenly, gas bubbles out of their blood — causing "the bends" (decompression sickness). This is Henry's Law in action! 😱

Henry's Law Statement

At constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid surface.

Henry's Law
p = K_H × x

Where:

  • p = partial pressure of gas
  • K_H = Henry's Law constant (unique for each gas at a given temperature)
  • x = mole fraction of gas in solution

📊 Henry's Law Graph

Straight line through origin → direct proportionality

⬆️ Effect of Temperature on Gas Solubility

Higher temperature → Gas molecules gain energy → Escape from solution → Solubility DECREASES

Also: K_H increases with temperature → lower x for same p → less solubility

🌍 APPLICATIONS OF HENRY'S LAW
  • 🥤 Carbonated drinks: CO₂ dissolved under high pressure. When you open the bottle (pressure drops), CO₂ escapes → fizzing!
  • 🤿 Deep-sea divers: Use O₂ + He mixture instead of air to avoid N₂ narcosis (N₂ dissolves in blood at high pressure)
  • ⛰️ High altitude: Less O₂ pressure → less O₂ dissolves in blood → altitude sickness
  • 🐟 Fish in water: Fish breathe O₂ dissolved in water. Warmer water has less dissolved O₂ (Henry's Law!) — that's why fish die in hot polluted rivers
  • 🏭 Anaesthesia: Anaesthetic gases dissolve in blood based on partial pressure
⚠️ IMPORTANT LIMITATIONS
  • Henry's Law applies only at low pressure and when gas doesn't react with solvent
  • Does NOT apply to NH₃ in water (it reacts) or HCl in water (ionises)
  • Does NOT apply to highly soluble gases

🌊 Solubility

Section 1.3 — Why does salt dissolve in water but not in oil?

🧲
"LIKE DISSOLVES LIKE" — The Golden Rule of Solubility! ✨

Polar solutes dissolve in polar solvents (sugar in water ✅). Non-polar solutes dissolve in non-polar solvents (camphor in benzene ✅). This is because similar intermolecular forces attract each other!

What is Solubility?

Solubility = Maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved in a specified amount of solvent at a specified temperature and pressure.

It depends on: Nature of solute, Nature of solvent, Temperature, Pressure

The Dissolution Process — What Actually Happens?

1
Solid solute is added to solvent → solute particles dissolve → concentration increases → Dissolution
2
Dissolved particles collide with undissolved solute → come back out → Crystallisation
3
Rate of dissolution = Rate of crystallisation → Dynamic Equilibrium!
4
At this point → Saturated Solution → no more solute can dissolve!
Equilibrium Equation
Solute + Solvent ⇌ Solution    (Equation 1.10)

Effect of Temperature on Solid-in-Liquid Solubility

Follows Le Chatelier's Principle — if you stress a system at equilibrium, it shifts to reduce that stress.

🔥➕
Endothermic dissolution (ΔH > 0)
Solubility INCREASES with temperature. Adding heat shifts equilibrium forward.
❄️➕
Exothermic dissolution (ΔH < 0)
Solubility DECREASES with temperature. Adding heat shifts equilibrium backward.

Henry's Law — Gases in Liquids 🐟

🥤
Why does soda go flat when you open it? HENRY'S LAW! 🎉

Inside a sealed soda bottle, CO₂ is under HIGH pressure → high solubility → stays dissolved. When you open it, pressure drops → solubility drops → CO₂ escapes as bubbles! Exactly Henry's Law!

Henry's Law: At constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid.

Henry's Law Formula
p = K_H × x

p = partial pressure of gas (in vapour phase)
K_H = Henry's Law Constant
x = mole fraction of gas in solution
📌
Key Point: Higher K_H → lower solubility at given pressure. K_H depends on nature of gas AND temperature.

Applications of Henry's Law (Important for Board!)

🥤
Fizzy Drinks
CO₂ sealed under high pressure → stays dissolved. Open bottle → pressure drops → CO₂ escapes → bubbles!
🤿
Scuba Divers & Bends
Deep water = high pressure = more N₂ dissolves in blood. Rising fast → N₂ forms bubbles in blood = "bends" (painful, life-threatening!). Solution: tanks with 11.7% He + 56.2% N₂
🏔️
High Altitude & Anoxia
Less O₂ pressure at altitude → less O₂ dissolves in blood → weakness, unclear thinking = "Anoxia"
🐠
Aquatic Life
Fish need dissolved O₂. Cold water holds MORE O₂ (lower temp → higher gas solubility). That's why fish prefer cold water!

Effect of Temperature on Gas Solubility

Dissolving gas in liquid is an exothermic process (like condensation). By Le Chatelier's Principle:

⬆️ Temperature increases → equilibrium shifts backward → gas comes out → solubility DECREASES

🎯
Contrast with solids: Solids — can go either way (endothermic or exothermic). Gases — ALWAYS decrease solubility with increasing temperature!
💨5. Raoult's Law & Vapour Pressure
💨 5. Raoult's Law & Vapour Pressure
🎬 VISUALIZE THIS!

Imagine a swimming pool 🏊. Water molecules are jumping OUT of the pool (evaporating). Now imagine you dump a TON of sugar into the pool. The sugar molecules block the water molecules at the surface — fewer water molecules can jump out. So the vapour pressure DECREASES! That's Raoult's Law in one picture.

Raoult's Law (For Non-Volatile Solute)

The partial vapour pressure of solvent over a solution is directly proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent.

Raoult's Law
p₁ = p°₁ × x₁

Where: p₁ = VP of solvent over solution, p°₁ = VP of pure solvent, x₁ = mole fraction of solvent

Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure (RLVP) — VERY IMPORTANT!
(p°₁ - p₁) / p°₁ = x₂ = n₂ / (n₁ + n₂)

This is a colligative property — it only depends on the number of solute particles (x₂), not their identity!

💧 Vapour Pressure Lowering — Visualized

Raoult's Law for Volatile Solute (Binary Liquid Solutions)

When BOTH components are volatile (like benzene + toluene), each exerts its own vapour pressure:

Partial pressure of component 1
p₁ = p°₁ × x₁
Partial pressure of component 2
p₂ = p°₂ × x₂
Total vapour pressure (Dalton's Law)
P_total = p₁ + p₂ = p°₁x₁ + p°₂x₂

Mole Fraction in Vapour Phase (y)

In the vapour above the solution, the composition is different from liquid!

Mole fraction in vapour
y₁ = p₁ / P_total = p°₁x₁ / P_total

The more volatile component (higher p°) is MORE in vapour phase than in liquid phase. This is the basis of DISTILLATION!

💨 Vapour Pressure & Raoult's Law

Section 1.4 — The "escape artist" story of molecules!

🎪
Imagine molecules are trying to ESCAPE from a liquid! 🏃‍♂️

Every liquid molecule wants to escape into vapour. The pressure created by these escaped molecules above the liquid is Vapour Pressure. When you add a non-volatile solute, it's like putting bouncers at the exit — fewer molecules escape → vapour pressure DECREASES! 🚪

Raoult's Law for Volatile Liquids

Raoult's Law (1886): For a solution of volatile liquids, the partial vapour pressure of each component is directly proportional to its mole fraction in solution.

Raoult's Law Formulas
p₁ = p₁° × x₁    (Component 1)
p₂ = p₂° × x₂    (Component 2)

p_total = p₁ + p₂ = x₁p₁° + x₂p₂°

p_total = p₁° + (p₂° - p₁°) × x₂

Where p° = vapour pressure of pure component at that temperature

Raoult's Law for Non-Volatile Solute (Very Important!)

When solute is non-volatile (like salt, sugar), it doesn't contribute to vapour pressure. Only the solvent evaporates.

Raoult's Law (Non-volatile solute)
p = p₁° × x₁ = p₁° × (1 - x₂)

Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure:
(p₁° - p) / p₁° = x₂ = n₂ / (n₁ + n₂)

This is the basis of Colligative Properties! (explained next section)

Ideal vs Non-Ideal Solutions

🎯
Board Exam Must-Know: Chloroform + Acetone = Negative deviation (H-bond forms between them: C-H···O). Ethanol + Water = Positive deviation (H-bonds broken between ethanol molecules, weaker A-B bonds form).
🧠 Deviation Memory Trick
Positive deviation = molecules are "happy to escape" = interactions are WEAKER between A-B than between A-A or B-B = "they don't like each other!" 😤

Negative deviation = molecules are "stuck together" = A-B interactions are STRONGER = "they love each other!" 💕 (like HCl+water — strong acid-base interaction)

Azeotropes — The "Inseparable Couple"!

Some non-ideal solutions show extreme deviations and form azeotropes — mixtures that boil at a constant temperature and cannot be separated by simple distillation!

⬆️🫧
Minimum Boiling Azeotrope
Formed by +ve deviation solutions. Boils at LOWER temperature than either pure component. Example: Ethanol-water (95.6% ethanol, bp 78.1°C)
⬇️🫧
Maximum Boiling Azeotrope
Formed by –ve deviation solutions. Boils at HIGHER temperature than either pure component. Example: HNO₃-water (68% HNO₃, bp 393.5K)
⚗️6. Ideal vs Non-Ideal & Azeotropes
⚗️ 6. Ideal vs Non-Ideal Solutions

Ideal Solutions ✅

Solutions that obey Raoult's Law over the entire range of composition.

  • ΔH_mix = 0 (no heat change on mixing)
  • ΔV_mix = 0 (no volume change)
  • A-A, B-B, and A-B interactions are all equal
🌍 EXAMPLES

Benzene + Toluene, n-hexane + n-heptane, chlorobenzene + bromobenzene. All chemically similar compounds!

➕ Positive Deviation

  • P_total > Raoult's Law prediction
  • A-B interactions WEAKER than A-A, B-B
  • ΔH_mix > 0 (endothermic)
  • ΔV_mix > 0 (volume increases)

Example: Ethanol + Water, Acetone + CS₂, Ethanol + Acetone

→ Forms MINIMUM boiling azeotrope (boils at lower temp)

➖ Negative Deviation

  • P_total < Raoult's Law prediction
  • A-B interactions STRONGER than A-A, B-B
  • ΔH_mix < 0 (exothermic)
  • ΔV_mix < 0 (volume decreases)

Example: Chloroform + Acetone, HNO₃ + Water, HCl + Water

→ Forms MAXIMUM boiling azeotrope (boils at higher temp)

🌡️ 7. Colligative Properties — The BIG 4!
🎬 THE KEY IDEA

Imagine you have 10 friends 👫 in a room vs 1 friend. The 10 friends make MORE noise, take MORE space, cause MORE "disturbance" — regardless of WHO they are. Colligative properties are exactly like this! They depend ONLY on the NUMBER of solute particles, not on who/what the particles are.

What are Colligative Properties?

Properties of solutions that depend on the number of solute particles (moles), NOT on their chemical identity.

The 4 Colligative Properties:

  1. 🔽 Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure (RLVP)
  2. 🔺 Elevation of Boiling Point (ΔTb)
  3. 🔽 Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)
  4. 💧 Osmotic Pressure (π)
⭐ BIGGEST BOARD QUESTION EVER

Q: Why are colligative properties useful?
A: They can be used to determine the molar mass of unknown solutes! You measure ΔTb or ΔTf, use the formula to find n, then M = w/n.

🌡️ Colligative Properties

Section 1.5–1.7 — 4 magical effects that depend ONLY on number of particles!

🧮
These properties DON'T care WHO you are, only HOW MANY you are! 🤓

Colligative comes from Latin "colligare" = to collect together. These 4 properties depend only on the NUMBER of solute particles (moles), NOT on their nature (whether it's sugar, salt, or gold!).

① Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure

Adding a non-volatile solute reduces the vapour pressure of the solvent. The relative lowering equals the mole fraction of the solute!

Formulas
(p° - p) / p° = x₂ (mole fraction of solute)

For dilute solutions: (p° - p)/p° = n₂/n₁ = w₂×M₁ / (M₂×w₁)

(where w = mass, M = molar mass, subscript 1=solvent, 2=solute)
🔬
Application: Used to find molar mass of non-volatile solutes. This is called the Ostwald-Walker method.

② Elevation of Boiling Point (ΔTb)

🍝
Why does pasta water boil later after adding salt? ELEVATION OF BOILING POINT! 🧂

Adding salt (solute) reduces the vapour pressure. For the solution to boil, we need VP to reach atmospheric pressure again — so we need a higher temperature → boiling point goes UP!

Formula
ΔTb = Tb(solution) - Tb(solvent) = Kb × m

Kb = Ebullioscopic constant (molal elevation constant)
m = molality of solution

Molar mass of solute: M₂ = (Kb × w₂ × 1000) / (ΔTb × w₁)

Kb for water = 0.52 K kg/mol

💡
Kb formula: Kb = (R × Tb² × M₁) / (1000 × ΔvapH) — derivable from thermodynamics but formula value given in board exams.

③ Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)

🚗❄️
Why doesn't antifreeze in car engines freeze even in –30°C?

Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) is dissolved in water. It lowers the freezing point far below 0°C! That's Depression of Freezing Point. Same reason roads are salted in winter!

Formula
ΔTf = Tf(solvent) - Tf(solution) = Kf × m

Kf = Cryoscopic constant (molal depression constant)
m = molality

Molar mass of solute: M₂ = (Kf × w₂ × 1000) / (ΔTf × w₁)

Kf for water = 1.86 K kg/mol (much bigger than Kb → easier to measure!)

✅
Why Kf > Kb? Kf depends on ΔfusH (small) while Kb depends on ΔvapH (large). Smaller denominator → bigger constant. Freezing point depression is ~3.5x more than boiling point elevation for water!

④ Osmotic Pressure (π) — The Membrane Boss!

🌊🚪
Why do vegetables shrink in salt water? OSMOSIS! 🥕

Water moves from low concentration to high concentration through a semipermeable membrane. In salty water, water leaves the vegetable → it shrinks. The pressure needed to STOP this flow is Osmotic Pressure (π)!

Van't Hoff Equation for Osmotic Pressure
π = CRT = (n/V)RT

π = osmotic pressure (in atm or Pa)
C = molar concentration (mol/L)
R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K
T = temperature in Kelvin

To find molar mass: M₂ = (w₂RT) / (πV)
🌱
Isotonic Solutions
Same osmotic pressure as cell fluid. IV fluids (saline 0.9%) are isotonic — no osmosis occurs!
💊
Reverse Osmosis
Apply pressure greater than π → water flows BACK from solution to pure water. Used in water purification (RO water)!
🌟
Why is osmotic pressure the BEST for molar mass? For a 1 g/L polymer in water, ΔTb ≈ 0.0001°C (too small!), but π ≈ 1.21 kPa (measurable!). So for large molecules (proteins, polymers), osmotic pressure is used!

Van't Hoff Factor (i) — For Electrolytes!

⚡
NaCl dissolves and SPLITS into Na⁺ + Cl⁻! Now there are 2 particles, not 1! 😲

Electrolytes (salts, acids, bases) dissociate into ions. More particles = bigger colligative effect! That's why the Van't Hoff factor i was introduced.

Van't Hoff Factor (i)
i = (Observed colligative property) / (Calculated colligative property for non-electrolyte)

i = (Total moles of particles in solution) / (Moles of formula units dissolved)

Modified formulas:
ΔTb = i × Kb × m
ΔTf = i × Kf × m
π = i × CRT
(p°-p)/p° = i × x₂

Association — When i < 1!

⚠️
Acetic acid in benzene: It forms dimers (two molecules join together via H-bonds). So 2 molecules → 1 dimer → i < 1! Abnormal colligative property (lower than expected). This happens for weak acids in non-polar solvents.

🧊9. Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)
🧊 9. Depression of Freezing Point (ΔTf)
🎬 STORY TIME!

In cold countries ❄️, roads are salted in winter. Why? Because salt water has a LOWER freezing point than pure water! The road doesn't freeze at 0°C anymore. This keeps roads safe. This is depression of freezing point — solute makes the solvent freeze at a LOWER temperature!

Why Does Freezing Point Decrease?

At freezing point, solid and liquid phases are in equilibrium (same VP). Adding solute LOWERS the VP of liquid — now the liquid VP is less than solid VP at 0°C. To re-establish equilibrium, we need to go to a lower temperature (where solid VP also decreases to match). Hence, freezing point is depressed!

🎬 THE BEST ANALOGY EVER!

Imagine a bouncer 🚨 at a club. The bouncer only allows WATER molecules in, not big VIP solute molecules. This bouncer is the semipermeable membrane (SPM). Water molecules rush from the "less crowded" side (pure water) to the "crowded" side (solution) trying to "dilute" it. This rush of water is OSMOSIS!

🔬 Osmosis — The SPM Diagram

Key Definitions

  • Osmosis: Flow of solvent from lower concentration to higher concentration through a semipermeable membrane
  • Semipermeable Membrane (SPM): Allows only solvent molecules to pass, NOT solute (e.g., pig's bladder, cellulose acetate)
  • Osmotic Pressure (π): The extra pressure that must be applied to the SOLUTION side to just STOP osmosis
Osmotic Pressure (Van't Hoff equation for dilute solutions)
π = CRT = (n₂/V)RT
Calculating Molar Mass from Osmotic Pressure
M₂ = (w₂RT) / (πV)

🌡️ Isotonic, Hypertonic & Hypotonic Solutions

Isotonic

Same osmotic pressure as body fluids (0.9% NaCl = normal saline). Cell is normal. Safe to inject!

Hypertonic

>0.9% NaCl. More concentrated than cells. Water flows OUT of cells. Cells SHRINK (crenation). 😨

Hypotonic

<0.9% NaCl. Less concentrated than cells. Water flows INTO cells. Cells SWELL and may BURST (hemolysis)! 💥

🔬 Blood Cell in Different Solutions

🔄 Reverse Osmosis (RO)

Apply pressure greater than osmotic pressure on the solution side → solvent flows from solution to pure solvent (REVERSE of osmosis!).

  • Used in desalination of sea water
  • Membrane used: cellulose acetate (permeable to water, impermeable to ions)
  • You drink RO water every day! 🚰
  • Many countries use large-scale RO desalination plants

⭐ Why Osmotic Pressure is the BEST for Molar Mass

Osmotic pressure is the preferred colligative property for finding molar masses of biomolecules and polymers because:

  • Measurements done at room temperature (proteins denature at high temp)
  • Huge magnitude even for very dilute solutions — easily measurable
  • Uses molarity (not molality), easier to prepare solutions

🔢 11. Van't Hoff Factor (i)
🎬 THE PROBLEM!

You dissolve 1 mole of NaCl in water. You expect it to behave like 1 mole of particles. But NaCl splits into Na⁺ + Cl⁻ — now you have 2 moles of particles! Your colligative property calculation will be WRONG unless you account for this. Van't Hoff's factor i is the correction factor!

The Van't Hoff Factor (i)

Introduced by J.H. van't Hoff in 1880 to correct for dissociation and association of solutes.

Definition of i
i = Normal molar mass / Abnormal (experimental) molar mass
Also equals
i = Observed colligative property / Calculated colligative property
Also equals
i = Total moles of particles AFTER / moles BEFORE association/dissociation

Dissociation (i > 1)

Solute splits into MORE particles

KCl → K⁺ + Cl⁻ → i = 2

K₂SO₄ → 2K⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → i = 3

Experimental M < True M

Electrolytes (ionic compounds)

Association (i < 1)

Solute molecules join together → FEWER particles

2CH₃COOH → (CH₃COOH)₂ in benzene → i = 0.5

Experimental M > True M

Organic acids in non-polar solvents

Modified Colligative Property Formulas with i

RLVP with Van't Hoff factor
(p°₁ - p₁) / p°₁ = i · x₂ = i · n₂/(n₁+n₂)
Boiling Point Elevation
ΔTb = i · Kb · m
Freezing Point Depression
ΔTf = i · Kf · m
Osmotic Pressure
π = i · n₂RT/V = i · CRT

Degree of Dissociation (α) from i

If 1 mole of solute AB dissociates into A⁺ + B⁻:

Relation between i and α (for 1 → 2 particles)
i = 1 + α → α = i - 1

For n ions: i = 1 + α(n-1) → α = (i-1)/(n-1)

Degree of Association (α) from i

If n molecules associate: nA → Aₙ

For dimerization (n=2): 2A → A₂
i = 1 - α/2 → α = 2(1-i)

🔬 12. Abnormal Molar Mass

What is Abnormal Molar Mass?

When the experimentally determined molar mass (from colligative properties) is different from the expected (theoretical) molar mass, it's called abnormal molar mass.

Dissociation

Molar mass found < Actual molar mass

Because more particles → bigger colligative effect → lower calculated M

KCl in water: M found ≈ 37.25 instead of 74.5

Association

Molar mass found > Actual molar mass

Fewer particles → smaller colligative effect → higher calculated M

CH₃COOH in benzene: M found ≈ 120 instead of 60

Acetic Acid in Benzene — Dimerization

In benzene (low dielectric constant), acetic acid molecules form dimers via hydrogen bonding:

Dimerization equation
2CH₃COOH ⇌ (CH₃COOH)₂

Number of particles HALVES → ΔTb or ΔTf will be HALF → calculated M will be TWICE the actual = 120 g/mol instead of 60

📖 Important Theory Questions (1-3 Marks)

PYQ 2023
🧠 THEORY Q1
Define: (a) Molality (b) Van't Hoff factor (c) Azeotrope

(a) Molality: Number of moles of solute dissolved per kilogram of solvent. Unit: mol/kg. It does not change with temperature.

(b) Van't Hoff factor (i): Ratio of normal molar mass to experimentally determined molar mass, OR ratio of observed colligative property to calculated colligative property. Accounts for association/dissociation.

(c) Azeotrope: A binary mixture that boils at a constant temperature and cannot be separated by simple distillation because liquid and vapour have the same composition at that temperature.

PYQ 2022
🧠 THEORY Q2
Why does adding a non-volatile solute to water lower its vapour pressure? Derive the expression for RLVP.

Why VP decreases: The surface of the solution is occupied partially by solute molecules, which do not evaporate. This reduces the fraction of solvent molecules at the surface that can escape into vapour phase, thus reducing vapour pressure.

Derivation:

By Raoult's Law: p₁ = p°₁ × x₁

Since x₁ + x₂ = 1, x₁ = 1 - x₂

p₁ = p°₁(1 - x₂) = p°₁ - p°₁x₂

p°₁ - p₁ = p°₁x₂

(p°₁ - p₁)/p°₁ = x₂ — This is RLVP and it equals mole fraction of solute.

PYQ 2024
🧠 THEORY Q3
State Henry's Law. Why is oxygen used along with helium by deep sea divers instead of air?

Henry's Law: At constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid surface. p = K_H × x.

Deep sea divers: At high underwater pressure, more N₂ from air dissolves in the blood (Henry's Law). High dissolved N₂ causes nitrogen narcosis (dizziness/intoxication). He has much lower solubility in blood (higher K_H) than N₂, so He-O₂ mixtures avoid this problem.

PYQ 2023
🧠 THEORY Q4
What is osmosis? Distinguish between osmosis and reverse osmosis. Give one application of reverse osmosis.

Osmosis: Spontaneous flow of solvent molecules from a dilute solution (or pure solvent) to a concentrated solution through a semipermeable membrane.

Application: Desalination of sea water to get fresh drinking water.

PYQ 2022
🧠 THEORY Q5
Explain why: (a) Boiling point of 1m NaCl solution is higher than 1m glucose solution. (b) Freezing point of sea water is less than pure water.

(a) NaCl dissociates: NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → i ≈ 2. So effective molality = 2 × 1 = 2m. For glucose (non-electrolyte), i = 1, effective molality = 1m. Since ΔTb = i·Kb·m, NaCl solution has higher ΔTb and hence higher BP.

(b) Sea water contains dissolved salts (NaCl, MgCl₂, etc.). These lower the vapour pressure (Raoult's Law) and cause depression of freezing point. Hence sea water freezes at a temperature lower than 0°C.

PYQ 2021
🧠 THEORY Q6
Why is osmotic pressure method preferred over other colligative properties for determining molar masses of proteins and polymers?
  • Large magnitude: Osmotic pressure is very large even for extremely dilute solutions (easily measurable with manometers)
  • Room temperature: Measurement done at room temperature — proteins and polymers are not stable at high temperatures (used in ΔTb)
  • Uses molarity: Easier to prepare volume-based solutions than weigh-based ones

PYQ 2024
🧠 THEORY Q8 — IMPORTANT!
What are colligative properties? Why do they depend on the number of solute particles and not their nature?

Colligative properties are those properties of solutions that depend only on the number of solute particles per unit volume, not on the chemical nature of the solute. They include RLVP, elevation of BP, depression of FP, and osmotic pressure.

Why independent of nature? These properties arise from the interaction between solvent molecules and the surface occupied by solute particles. Any particle (regardless of chemical identity) blocks solvent molecules equally. A solute molecule of sugar and a molecule of NaCl occupy the same area on the surface and equally reduce the solvent's ability to escape.

However, when comparing equal moles of electrolytes vs non-electrolytes, the electrolyte gives MORE particles (due to dissociation) and hence shows a larger effect — which is why Van't Hoff factor 'i' is used.

🎯 Important Theory Questions (2-3 Marks)

2-MarkCBSE 2023

Q1. Why is molarity a temperature-dependent quantity?

Molarity = moles of solute / volume of solution in litres. Volume of a solution changes with temperature (expands on heating, contracts on cooling). Since volume is in the denominator, molarity changes with temperature. In contrast, molality (uses mass) and mole fraction (uses moles) do not change with temperature.
2-MarkCBSE 2022

Q2. State Henry's Law. Mention two of its applications.

Henry's Law: At constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas above the liquid. Mathematically: p = K_H × x.

Applications:
1. Carbonated drinks: CO₂ is sealed under high pressure to increase its solubility.
2. Scuba diving: Divers breathe helium-diluted air at high pressure to prevent "bends" (nitrogen bubble formation in blood).
3-MarkCBSE 2024

Q3. What are colligative properties? Name all four. Why do electrolytes show abnormal colligative properties?

Colligative properties depend only on the number of solute particles (moles), not on their nature. The four colligative properties are:
1. Relative lowering of vapour pressure
2. Elevation of boiling point
3. Depression of freezing point
4. Osmotic pressure

Electrolytes dissociate in solution, producing more particles than expected. For example, NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻ (2 particles per formula unit). This increases the number of solute particles, leading to a greater colligative effect than predicted for a non-electrolyte. The Van't Hoff factor (i > 1) accounts for this.
2-MarkCBSE 2023

Q4. Explain why aquatic species prefer to live in cold water rather than warm water.

Dissolution of gases in liquids is an exothermic process. By Le Chatelier's Principle, increasing temperature shifts the equilibrium backward, decreasing the solubility of gases. Cold water therefore contains more dissolved O₂ than warm water. Since aquatic species need dissolved O₂ to survive, they prefer cold water where more oxygen is available.
3-MarkCBSE 2022

Q5. Define: (a) Ideal solution (b) Azeotrope (c) Osmotic pressure

(a) Ideal solution: A solution that follows Raoult's Law at all concentrations and temperatures. The enthalpy of mixing (ΔH_mix) and volume change of mixing (ΔV_mix) are both zero. A-B intermolecular forces equal A-A and B-B forces. Example: benzene + toluene.

(b) Azeotrope: A mixture of two liquids that has a constant boiling point and cannot be separated by fractional distillation. It occurs when solutions show extreme deviations from Raoult's Law. Types: minimum boiling azeotrope (+ve deviation, e.g., ethanol-water 95.6%) and maximum boiling azeotrope (–ve deviation, e.g., HNO₃-water).

(c) Osmotic pressure: The excess pressure that must be applied on the solution side to stop the flow of solvent through a semipermeable membrane. π = CRT (Van't Hoff equation).
2-MarkCBSE 2024

Q6. Differentiate between positive and negative deviations from Raoult's Law with one example each.

Positive deviation: p_total > value predicted by Raoult's Law. A-B interactions are weaker than A-A and B-B. ΔH_mix > 0 (endothermic). ΔV_mix > 0. Example: Ethanol + Water.

Negative deviation: p_total < value predicted by Raoult's Law. A-B interactions are stronger than A-A and B-B. ΔH_mix < 0 (exothermic). ΔV_mix < 0. Example: Chloroform + Acetone (H-bond forms between them).
1-MarkCBSE Multiple Years

Q7. Which has a greater effect on freezing point: 1M NaCl or 1M glucose? Why?

1M NaCl has a greater effect. NaCl is an electrolyte that dissociates into Na⁺ + Cl⁻ (Van't Hoff factor i=2), giving 2 moles of particles per mole of NaCl. Glucose doesn't dissociate (i=1). Since ΔTf = i × Kf × m, NaCl produces twice the depression in freezing point.
