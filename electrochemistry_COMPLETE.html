SECTION 1

Electrochemical Cells — The Big Picture

🧒
🟢 Explain Like I'm 10

Two buckets with different liquids, connected by a straw (salt bridge) and copper wires. Chemicals react and push electrons through the wire by themselves = galvanic cell (like a battery). Force electrons in from a power plug = electrolytic cell (like charging your phone). Same hardware — two completely different modes! 🔋↔️🔌

Think of it like this:
An electrochemical cell is basically a magic box — you put chemicals in, electricity comes out (Galvanic cell) OR you put electricity in, chemical change happens (Electrolytic cell). Simple as that!
📖 EXAM-READY DEFINITION

An electrochemical cell is a device that consists of two metallic electrodes dipping in an electrolytic solution. It interconverts chemical energy and electrical energy.

⚡ Galvanic Cell (Voltaic Cell)

Converts chemical energy → electrical energy

The reaction is SPONTANEOUS (happens on its own)

REAL LIFE

TV remote battery, car battery, phone battery — all galvanic cells! 🔋

🔌 Electrolytic Cell

Converts electrical energy → chemical energy

The reaction is NON-SPONTANEOUS (needs a push)

REAL LIFE

Gold plating jewellery, making pure copper, aluminium production 🏭

🧂 Why is a Salt Bridge Needed?

Think of the salt bridge as a traffic cop 🚦 between the two solutions. Without it:

  • The anode solution would become positively charged (too many Zn²⁺ ions) → reaction STOPS
  • The cathode solution would become negatively charged (SO₄²⁻ left behind) → reaction STOPS

The salt bridge maintains electrical neutrality by allowing K⁺ ions to move toward cathode and Cl⁻/NO₃⁻ ions to move toward anode. This completes the circuit! ✅

📖 EXAM DEFINITION

A salt bridge is a U-shaped tube containing saturated solution of an inert electrolyte like KCl or KNO₃ set in agar-agar gel. It maintains electrical neutrality of the electrode compartments and completes the circuit.

✍️ Cell Notation (Standard Way to Write a Cell)

Boards love asking you to write cell notation! The rules:

CELL NOTATION FORMAT
Anode | Anode solution || Cathode solution | Cathode
  • | = single line = phase boundary (solid/liquid interface)
  • || = double line = salt bridge
  • Anode is always written on the LEFT
  • Cathode is always written on the RIGHT
EXAMPLE — Daniell Cell
Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s) E°cell = +1.10 V

🌡️ Standard Electrode Potential (E°)

📖 EXAM DEFINITION

The standard electrode potential (E°) of an electrode is the potential developed by the electrode when it is dipped in a solution of its own ions of unit concentration (1M), at 298K and 1 atm pressure, measured against the Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) taken as zero.

KEY FORMULAS
E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode
ΔrG° = −nFE°cell
ΔrG° = −RT ln K
  • n = number of electrons transferred
  • F = Faraday's constant = 96500 C mol⁻¹
  • K = equilibrium constant of the cell reaction
⚠️ BOARD EXAM TRAP

If E°cell is positive → ΔrG° is negative → reaction is spontaneous ✅
If E°cell is negative → ΔrG° is positive → reaction is non-spontaneous ❌

🔄 What Happens When External Voltage Is Applied to Daniell Cell?

ConditionWhat Happens
E_ext < 1.10 VCell works normally: Zn dissolves, Cu deposits, electrons flow Zn→Cu
E_ext = 1.10 VNo current, no reaction — perfectly balanced (equilibrium)
E_ext > 1.10 VReversal! Cu dissolves, Zn deposits — now acting as electrolytic cell!

💡 This beautifully shows how a galvanic cell becomes an electrolytic cell when you force a higher external voltage!

🔤 Half-Cell Reactions — The Building Blocks

Every cell reaction is two half-reactions combined:

Step 1 — Cathode (reduction): Electrons on LEFT. Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)
Step 2 — Anode (oxidation): Electrons on RIGHT. Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻
Step 3 — Overall: Add them. Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)   E°cell = +1.10 V
🌍 Try This at Home!

Put a zinc nail in blue CuSO₄ solution — the nail turns copper-colored and the blue fades! That's Zn displacing Cu because Zn is more active. You made a Daniell cell! 🔬

📐 Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) — The Universal Reference
SHE: Pt electrode in 1M H⁺, H₂ at 1 bar, 298K. E° = 0.00V by definition. All E° values measured vs SHE.
⚠️ Must-Know SHE Facts
  • E°(SHE) = 0.00 V at ALL temperatures — international convention
  • Half-reaction: H⁺(aq) + e⁻ → ½H₂(g)
  • Conditions: [H⁺] = 1M, P(H₂) = 1 bar, T = 298K
  • Platinum used: inert, excellent conductor, adsorbs H₂ gas
  • Pt-black coating: finely divided Pt = huge surface area = rapid equilibrium
  • Individual electrode potentials CANNOT be measured alone — only DIFFERENCES (cell EMF)

📐 Nernst Equation — Effect of Concentration on EMF

The Nernst equation tells us how EMF changes when concentrations are NOT standard (not 1M). Think of it as the "real world" correction!

NERNST EQUATION
E = E° − (RT/nF) × ln Q
At 298K: E = E° − (0.0592/n) × log Q
  • R = 8.314 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ (gas constant)
  • T = temperature in Kelvin
  • n = moles of electrons transferred
  • F = 96500 C mol⁻¹
  • Q = reaction quotient [Products]/[Reactants]
💡 HOW TO APPLY (Step by Step)
Write the cell reaction and identify n
Write Q = [products]/[reactants] (don't include pure solids/liquids)
Substitute E° and Q in the Nernst equation
Calculate E
Example: Zn | Zn²⁺(0.001M) || Cu²⁺(0.0001M) | Cu E = 1.10 − (0.0592/2) × log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺]) E = 1.10 − 0.0296 × log(0.001/0.0001) E = 1.10 − 0.0296 × 1 = 1.07 V

SECTION 2

Electrolytic Cells & Electrolysis

Now the REVERSE!
Instead of the cell making electricity, we're now forcing electricity INTO a solution to make a reaction happen. Like charging a dead battery! The most important thing here: the external DC source forces electrons to flow. The negative terminal of the battery pushes electrons into the cathode, and pulls them away from the anode!
📖 EXAM DEFINITION

An electrolytic cell is a device in which electrical energy from an external source is used to bring about a non-spontaneous chemical reaction. The process is called electrolysis.

📐 Electrolytic Cell — Copper Purification

🏭 Copper Purification — Industrial Process

This is one of the most important applications of electrolysis! Here's how it works:

Impure copper is made the ANODE (connected to +ve terminal)
Pure copper sheet is made the CATHODE (connected to −ve terminal)
CuSO₄ solution is used as the electrolyte
On passing DC current: impure Cu dissolves at anode, pure Cu deposits at cathode
Anode: Cu(s) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ (Oxidation)
Cathode: Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) (Reduction)

💡 Impurities like gold, silver, platinum that don't dissolve fall below the anode as "anode mud" — this is actually valuable!

⚗️ Electrolysis of Aqueous NaCl — Chlor-Alkali Process

This is SUPER important for boards! When NaCl is dissolved in water, we have Na⁺, Cl⁻, H⁺ (from water), and OH⁻ ions. Multiple species compete at each electrode:

⚠️ COMPETITION AT CATHODE
ReactionE°Winner?
Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na(s)−2.71 V❌ Loses
H⁺ + e⁻ → ½H₂(g)0.00 V✅ Wins

Higher E° is preferred at cathode → H⁺ is reduced → H₂ gas produced

⚠️ COMPETITION AT ANODE
ReactionE°Expected?
2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻+1.23 VShould win (lower E°)
2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻+1.36 VActually wins!

Lower E° should be preferred at anode. But OVERPOTENTIAL of oxygen makes Cl₂ discharge easier in practice!

OVERALL REACTION — NaCl Electrolysis
Cathode: H₂O(l) + e⁻ → ½H₂(g) + OH⁻(aq)
Anode: Cl⁻(aq) → ½Cl₂(g) + e⁻
Net: NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) → NaOH(aq) + ½H₂(g) + ½Cl₂(g)
🏭 INDUSTRIAL IMPORTANCE

NaOH (used in soap making), Cl₂ (water purification, bleach), H₂ (fuel, ammonia production) — all from sea water! This is why it's called the Chlor-Alkali Process 🌊

🌡️ Overpotential — Quick Concept

📖 DEFINITION

Overpotential is the extra potential (beyond the theoretical E°) that must be applied to make a slow electrode reaction occur at a reasonable rate. Oxygen has high overpotential, which is why Cl₂ is produced instead of O₂ even though O₂ should theoretically be preferred.

🏗️ Metals Extracted by Electrolysis

Metals that cannot be reduced by carbon or other reducing agents are extracted by electrolysis of their molten (fused) salts:

MetalSourceProcess
Sodium (Na)Molten NaClDown's Process
Magnesium (Mg)Molten MgCl₂Electrolysis of fused salt
Aluminium (Al)Al₂O₃ (Bauxite)Hall-Héroult Process (with cryolite Na₃AlF₆)
💡 WHY MOLTEN AND NOT AQUEOUS?

If we use aqueous solution of NaCl, water gets reduced (H₂ forms at cathode) instead of Na metal, because Na⁺ has more negative E° than water. In molten state, only Na⁺ is available → Na metal deposits! 🎯

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

Michael Faraday — The GOAT of Electrolysis!
In 1833-34, Faraday discovered the quantitative laws of electrolysis. Think of it this way: if you want to plate more gold on a ring, you need to pass more electricity AND more time. Faraday measured exactly HOW MUCH. He's the reason we have a unit called Faraday (F) = 96500 C/mol!

⚡ First Law of Electrolysis

📖 DEFINITION

The amount of substance deposited or dissolved at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

MATHEMATICAL FORM
w ∝ Q → w = ZQ = ZIt
  • w = mass deposited (grams)
  • Z = electrochemical equivalent
  • Q = charge passed (Coulombs)
  • I = current (Amperes)
  • t = time (seconds)

⚖️ Second Law of Electrolysis

📖 DEFINITION

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes connected in series, the masses of substances deposited are proportional to their equivalent weights (molar mass ÷ n-factor).

MATHEMATICAL FORM
w₁/w₂ = E₁/E₂
  • E = Equivalent weight = Molar mass / n
  • n = electrons per ion

🔢 Faraday's Constant — The Magic Number

One Faraday = charge on one mole of electrons. Let's derive it:

DERIVATION
Charge on 1 electron = 1.6021 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Charge on 1 mole of electrons = Nₐ × e
= 6.022 × 10²³ × 1.6021 × 10⁻¹⁹
F = 96487 C mol⁻¹ ≈ 96500 C mol⁻¹
🎯 HOW TO USE FARADAY'S CONSTANT

To deposit 1 mole of any metal, you need n × F coulombs, where n = charge on the metal ion.

Metal ionElectrons neededCharge required
Ag⁺ → Ag11F = 96500 C
Cu²⁺ → Cu22F = 193000 C
Al³⁺ → Al33F = 289500 C
Mg²⁺ → Mg22F = 193000 C

🧮 Numerical Problem Solving — Master Formula

THE MASTER FORMULA FOR NUMERICALS
Mass deposited = (Molar Mass × I × t) / (n × F)
Or equivalently: w = (M × Q) / (n × 96500)
  • M = molar mass of the element (g/mol)
  • I = current (amperes)
  • t = time (seconds)
  • n = number of electrons in half-reaction
📝 SOLVED EXAMPLE (NCERT 2.10) — Board Level

Q: A CuSO₄ solution is electrolysed for 10 min with 1.5A current. Find mass of Cu deposited.

Given: I = 1.5 A, t = 10 min = 600 s, M(Cu) = 63 g/mol, n = 2 (Cu²⁺)
Q = I × t = 1.5 × 600 = 900 C
Use formula: w = (M × Q)/(n × F) = (63 × 900)/(2 × 96500)
w = 56700/193000 = 0.2938 g ≈ 0.294 g ✅
📝 SOLVED EXAMPLE — Board 2022 Type

Q: How much electricity is required to produce 20.0g of Ca from molten CaCl₂? (M of Ca = 40)

Reaction: Ca²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Ca, so n = 2
Moles of Ca = 20/40 = 0.5 mol
Faradays needed = 0.5 × 2 = 1.0 F
Q = 1.0 × 96500 = 96500 C ✅

Batteries — Primary & Secondary

Batteries = Portable Galvanic Cells!
Remember: ALL batteries are galvanic cells. The difference is just Primary (use once, throw away — like your TV remote AAA batteries) vs Secondary (rechargeable — like your phone battery!). Let's understand each one deeply.

🔴 Primary Batteries

📖 DEFINITION

In primary batteries, the electrode reactions occur only once. Once discharged, they cannot be recharged. The reaction is irreversible.

Examples: Dry cell (Leclanche), Mercury cell

🟢 Secondary Batteries

📖 DEFINITION

Secondary batteries can be recharged by passing current in the opposite direction. They undergo many charge-discharge cycles.

Examples: Lead storage battery, Nickel-cadmium cell

Fuel Cells = Clean Energy of the Future!

SECTION 6

Corrosion — An Electrochemical Phenomenon

Corrosion = Slow Destruction! 😰
Every year India loses crores of rupees due to corrosion. That rusty iron gate, tarnished silver, green copper statue — all electrochemical reactions! The key insight: corrosion creates a tiny galvanic cell on the metal surface. One spot becomes anode (corrodes), another becomes cathode. Mind-blowing right? 🤯
📖 EXAM-READY DEFINITION

Corrosion is the process of slow deterioration of a metal due to its attack by atmospheric gases (O₂, CO₂, H₂S, etc.) or moisture. It is essentially an electrochemical phenomenon where the metal is oxidised at the anodic spots.

📐 Mechanism of Rusting of Iron — Electrochemical Process

🦀 Rusting of Iron — Step by Step Electrochemistry

COMPLETE MECHANISM
Anode: 2Fe(s) → 2Fe²⁺ + 4e⁻    (E° = −0.44 V)
Cathode: O₂(g) + 4H⁺(aq) + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O(l)    (E° = +1.23 V)
Overall: 2Fe(s) + O₂(g) + 4H⁺(aq) → 2Fe²⁺(aq) + 2H₂O(l)   (E°cell = 1.67 V)
Further: 2Fe²⁺ + 2H₂O + ½O₂ → Fe₂O₃(s) + 4H⁺ (Rust forms!)

💡 H⁺ comes from H₂CO₃ (CO₂ dissolved in water) or other acidic gases. That's why rain or polluted air accelerates rusting!

⚠️ WHY E°cell IS POSITIVE (1.67V) FOR RUSTING

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = 1.23 − (−0.44) = 1.67 V (positive!) → Rusting is thermodynamically SPONTANEOUS — it will ALWAYS happen if conditions are right! This is why iron rusts without any external push.

🛡️ Prevention of Corrosion — 5 Methods

#MethodExampleHow it works
1Painting/CoatingPaint on bridges, carsPhysical barrier — no O₂ or H₂O contact
2Chemical coatingBisphenol, varnishChemical barrier
3GalvanizingZinc coating on ironZn is more active — protects Fe even if scratched
4TinningSn coating on iron (tin cans)Physical barrier (but NOT sacrificial)
5Cathodic ProtectionMg/Zn blocks on ships, pipelinesSacrificial anode — Mg/Zn corrodes instead of Fe
🧠 GALVANIZING vs TINNING — Board Confusion!

Galvanizing (Zn coating): Even if coating is scratched, Zn still protects Fe because Zn is MORE active (higher in activity series). Zn becomes anode and sacrifices itself.

Tinning (Sn coating): If coating is scratched, Fe becomes anode (Fe is MORE active than Sn) → Fe corrodes FASTER! That's why tin cans rust once scratched!

🚢 REAL LIFE: SHIPS AND PIPELINES

Blocks of magnesium or zinc are attached to steel hulls of ships and underground pipelines. These sacrificial anodes corrode away while protecting the steel. When they're fully corroded, they're replaced — cheap and effective! This is cathodic protection 💪

Nernst Equation Deep-Dive & Gibbs Energy

🧒
🟢 Nernst Analogy

E° is the "sticker price" of a car. But the actual price (real EMF) changes with the deal — more Cu²⁺ ions = more demand at cathode = higher voltage. Nernst equation adjusts the sticker price to the actual deal price based on real concentrations!

📝 Nernst for Daniell Cell — Full Derivation

Reaction: Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s), n = 2

DANIELL CELL NERNST
E(cell) = E°(cell) − (0.0592/2) × log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺])
  • [Cu²⁺] ↑ → Q ↓ → E(cell) increases ↑
  • [Zn²⁺] ↑ → Q ↑ → E(cell) decreases ↓
🔋 Why Batteries Die

As the cell operates: [Cu²⁺] keeps dropping (Cu deposits), [Zn²⁺] keeps rising (Zn dissolves). Q increases continuously. E(cell) decreases by Nernst until E = 0 → battery dead! That's your phone draining.

📝 NCERT Example 2.1 — Mg-Ag Cell

Mg(s) + 2Ag⁺(0.0001M) → Mg²⁺(0.130M) + 2Ag(s). E°cell = 3.17V. Find E(cell).

n = 2 (Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻)
Q = [Mg²⁺] / [Ag⁺]² = 0.130 / (0.0001)² = 0.130 / 10⁻⁸ = 1.3 × 10⁷
E(cell) = 3.17 − (0.0592/2) × log(1.3 × 10⁷)
E(cell) = 3.17 − 0.0296 × 7.114 = 3.17 − 0.210 = 2.96 V ✅

📝 NCERT Example 2.2 — Kc for Cu-Ag Cell

Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2Ag(s), E°cell = 0.46V. Find Kc.

log Kc = (n × E°) / 0.0592 = (2 × 0.46) / 0.0592 = 0.92/0.0592 = 15.54
Kc = 3.92 × 10¹⁵ ✅

📝 NCERT Example 2.3 — ΔrG° for Daniell Cell

E°cell = 1.10V, n = 2, F = 96487 C mol⁻¹
ΔrG° = −nFE° = −2 × 96487 × 1.10 = −212,271 J/mol
ΔrG° = −212.27 kJ mol⁻¹ ✅ (large negative → very spontaneous!)

SECTION 2B — CONDUCTANCE

Conductance of Electrolytic Solutions

🚿
🟢 Think of Electricity Like Water in a Pipe

Resistance = narrow pipe → water flows slowly. Conductance = wide pipe → water flows easily. Resistivity = how "sticky" the pipe material is. Conductivity = how easily the material lets flow through!

📖 Resistance (R)

Opposition to current flow. R = ρ × (l/A). Unit: Ohm (Ω). Where l = length, A = cross-section area, ρ = resistivity.

FORMULA
R = ρ × (l / A)
📖 Conductance (G)

Reciprocal of resistance. G = 1/R. Ease of current flow. Unit: Siemens (S) = Ω⁻¹.

📖 Resistivity (ρ)

Resistance of a conductor of unit length and unit area. Unit: Ω·m. Conversion: 1 Ω·m = 100 Ω·cm

📖 Conductivity (κ)

Reciprocal of resistivity. κ = G × (l/A) = 1/ρ. Unit: S m⁻¹ or S cm⁻¹. Conversion: 1 S cm⁻¹ = 100 S m⁻¹

📖 Cell Constant (G*)

G* = l/A. Found using KCl of known κ: G* = R × κ. Then for any unknown: κ = G*/R. Unit: cm⁻¹.

📖 Molar Conductivity (Λm)

Conductance of ALL ions from 1 mole of electrolyte. Λm = κ × 1000 / C (κ in S/cm, C in mol/L). Unit: S cm² mol⁻¹. INCREASES on dilution!

🔌 Types of Conductance

🔩 Metallic (Electronic) Conductance

Charge carriers: free electrons. In metals and graphite. Metal composition does NOT change.

🌡️ Temperature ↑ → Conductance ↓ (more lattice vibrations block electrons)

Examples: Cu, Ag, Au, Fe, graphite

💧 Electrolytic (Ionic) Conductance

Charge carriers: ions in solution. Composition CAN change on prolonged DC current (electrolysis).

🌡️ Temperature ↑ → Conductance ↑ (ions move faster)

Examples: HCl(aq), KCl(aq), NaOH(aq)

⚠️ BOARD TRAP — Temperature Effect is OPPOSITE!

Metal conductance DECREASES with temperature. Electrolytic conductance INCREASES with temperature. This is opposite! Very common MCQ and 1-mark question.

📖 Molar Conductivity

Conductance of the volume V of solution containing one mole of electrolyte, kept between two electrodes 1 cm apart.

FORMULA
Λm = κ × 1000 / C
  • κ in S cm⁻¹  |  C in mol/L  |  Λm in S cm² mol⁻¹
  • In SI units: Λm (S m² mol⁻¹) = κ (S m⁻¹) / c (mol m⁻³)
  • Conversion: 1 S m² mol⁻¹ = 10⁴ S cm² mol⁻¹
⚠️ Unit Conversion Trap — Board Loves This!

If κ is given in S cm⁻¹ and C in mol L⁻¹: multiply κ by 1000 (to convert L to cm³).
Example: κ = 0.002 S cm⁻¹, C = 0.1 mol/L → Λm = (0.002 × 1000)/0.1 = 20 S cm² mol⁻¹

📈 Variation with Concentration

📉 Conductivity (κ) vs Dilution

ALWAYS DECREASES on dilution — for both strong AND weak electrolytes.

Why? On dilution → fewer ions per unit volume → less current per unit volume.

🧃 Juice Analogy

Add more water to juice → taste weakens. More water = fewer juice molecules per mL. Same idea with ions!

📈 Molar Conductivity (Λm) vs Dilution

ALWAYS INCREASES on dilution — for both strong AND weak electrolytes.

Why? Λm = κ/c. Volume V per mole increases greatly — more than compensates decrease in κ. Ions have more freedom!

🏊 Pool Analogy

Add more water → ions have MORE SPACE to move freely → each ion conducts better → Λm increases!

  • A = constant depending on electrolyte type (1-1, 2-1, 2-2) and solvent

⚖️ Kohlrausch's Law of Independent Migration of Ions

📖 The Discovery Story

Friedrich Kohlrausch noticed a pattern in Λ°m values:

Λ°m(KCl) − Λ°m(NaCl) = Λ°m(KBr) − Λ°m(NaBr) = Λ°m(KI) − Λ°m(NaI) ≈ 23.4 S cm² mol⁻¹
  • The difference between K⁺ and Na⁺ is always the same regardless of which anion!

Conclusion: each ion contributes independently to molar conductivity!

📖 Kohlrausch's Law

At infinite dilution, each ion migrates independently and contributes a definite amount (λ°) to the total molar conductivity, regardless of the nature of the other ion present.

KOHLRAUSCH'S LAW
Λ°m = ν₊λ°₊ + ν₋λ°₋
  • ν₊, ν₋ = number of cations/anions per formula unit
  • λ°₊, λ°₋ = individual ionic molar conductivities at infinite dilution
  • Example: CaCl₂ → Λ°m = 1×λ°(Ca²⁺) + 2×λ°(Cl⁻)
Kohlrausch's Law — Each Ion Moves Independently!
Fig: NaCl has Λ°m=126.4, KCl has Λ°m=149.8. Difference (23.4) = λ°(K⁺) − λ°(Na⁺). Cl⁻ always contributes 76.3 regardless of pairing!

⚡ Why H⁺ and OH⁻ Are So High — Grotthuss Mechanism!

H⁺ doesn't physically travel! It "jumps" from one water molecule to the next (proton hopping — like passing a ball in a queue). H₂O → H₃O⁺ → H₂O chain propagation. This is essentially the speed of bond-breaking/forming — much faster than actual ion movement! Same for OH⁻ in reverse direction.

🎯 Applications of Kohlrausch's Law (Board Favourite!)

1. Find Λ°m of Weak Electrolytes (MOST IMPORTANT)

Weak electrolytes cannot be extrapolated from graph. Use combination:

FOR CH₃COOH (Acetic Acid)
Λ°m(CH₃COOH) = Λ°m(HCl) + Λ°m(CH₃COONa) − Λ°m(NaCl)
  • Logic: CH₃COO⁻ from CH₃COONa + H⁺ from HCl, cancel Na⁺ and Cl⁻
📝 WORKED EXAMPLE

Λ°m: NaCl = 126.4, HCl = 426.2, CH₃COONa = 91.0 S cm² mol⁻¹

Λ°m(CH₃COOH) = 91.0 + 426.2 − 126.4 = 390.8 S cm² mol⁻¹

2. Degree of Dissociation (α) for Weak Electrolytes

α = Λm(c) / Λ°m

3. Dissociation Constant (Ka)

Ka = C × α² / (1 − α)
📝 WORKED EXAMPLE — Ka of Acetic Acid

Given: Λm(0.025M CH₃COOH) = 32 S cm² mol⁻¹, Λ°m = 390.8 S cm² mol⁻¹

α = 32/390.8 = 0.0819 (8.19% dissociated)

Ka = 0.025 × (0.0819)² / (1 − 0.0819) = 1.83 × 10⁻⁴

HOTS2M Why does pure water have very low but non-zero conductivity? ▼
✅ ANSWER

No, NOT accurate. When DC is passed, electrolysis occurs: K⁺ migrates to cathode (discharged as K metal or H₂), Cl⁻ to anode (discharged as Cl₂). This permanently changes the solution composition and concentration → resistance keeps changing. Back-EMF builds up → measurements unreliable. That's why AC is always used — it reverses so rapidly that net electrolysis doesn't occur.

PYQ 2022 3M Explain how rusting of iron is envisaged as setting up of an electrochemical cell. Give the reactions at anode and cathode. ▼
✅ ANSWER

At certain spots on iron, oxidation occurs (anode), while O₂ is reduced at other spots (cathode). Electrons flow through the metal. Water/H₂CO₃ acts as electrolyte.

Cathode: O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O (E° = +1.23 V)

✅ ANSWER

During recharging, the overall reaction is REVERSED:

2PbSO₄(s) + 2H₂O(l) → Pb(s) + PbO₂(s) + 2H₂SO₄(aq)

Materials involved: PbSO₄ (both electrodes initially), water, electric current (external). Products: Pb (anode), PbO₂ (cathode), H₂SO₄ (electrolyte regenerated)

PYQ 2020 1M Why does the voltage of a mercury cell remain constant throughout its life? ▼
✅ ANSWER

The overall reaction in a mercury cell (Zn(Hg) + HgO → ZnO + Hg) does not involve any ion whose concentration changes in solution — it is entirely a solid-to-solid reaction. So the reaction quotient Q remains constant → voltage remains constant (by Nernst equation).

✅ ANSWER

Anode: 2H₂(g) + 4OH⁻(aq) → 4H₂O(l) + 4e⁻

HOTS 2M In coastal areas, iron pipes in contact with sea water corrode much faster than iron pipes in freshwater. Explain why. ▼
✅ ANSWER

Sea water contains dissolved salts (mainly NaCl) which significantly increase the ionic conductivity of water. Higher conductivity means:

  • Electrolyte (sea water) conducts current more efficiently
  • The electrochemical corrosion cell works faster
  • More electrons flow → more iron dissolves per unit time

Fresh water has very low ionic content → poor conductor → slow corrosion rate.

THEORY 2M Why is galvanizing better than tinning for preventing corrosion of iron? ▼
✅ ANSWER

In galvanizing, zinc coating protects iron even if the coating is scratched, because Zn is more active (more negative E°) than Fe. Zn acts as a sacrificial anode and corrodes preferentially.

In tinning, if the coating is scratched, Fe is more active than Sn, so Fe becomes the anode and corrodes faster than bare iron. This is why tin cans rust quickly when scratched.

THEORY 2M What is the function of a salt bridge in an electrochemical cell? Name two materials used in it. ▼
✅ ANSWER

Functions of salt bridge:

  • Maintains electrical neutrality of both electrode compartments by allowing ion migration
  • Completes the internal circuit of the electrochemical cell
  • Prevents mixing of the two electrolyte solutions

Materials: KCl or KNO₃ in agar-agar gel

THEORY 1M Why is platinum used as electrodes in fuel cells? ▼
✅ ANSWER

Platinum (and palladium) are used as catalysts in fuel cell electrodes because:

  • They increase the rate of electrode reactions (catalytic activity)
  • They are inert — do not react with the fuel or electrolyte
  • They are good conductors of electricity
THEORY 2M Suggest two materials other than hydrogen that can be used as fuels in fuel cells. ▼

✅ ANSWER

Higher [H⁺] = higher E° → acts as cathode. Lower [H⁺] acts as anode.

Electrode (i) [0.1M, lower H⁺] → ANODE | Electrode (ii) [1M, higher H⁺] → CATHODE

Cell: Pt|H₂|H⁺(0.1M) || H⁺(1M)|H₂|Pt

Special: This is a concentration cell — no NET chemical change, only concentration difference drives EMF. Used to measure pH and junction potentials!

HOTS3M For cell: Zn|Zn²⁺(C₁)||Cu²⁺(C₂)|Cu, E°cell=1.10V. At what ratio C₂/C₁ will E(cell) = E°cell? What if C₁=C₂? ▼
✅ ANSWER

Nernst: E(cell) = 1.10 − (0.0592/2)×log([Zn²⁺]/[Cu²⁺]) = 1.10 − 0.0296×log(C₁/C₂)

When E(cell) = E°cell: log(C₁/C₂) = 0 → C₁/C₂ = 1 → C₁ = C₂

When C₁ = C₂: same as above → E(cell) = E°cell = 1.10V. The cell behaves as standard. This shows E° is actually the EMF when Q = 1 (equal concentrations).

HOTS3M Fe has higher E° than Zn. Why doesn't Fe protect Zn from corrosion? Why does galvanizing (Zn on Fe) work but the reverse doesn't? ▼
✅ ANSWER

Purpose of galvanizing: protect IRON (not zinc). If the coating is scratched, both Zn and Fe are exposed. Since E°(Zn²⁺/Zn) = −0.76V < E°(Fe²⁺/Fe) = −0.44V, Zn is more easily oxidised → Zn acts as sacrificial anode → Zn corrodes first, Fe is protected as cathode. ✓

If Fe coated Zn: Fe has higher E° → less easily oxidised → Zn would still corrode as anode. Fe coating on Zn would actually accelerate Zn corrosion! ✗

Zinc willingly sacrifices itself to protect iron — like a bodyguard. 💪

HOTS2M Why is platinum black used in SHE instead of smooth Pt? ▼
✅ ANSWER

Platinum black = finely divided Pt particles = extremely large surface area. This: (i) ensures rapid adsorption of H₂ gas (ii) fast establishment of equilibrium between H₂(g) and H⁺(aq) (iii) reduces electrode polarisation → gives stable, reproducible E° = 0.00V reference.

🔁 Board Exam Quick Facts

⚡

Galvanic cell: Chemical → Electrical. Spontaneous. ΔG < 0. E > 0. Anode = (−).

🔌

Electrolytic cell: Electrical → Chemical. Non-spontaneous. ΔG > 0. Anode = (+) [connected to + terminal].

🐂

AN OX / RED CAT: Anode=Oxidation | Cathode=Reduction. Always, in BOTH cell types.

🌉

Salt bridge: Maintains electrical neutrality, completes internal circuit, prevents mixing. Materials: KCl or KNO₃ in agar-agar.

📏

Nernst: E = E° − (0.0592/n)logQ. Adjusts standard EMF for real concentrations.

🔗

Golden Triangle: ΔG° = −nFE° = −RT ln Kc. Three ways of saying the same thing.

📉

κ vs dilution: DECREASES (fewer ions/volume). Λm INCREASES (greater ionic freedom per mole).

📜

Kohlrausch: Λ°m = ν₊λ°₊ + ν₋λ°₋. Use to find Λ°m of weak electrolytes (CH₃COOH etc.)!

⚖️

Faraday 1st: m ∝ Q = I×t. Faraday 2nd: m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂. 1F = 96500 C mol⁻¹.

🔋

Primary: Dry cell (1.5V, not rechargeable), Mercury cell (1.35V, constant). Secondary: Lead-acid (2V/cell, 12V total, rechargeable).

🚀

Fuel cell: H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O + electricity. ~70% efficient. Only water produced. No pollution!

🦀

Rusting: Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (anode) | O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ → 2H₂O (cathode). E°cell = 1.67V → SPONTANEOUS!

🛡️

Galvanizing (Zn on Fe): Zn (lower E°) = sacrificial anode → protects Fe even after scratch. Tinning (Sn on Fe) = only barrier protection, NO electrochemical protection.

🌡️

Temperature: Metallic conductance DECREASES ↓. Electrolytic conductance INCREASES ↑. (Opposites!)

⚗️

Chlor-alkali: NaCl(aq) + H₂O → NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂. Cathode: H₂. Anode: Cl₂ (overpotential makes Cl₂ preferred over O₂).
