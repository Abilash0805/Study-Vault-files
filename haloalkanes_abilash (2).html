Chapter Kickoff

What ARE These Compounds? 🤔

🧑‍🔬
Hey Abilash! Imagine your friend Carbon. He's hanging out with Hydrogen in a hydrocarbon.
Now imagine one day, Hydrogen gets replaced by a Halogen (F, Cl, Br, or I). That's it — that's a haloalkane! It's literally just a hydrocarbon where H got kicked out and a halogen moved in. Simple as that. 😄
📖 Board-Ready Definition
Haloalkanes (Alkyl Halides): Organic compounds obtained by replacing one or more hydrogen atoms of an aliphatic hydrocarbon with halogen atoms (F, Cl, Br, I). The halogen is attached to sp³ hybridised carbon.

Haloarenes (Aryl Halides): Organic compounds in which halogen atoms are directly bonded to sp² hybridised carbon atoms of an aromatic (benzene) ring.

🌍 Real World Examples — Why do we care?

💊
Antibiotic
Chloramphenicol — a chlorine-containing antibiotic produced by microorganisms. Used to treat typhoid fever.
🦋
Hormone
Thyroxine — an iodine-containing hormone your body produces. Its deficiency causes goiter.
🦟
Antimalarial
Chloroquine — a synthetic halogen compound used to treat malaria.
😴
Anaesthetic
Halothane — used as an anaesthetic during surgery. You've seen this in movies — the doctor puts a cloth on someone's face!
🩸
Blood Substitute
Fluorinated compounds — fully fluorinated compounds are being studied as potential blood substitutes in surgery.
🧹
Solvent
CCl₄, CHCl₃ — used as solvents for non-polar compounds and as starting materials in organic synthesis.
⚠️ Environmental Alert: Halogenated compounds persist in the environment due to their resistance to breakdown by soil bacteria. This is why DDT and CFCs are so dangerous — they don't decompose!

Section 6.2

Naming Compounds (Nomenclature) 🏷️

📝
Naming is like giving someone an address!
Common name = your nickname (Methylene chloride). IUPAC = your official full government name (Dichloromethane). Boards usually ask for IUPAC — so master it!

🔑 IUPAC Rules — Step by Step

1
Find the longest chain containing the halogen-bearing carbon. This is the parent chain.
2
Number the chain so the halogen gets the lowest possible locant (number).
3
Name the halogen as a prefix: fluoro, chloro, bromo, iodo.
4
Alphabetical order if multiple substituents are present.
5
Write: [Position]-[Halogen name][Parent chain name]. E.g., 2-Chlorobutane.

Section 6.3

Nature of C–X Bond ⚡

🧲
Imagine Carbon and Halogen playing tug of war!
Halogen is much stronger (more electronegative) and always pulls the electrons towards itself. So Carbon becomes slightly positive (δ+) and Halogen becomes slightly negative (δ−). This polarity is the KEY to all the chemistry!
📖 Board-Ready Definition
The C–X bond in haloalkanes is polar covalent due to higher electronegativity of halogen compared to carbon. The carbon atom bears a partial positive charge (δ+) and halogen bears a partial negative charge (δ−).

—Cδ+—Xδ−

Section 6.4 & 6.5

Methods of Preparation 🧪

Method 1: From Alcohols

🍺
Alcohol is like your starting material — the raw material!
You just replace the -OH group with a halogen using different reagents. Think of it as swapping out a part in a LEGO set!
🔬 Reaction 1A: Using Halogen Acids (HX)
R–OH + HCl ──ZnCl₂──→ R–Cl + H₂O

R–OH + HBr ──H₂SO₄──→ R–Br + H₂O

R–OH + HI ──H₃PO₄──→ R–I + H₂O
⚠️ ZnCl₂ needed for 1° and 2° alcohols with HCl. For 3° alcohols — just shake with conc. HCl at room temp!
⚠️ Reactivity of alcohols: 3° > 2° > 1° (more substituted = more reactive)
⚠️ Why NOT H₂SO₄ with KI? H₂SO₄ oxidizes HI to I₂ making it useless! Use H₃PO₄ instead. (Common board question!)
🔬 Reaction 1B: Using Phosphorus Halides (PX₃, PX₅)
3R–OH + PX₃ ────→ 3R–X + H₃PO₃    (X = Cl, Br)

R–OH + PCl₅ ────→ R–Cl + POCl₃ + HCl

2R–OH + red P + X₂ ─→ R–X    (PBr₃, PI₃ generated in-situ)
Note: PBr₃ and PI₃ are generated IN SITU (in the reaction mixture itself) by reaction of red phosphorus with Br₂ and I₂.
🔬 Reaction 1C: Using Thionyl Chloride — SOCl₂ (BEST METHOD!)
R–OH + SOCl₂ ────→ R–Cl + SO₂↑ + HCl↑
✅ BEST METHOD because: Both byproducts (SO₂ and HCl) are gases — they escape! So you get PURE alkyl halide without any purification needed.
This is why thionyl chloride is called the "preferred reagent".

Method 2: From Hydrocarbons

🔬 Reaction 2A: Free Radical Halogenation (Alkanes)
CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃ ──Cl₂/UV──→ CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂Cl + CH₃CH₂CHClCH₃
❌ DISADVANTAGE: Gives a complex MIXTURE of isomeric mono- and polyhaloalkanes. Yield of any single compound is LOW. Hard to separate pure compounds.
✅ Works best for methane where only one product is possible.
🔬 Reaction 2B: Addition of HX to Alkenes (Markovnikov)
CH₃–CH=CH₂ + HBr ──→ CH₃–CHBr–CH₃ (major, Markovnikov)
                            + CH₃–CH₂–CH₂Br (minor)
Markovnikov's Rule: H adds to carbon with MORE H atoms (more substituted H+, halide to less H carbon).
With PEROXIDE: Anti-Markovnikov addition occurs (Br goes to less substituted carbon).
🔬 Reaction 2C: Addition of Halogens to Alkenes (vic-Dihalide)
CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ ──CCl₄──→ BrCH₂–CH₂Br (1,2-Dibromoethane)
💡 This is also used as a TEST for unsaturation! Bromine water (reddish-brown) gets decolourized when added to an alkene. If it decolourizes — double bond present!

Method 3: Halogen Exchange Reactions

⭐ Finkelstein Reaction (R–Cl/Br → R–I)
R–X + NaI ──dry acetone──→ R–I + NaX↓

                (X = Cl or Br)
✅ NaCl/NaBr precipitates in dry acetone → drives equilibrium forward (Le Chatelier's Principle).
NaI is soluble in acetone but NaCl/NaBr is not → this makes it a one-way reaction!
⭐ Swarts Reaction (R–Cl/Br → R–F)
R–Br + AgF ──heat──→ R–F + AgBr

Also with: Hg₂F₂, CoF₂, SbF₃
Why can't we use HF directly? HF is too dangerous and corrosive. Metallic fluorides are safer.
Fluorine is too reactive for direct halogenation — so we use this indirect method.

Method 4: Preparation of Haloarenes

🔬 Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution (EAS)
C₆H₆ + Cl₂ ──FeCl₃ or Fe──→ C₆H₅Cl + HCl (Chlorobenzene)

C₆H₅CH₃ + X₂ ──Fe/dark──→ o-Halotoluene + p-Halotoluene
❌ Iodination is REVERSIBLE. Needs an oxidizing agent (HNO₃ or HIO₄) to oxidize HI formed.
❌ Fluoro compounds CANNOT be made this way (F₂ too reactive).
✅ o- and p-isomers easily separated due to LARGE difference in melting points.
⭐ Sandmeyer's Reaction (VERY IMPORTANT FOR BOARDS!)
Step 1: ArNH₂ + NaNO₂ + HX ──273-278K──→ ArN₂⁺X⁻ (Diazonium salt)

Step 2 (Cl/Br): ArN₂⁺X⁻ + Cu₂X₂ ────→ Ar–X + N₂↑

Step 2 (I):     ArN₂⁺X⁻ + KI ──────→ Ar–I + N₂↑
🔑 KEY: For Cl and Br: use Cu₂Cl₂ or Cu₂Br₂ (cuprous halide). For I: just KI (no cuprous halide needed!).
Sandmeyer's is the ONLY practical way to prepare aryl fluoride via diazonium → treat with HBF₄ (Balz-Schiemann reaction).
🎯 Memory Trick
"For Iodine, KI is fine, No cuprous for I!"
Iodine replacement in Sandmeyer's does NOT need CuI — just KI. Don't get confused!
Section 6.6

Physical Properties 🌡️

🎨 Colour

Pure alkyl halides are colourless. But bromides and iodides develop colour when exposed to light (photodecomposition). Many volatile halogen compounds have sweet smell.

💧 Solubility

Haloalkanes are insoluble in water (can't form H-bonds with water as there's no H attached to electronegative atom). They ARE soluble in organic solvents like ether, alcohol, benzene.

🌡️ Boiling Points — Key Rules

1
Higher than parent hydrocarbons — because halogen atoms increase polarity and van der Waals forces.
e.g., CH₃Cl (bp: -24°C) vs CH₄ (bp: -161°C)
2
For same alkyl group: RI > RBr > RCl > RF
Larger halogen = more electrons = stronger van der Waals forces.
3
Branching decreases boiling point — branched molecules have less surface area = weaker van der Waals forces.
1-Bromobutane (bp 375K) > 2-Bromobutane (bp 364K) > 2-Bromo-2-methylpropane (bp 346K)
4
Among isomeric dihalobenzenes: Boiling points nearly same, BUT para isomer has HIGHER melting point (more symmetric → fits crystal lattice better).
5
State at room temperature: CH₃Cl, CH₃Br, C₂H₅Cl = GASES. Most higher halides = LIQUIDS or SOLIDS.
⭐ Exam Trick — Density: Liquid haloalkanes are DENSER than water. Density order: RF < RCl < RBr < RI (heavier halogen = denser compound). All are immiscible with water.
Section 6.7

Chemical Properties (Reactions) ⚗️

⚔️
The C–X bond is the battlefield!
Since Carbon is slightly positive (δ+), it gets attacked by nucleophiles. Since it's also possible to remove HX, elimination can happen too. These two types of reactions are the heart of haloalkane chemistry!

🎯 A. Nucleophilic Substitution Reactions

📖 Definition
Reactions in which a nucleophile (electron-rich species — has lone pair or negative charge) attacks the electrophilic carbon (δ+) of C–X bond and replaces the halogen (leaving group).

🔵 KCN → Nitrile (R–CN)

KCN is ionic — CN⁻ attacks through carbon end (more electronegative N shields itself). Product: Nitrile — chain increases by 1 carbon!

R–X + KCN → R–C≡N + KX
🟠 AgCN → Isonitrile (R–NC)

AgCN is covalent — CN attacks through nitrogen end. Product: Isonitrile (carbylamine) — chain does NOT increase!

R–X + AgCN → R–N≡C + AgX

KCN → R–CN (Nitrile) | AgCN → R–NC (Isonitrile/Carbylamine)
NaNO₂ → R–NO₂ (Nitroalkane) | AgNO₂ → R–ONO (Alkyl nitrite)
🔬 Reaction with NaOH/KOH
R–X + NaOH(aq) ──→ R–OH + NaX    [Nucleophilic Substitution — gives ALCOHOL]

R–X + KOH(alc) ──→ Alkene + KX + H₂O    [Elimination — gives ALKENE]
🔑 KEY DIFFERENCE: Aqueous KOH/NaOH → substitution (alcohol). Alcoholic KOH → elimination (alkene).
This is the most common board practical question!

⬆️ B. Elimination Reactions (Dehydrohalogenation)

📖 Definition
When haloalkane is treated with alcoholic KOH (strong base), HX is eliminated (H from β-carbon and X from α-carbon) to form an alkene.
🔬 Dehydrohalogenation (Zaitsev's Rule applies)
CH₃–CH₂–CHBr–CH₃ + KOH(alc) ──→ CH₃–CH=CH–CH₃ (major, Zaitsev)
                                              + CH₃–CH₂–CH=CH₂ (minor)
Zaitsev's Rule: The major alkene formed is the more substituted alkene (more stable, more thermodynamic product).

⚙️ C. Reaction with Metals

⭐ Grignard Reagent (VERY IMPORTANT!)
R–X + Mg ──dry ether──→ R–Mg–X (Grignard Reagent)
🔑 Grignard reagents are EXTREMELY important — they are used to make alcohols, carboxylic acids, and more in organic synthesis.
Must be prepared in DRY conditions — even traces of water destroy it!
The C–Mg bond is polar (C⁻–Mg⁺), making carbon a nucleophile — opposite of normal!
⭐ Wurtz Reaction
2R–X + 2Na ──dry ether──→ R–R + 2NaX
Used to make higher alkanes. E.g., 2CH₃Br + 2Na → CH₃–CH₃ + 2NaBr
⚠️ Only useful when SAME alkyl halides used. Different halides give a mixture.
Mechanism Deep Dive

SN1 vs SN2 Mechanisms 🔬

🥊
Think of SN1 and SN2 like two different fighting styles!
SN2 is like a sudden tackle — nucleophile directly attacks from behind, no waiting! One step. SN1 is like ambush — the halogen leaves first (making a carbocation), THEN the nucleophile attacks. Two steps!

SN2 — Bimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

📖 Definition
A one-step mechanism where the nucleophile attacks the back of the C–X bond simultaneously as X leaves. Rate depends on concentration of BOTH substrate AND nucleophile.

Rate = k[RX][Nu⁻] (second order — depends on both reactants)

SN1 — Unimolecular Nucleophilic Substitution

📖 Definition
A two-step mechanism where the C–X bond first breaks to form a carbocation intermediate, then the nucleophile attacks. Rate depends ONLY on concentration of substrate.

Rate = k[RX] (first order — depends only on substrate)

Stereochemistry

Optical Activity & Stereochemistry 🔄

🖐️
Think of your hands!
Your left and right hands look similar but are mirror images — you can't superimpose them. Molecules can be like this too! These are called enantiomers (optical isomers), and they rotate plane-polarized light in opposite directions.
📖 Key Terms
Chiral molecule: A molecule with a non-superimposable mirror image. Has a chiral centre (carbon with 4 different groups).

Enantiomers: Non-superimposable mirror image molecules. They have identical physical properties but rotate plane polarized light in opposite directions.

Racemic mixture: An equimolar mixture of two enantiomers. Shows no net optical rotation. Also called (±) or dl-mixture.

Walden Inversion: In SN2, the configuration at the chiral centre is completely inverted (like an umbrella turning inside out in wind!).
⭐ Key Stereochemistry Facts for Boards:
• SN2 → Complete INVERSION of configuration (if R → gives S, and vice versa)
• SN1 → Racemization (50% R + 50% S = racemic mixture) — because carbocation is planar, Nu can attack from both sides
• If optical isomers rotate light clockwise → dextrorotatory (+). Anti-clockwise → laevorotatory (−).
Named Reactions

All Named Reactions ⭐

🏆
Named reactions are like celebrity reactions!
They're so famous they got named after scientists. Board exams LOVE to ask "What is Sandmeyer's reaction?" or "What is Finkelstein reaction?". Know ALL of them!
1. Finkelstein Reaction
R–X + NaI ──acetone──→ R–I + NaX↓

Purpose: Convert alkyl Cl/Br to alkyl Iodide
Solvent: Dry acetone
Why it works: NaCl/NaBr precipitate, driving reaction forward

2. Swarts Reaction
R–Br + AgF ──→ R–F + AgBr

Purpose: Convert R–Cl/Br to R–F
Reagent: AgF, Hg₂F₂, CoF₂, or SbF₃
Why: Can't fluorinate directly — F₂ too reactive

3. Sandmeyer's Reaction
ArN₂⁺X⁻ + Cu₂X₂ ──→ Ar–X + N₂ (X=Cl,Br)
ArN₂⁺X⁻ + KI ──→ Ar–I + N₂

Purpose: Prepare aryl halides from diazonium salts
For Cl/Br: Cu₂Cl₂ or Cu₂Br₂
For I: Just KI (no cuprous needed!)

4. Wurtz Reaction
2R–X + 2Na ──dry ether──→ R–R + 2NaX

Purpose: Coupling two alkyl groups
Limitation: Use only same R–X, otherwise mixture
e.g.: 2CH₃Br + 2Na → C₂H₆ + 2NaBr

5. Grignard Reagent Preparation
R–X + Mg ──dry ether──→ R–MgX

Purpose: Makes C–Mg bond (C is now nucleophile!)
Conditions: MUST be dry (water destroys it)
Used for: Alcohol, carboxylic acid synthesis

6. Williamson Ether Synthesis
R–X + NaOR' ──→ R–O–R' + NaX

Purpose: Making ethers
Type: SN2 mechanism
Best with: Primary alkyl halides (3° give elimination)

Section 6.10

Polyhalogen Compounds 🌍

🧪 CHCl₃ — Chloroform (Trichloromethane)
Preparation:Acetone or ethanol + bleaching powder (Ca(OCl)₂·CaCl₂)
Properties:Sweet-smelling, colourless liquid; BP 334K
Oxidation:In presence of air & light → Phosgene (COCl₂) — deadly!
Use:Anaesthetic (old), solvent, lab reagent
Storage:Dark bottles with ~1% ethanol (to prevent phosgene)
CHCl₃ + [O] → COCl₂ (Phosgene — POISONOUS GAS!)
🧪 CCl₄ — Carbon Tetrachloride (Tetrachloromethane)
Preparation:CS₂ + Cl₂ with AlCl₃ catalyst: CS₂ + 3Cl₂ → CCl₄ + S₂Cl₂
Properties:Colourless, heavy liquid; non-flammable
Uses:Solvent, fire extinguisher (Pyrene), dry cleaning
Hazard:Toxic — liver and kidney damage
☠️ DDT — Dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane
First prepared:1873
Insecticidal use:Paul Müller (1939) — won Nobel Prize 1948!
Use:WWII: control typhus, malaria mosquitoes
Problem:Non-biodegradable — persists in environment, accumulates in food chain (Biomagnification)
Status:Banned in most countries now
🌤️ Freons (CFCs)
Full form:Chlorofluorocarbons
Example:CCl₂F₂ (Freon-12, Dichlorodifluoromethane)
Uses:Refrigerants, aerosol propellants, AC coolants
Problem:Destroys ozone layer (O₃) — causes ozone hole, UV reaches earth → skin cancer
Montreal Protocol:1987 — international treaty to phase out CFCs
🌍 How CFCs Destroy Ozone (Environmental Chemistry Link!)
CF₂Cl₂ ──UV──→ CF₂Cl• + Cl•

Cl• + O₃ ──→ ClO• + O₂

ClO• + O ──→ Cl• + O₂

NET: O₃ → O₂ + O (ozone destroyed, Cl• is catalyst — keeps repeating!)
One Cl atom can destroy ~1,00,000 ozone molecules! That's why CFCs are so dangerous.
⚠️ DDT & Biomagnification: DDT doesn't break down. Small organisms absorb it → fish eat organisms (10x) → birds eat fish (10x more) → top predators have extremely high DDT concentration. This causes thin eggshells in birds of prey → species extinction!
Previous Year Questions

Board Exam Questions 📋

📋 CBSE Board 2023 | 2 Marks
Q1. Write the IUPAC name of the following compound and classify it as primary, secondary or tertiary amine: (CH₃)₃CBr
IUPAC Name: 2-Bromo-2-methylpropane
Classification: Tertiary (3°) alkyl bromide — the bromine is attached to a carbon bonded to 3 other carbon atoms.
📋 CBSE Board 2023 | 3 Marks
Q2. What happens when: (a) n-Butyl bromide is treated with alcoholic KOH? (b) Methyl bromide is treated with AgCN? (c) Chlorobenzene is treated with Na in dry ether?
(a) CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂Br + KOH(alc) → CH₃CH₂CH=CH₂ + KBr + H₂O (Elimination — gives but-1-ene)
(b) CH₃Br + AgCN → CH₃NC + AgBr (Isocyanide/carbylamine — AgCN attacks through N)
(c) 2C₆H₅Cl + 2Na → C₆H₅–C₆H₅ + 2NaCl (Fittig Reaction — gives biphenyl)
📋 CBSE Board 2022 | 2 Marks
Q3. Why is sulphuric acid not used during the reaction of alcohols with KI to prepare alkyl iodides?
Sulphuric acid is an oxidizing agent. It oxidizes HI (formed in situ) to I₂, which cannot act as a nucleophile to replace –OH. Therefore, instead of H₂SO₄, phosphoric acid (H₃PO₄) is used as it is non-oxidizing and still provides the acidic conditions needed.
📋 CBSE Board 2022 | 5 Marks
Q4. (a) What is Sandmeyer's reaction? Give an example. (b) Explain why SN2 reactions are preferred with primary alkyl halides. (c) Write the structure of the product when benzyl chloride reacts with aqueous NaOH.
(a) Sandmeyer's Reaction: When freshly prepared diazonium salt solution is mixed with cuprous chloride or cuprous bromide, the diazonium group is replaced by –Cl or –Br.
C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + Cu₂Cl₂ → C₆H₅Cl + N₂↑ (Chlorobenzene)

Q6. Explain why the C–Cl bond in chlorobenzene is shorter and stronger than in chlorocyclohexane.
In chlorobenzene (haloarene), the Cl is attached to an sp² hybridised carbon. The lone pair of Cl participates in resonance with the aromatic ring, giving the C–Cl bond partial double bond character. This makes it shorter and stronger.

In chlorocyclohexane, Cl is attached to sp³ hybridised carbon — purely single bond, no resonance — hence longer and weaker.

✅ Theory Questions (2-3 Marks)

Q5. What is Walden Inversion? In which type of nucleophilic substitution does it occur?

Walden Inversion is the complete inversion of configuration at a chiral carbon during a nucleophilic substitution reaction. It is named after Paul Walden. It occurs in SN2 mechanism. The nucleophile attacks from the back side (anti to the leaving group), and as the C–X bond breaks, all three remaining groups flip to the opposite side — like an umbrella turning inside out in wind. This results in a product with inverted configuration (R → S or S → R).

Q6. Why are haloalkanes more reactive than haloarenes? Explain with bond nature.

In haloalkanes, halogen is attached to sp³ carbon. The C–X bond is a pure single bond with no resonance. It can be easily broken by nucleophiles.

In haloarenes, halogen is attached to sp² carbon of aromatic ring. The lone pair of halogen is in conjugation with the π electrons of the benzene ring (resonance), giving partial double bond character to C–X. This makes the C–X bond shorter, stronger, and resistant to nucleophilic attack. Hence haloalkanes are far more reactive than haloarenes towards nucleophilic substitution.

Q7. Why is thionyl chloride (SOCl₂) preferred over other reagents for preparing alkyl chloride from alcohol?

When alcohol reacts with SOCl₂:
R–OH + SOCl₂ → R–Cl + SO₂↑ + HCl↑

The byproducts SO₂ and HCl are both gases and escape from the reaction mixture. This means no purification step is needed, and pure alkyl chloride is obtained directly. Other methods (like PCl₃, PCl₅) produce non-gaseous byproducts that need to be separated. Hence SOCl₂ is the preferred reagent.

Q8. Why does the para isomer of dihalobenzene have a higher melting point than ortho and meta isomers?

The para isomer is highly symmetric (halogens on opposite ends of the benzene ring). This symmetrical structure allows the molecules to pack more tightly in the crystal lattice, increasing the lattice energy and intermolecular forces. As a result, more energy is needed to melt the crystal, giving a higher melting point. The ortho and meta isomers are less symmetric and pack less efficiently in the lattice, so they have lower melting points.

Q9. Explain why chloroform is stored in dark coloured bottles with a small amount of ethanol.

When chloroform is exposed to air and light, it undergoes slow oxidation to form phosgene (COCl₂), which is a highly toxic gas:
2CHCl₃ + O₂ → 2COCl₂ + 2HCl

To prevent this:
1. Dark bottles — prevent light from catalyzing the oxidation.
2. ~1% ethanol — ethanol acts as a preservative. If any phosgene forms, ethanol reacts with it to form harmless diethyl carbonate: COCl₂ + 2C₂H₅OH → (C₂H₅O)₂CO + 2HCl

Q10. Explain the environmental impact of DDT and CFCs.

DDT: DDT is non-biodegradable. It persists in the environment and accumulates in fatty tissues of organisms. Through the food chain (biomagnification), its concentration increases at each trophic level. This causes toxicity in top predators — thinning of eggshells in birds of prey, reproductive failure, and extinction. DDT is now banned in most countries.

CFCs: In the stratosphere, UV radiation breaks CFCs to release Cl• radicals. These catalytically destroy ozone (O₃ → O₂), depleting the ozone layer. The ozone hole allows harmful UV-B radiation to reach Earth's surface, causing skin cancer, cataracts, and disruption of marine ecosystems. The Montreal Protocol (1987) was signed to phase out CFC use globally.

Solubility & Density 💧⚖️

🚿
Why doesn't oil mix with water? Same reason haloalkanes don't!
Water molecules love each other (strong H-bonds). To dissolve a haloalkane, you'd need to break those H-bonds AND the haloalkane's van der Waals forces. But the new attractions formed between haloalkane and water are WEAKER than the H-bonds you broke — not worth it! So haloalkanes stay separate.
📖 Board-Ready Definition — Solubility
Haloalkanes are very slightly soluble in water because the energy required to break the strong hydrogen bonds between water molecules is greater than the energy released when new (weaker) haloalkane–water interactions form. However, they dissolve readily in organic solvents (ether, alcohol, benzene) because the new intermolecular forces between haloalkane and organic solvent are comparable to the ones being broken.

Mechanism Deep Dive — Part 2

SN1 & SN2 — Every Detail 🔬

🔵 SN2 — Complete Mechanism

📖 Full Board Definition
SN2 is a single-step concerted mechanism. The nucleophile attacks the carbon from the back (180° opposite to leaving group) simultaneously as the C–X bond breaks. In the transition state, carbon is bonded to five atoms (pentacoordinate).
Rate = k[RX][Nu⁻] | Reactivity: CH₃X > 1° > 2° >> 3° | Stereochemistry: Walden Inversion

📐 Steric Hindrance — Why 3° Can't Do SN2

🟠 SN1 — Complete Mechanism

📖 Full Board Definition
SN1 is a two-step mechanism. Step I (slow, RDS): C–X bond breaks heterolytically → planar carbocation + X⁻. Step II (fast): Nu⁻ attacks carbocation from both faces.
Rate = k[RX] | Reactivity: 3° > 2° > 1° | Stereochemistry: Racemisation
⭐ SN1 Full Example: (CH₃)₃CBr + OH⁻
Step I (SLOW — rate-determining):
(CH₃)₃C–Br ⇌ (CH₃)₃C⁺ + Br⁻
↑ Planar carbocation (sp², stable 3°)
↑ Energy from solvation of Br⁻ by protic solvent

Step II (FAST):
(CH₃)₃C⁺ + OH⁻ → (CH₃)₃C–OH
(2-Methylpropan-2-ol)
⚡ Step I is slow and REVERSIBLE (⇌). The energy for C–Br breaking comes from solvation of Br⁻ by the polar protic solvent's protons.
⚡ Greater stability of carbocation → faster SN1. 3° > 2° > 1° > CH₃⁺
⚡ Allylic/benzylic carbocations are also very stable (resonance stabilisation) → fast SN1.
⚡ The planar carbocation gets Nu from BOTH faces → 50% R + 50% S = RACEMIC MIXTURE.
📌 For BOTH SN1 and SN2: R–I > R–Br > R–Cl >> R–F (larger halogen = weaker bond = better leaving group)
Section 6.7.1(c) — Full Stereochemistry

Stereochemistry — Everything 🔄

🪞
Welcome to the world of mirror-image molecules!
Imagine a left-handed glove — its mirror image is a right-handed glove. They look the same but are DIFFERENT. This is chirality, and it causes optical activity — the ability to rotate light. Only molecules with 4 DIFFERENT groups on a carbon are chiral!

💡 1. Optical Activity

📖 Board-Ready Definition
Optical activity — the ability to rotate plane-polarised light. Measured by a polarimeter (Nicol prism setup).

• Dextrorotatory (d / +): rotates light clockwise (right). Place (+) before degree.
• Laevorotatory (l / −): rotates light anticlockwise (left). Place (−) before degree.

Such (+) and (−) isomers are called optical isomers; the phenomenon is optical isomerism.

✋ 2. Chirality, Chiral Centre & Asymmetric Carbon

📖 Definitions (Van't Hoff & Le Bel, 1874)
Chiral molecule: A molecule that is non-superimposable on its mirror image.
Achiral molecule: Superimposable on its mirror image — optically inactive.
Asymmetric carbon (chiral centre / stereocentre): Carbon bonded to 4 different groups.

Test: Draw molecule + mirror image. Can you perfectly overlap them? No → chiral. Yes → achiral.

Examples of chiral molecules: 2-chlorobutane, BrClCHI, 2-bromopropanoic acid, butan-2-ol.
Achiral example: propan-2-ol (two CH₃ groups same on the central carbon → NOT chiral).

🔁 3. Enantiomers

📖 Definition
Enantiomers are stereoisomers that are non-superimposable mirror images of each other.

Properties: Identical melting point, boiling point, refractive index — all physical properties same.
Only difference: they rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions (equal magnitude, opposite sign).
If one is (+), the other is (−). History: Louis Pasteur (1848) first demonstrated this with tartrate crystals.

⚖️ 4. Racemic Mixture

📖 Definition
Racemic mixture = equimolar mixture of two enantiomers. Shows zero net optical rotation (rotations cancel).
Notation: (±) or dl before the name — e.g., (±)-butan-2-ol
Racemisation: The process of converting a pure enantiomer into a racemic mixture.

🔀 5. Inversion, Retention & Racemisation

✅ Retention

Configuration preserved. No bond to stereocentre broken. Occurs when substituent change is away from the chiral centre.

Example: (–)-2-Methylbutan-1-ol + HCl (heat) → (+)-1-Chloro-2-methylbutane (retention — same spatial arrangement, sign changed because groups changed priority!)

🔄 Inversion (Walden)

Configuration completely inverted. Bond to stereocentre IS broken, Nu attacks from back. Like umbrella flipping in wind. Occurs in SN2. R-config → S-config (or vice versa).

⚖️ Racemisation

Equal mixture of R and S formed. Occurs in SN1 because planar carbocation is attacked equally from both faces → 50:50 R:S → zero rotation.

🎯 The Ultimate Stereochemistry Trick
"SN2 = Inversion (umbrella) | SN1 = Racemic (coin toss)"
SN2 forces one-face attack → complete flip. SN1 flat carbocation → equal chance both faces → 50:50 = racemic.

Section 6.7.1 — Elimination

Elimination Reactions (β-Elimination) ⬆️

💨
Think of Elimination like breaking up a toxic relationship!
The base (KOH) acts like a friend who says "Let's get out of here!" It grabs the H from the β-carbon, and the halogen leaves the α-carbon, and a beautiful double bond (alkene) forms between them. It's called β-elimination because H is removed from the β-carbon!
📖 Board-Ready Definition
When a haloalkane with a β-hydrogen atom is heated with alcoholic KOH, there is elimination of H from the β-carbon and X from the α-carbon to form an alkene. This is called β-elimination or dehydrohalogenation.

α-carbon: The carbon directly bonded to the halogen.
β-carbon: The carbon adjacent to the α-carbon.
🔬 General Elimination Reaction
CH₃–CH₂–Br + KOH(alc.) ──heat──→ CH₂=CH₂ + KBr + H₂O

(Bromoethane)                          (Ethene)

⭐ Zaitsev's Rule — Which Alkene is Major?

📖 Zaitsev's Rule (1875 — Alexander Zaitsev)
"In dehydrohalogenation reactions, the preferred product is that alkene which has the greater number of alkyl groups attached to the doubly bonded carbon atoms."

Simple version: More substituted alkene = more stable = major product.
⭐ Zaitsev's Rule Example: 2-Bromopentane
H₃C–CH₂–CH₂–CHBr–CH₃ + KOH(alc.) ──→

H₃C–CH₂–CH=CH–CH₃ (Pent-2-ene, 81%) ← MAJOR (more substituted)
+ H₃C–CH₂–CH₂–CH=CH₂ (Pent-1-ene, 19%) ← minor (less substituted)
✅ Pent-2-ene is MAJOR because the double bond has TWO alkyl groups on it (CH₃ and C₂H₅).
❌ Pent-1-ene is minor because the double bond has only ONE alkyl group on it (C₃H₇).
⚔️ Elimination vs Substitution — The Battle!
When an alkyl halide reacts with a base/nucleophile, there are TWO competing pathways: substitution (SN1/SN2) and elimination. Which wins depends on:

• Primary halide → prefers SN2 (nucleophile attacks carbon directly)
• Secondary halide → SN2 or elimination depending on base strength/size
• Tertiary halide → SN1 or elimination depending on stability
• Bulky base (like t-BuO⁻) → prefers elimination (can't approach tetravalent carbon)
• Small Nu/base (like OH⁻) → prefers substitution
• High temperature → favours elimination
• Aq. KOH → substitution | Alc. KOH → elimination
Section 6.7.1 — Metals

Reactions with Metals ⚙️

🧲
When carbon meets metal — magic happens!
Normally carbon is happy with organic stuff. But when it reacts with metals like Mg or Na, it forms organometallic compounds — compounds with a C–Metal bond. These are super powerful tools for making new carbon–carbon bonds!

🏆 Grignard Reagent — The Most Important Organometallic!

📖 Definition
Grignard Reagents (RMgX) are alkyl magnesium halides formed by reacting haloalkanes with magnesium in dry ether. Discovered by Victor Grignard in 1900 (Nobel Prize 1912).

In R–Mg–X:
• C–Mg bond: covalent but highly polar (δ− on C, δ+ on Mg) — carbon becomes a nucleophile!
• Mg–X bond: essentially ionic

Section 6.7.2

Reactions of Haloarenes 🔵

🛡️
Aryl halides are the fortress of organic chemistry!
The benzene ring protects the C–X bond through resonance. Nucleophiles can't attack easily (the ring is already electron-rich and repels them). But electrophiles LOVE attacking the ring (EAS). And with special conditions (623K, 300 atm!), you can force a nucleophile in.

❌ Why Haloarenes Are LESS Reactive Towards Nucleophilic Substitution

Reason 1: Resonance Effect

The lone pair on Cl is in conjugation with the π system of the benzene ring → C–Cl gets partial double bond character → stronger, shorter bond → hard to break.
Haloalkane C–Cl: 177 pm | Haloarene C–Cl: 169 pm (shorter = stronger!)

Reason 2: sp² vs sp³ Carbon

In haloarene, C attached to X is sp² (more s-character = more electronegative = holds C–X electrons more tightly). In haloalkane, it's sp³ (less s-character = less grip). Harder to break = less reactive.

Reason 3: Phenyl Cation Unstable

SN1 needs carbocation formation. Phenyl cation (C₆H₅⁺) is extremely unstable — NOT stabilised by resonance (sp² carbon can't accommodate the positive charge). So SN1 is ruled out completely for haloarenes.

Reason 4: Electronic Repulsion

The benzene ring is electron-rich (π cloud above and below). Nucleophiles are also electron-rich. Like charges repel — nucleophile can't approach the ring easily.

🔥 Nucleophilic Substitution — Extreme Conditions Needed!

🔬 Chlorobenzene → Phenol (Dow Process)
C₆H₅Cl + NaOH(aq) ──623K, 300 atm──→ C₆H₅ONa ──H⁺──→ C₆H₅OH
(Sodium phenoxide) (Phenol)
⚠️ Requires extremely harsh conditions — 623K (350°C!) and 300 atmospheres pressure. Shows just how resistant haloarenes are to nucleophilic substitution.
This is the Dow Process for making phenol.

⭐ Effect of –NO₂ at Ortho/Para — Activates Nucleophilic Attack!

📖 Key Concept
Electron-withdrawing groups (–NO₂) at ortho and para positions increase the reactivity of haloarenes towards nucleophilic substitution. Meta-position –NO₂ has NO effect.

Why? The –NO₂ group withdraws electron density from the ring and stabilises the carbanion intermediate through resonance. The negative charge in the transition state falls on carbons at ortho and para positions relative to Cl — exactly where –NO₂ can stabilise it. Meta position –NO₂ cannot overlap with these positions, so no stabilisation and no rate enhancement.

Ex 6.4 — Which has highest dipole moment: CH₂Cl₂, CHCl₃, or CCl₄?

Answer: CH₂Cl₂ (Dichloromethane)

CCl₄: All 4 C–Cl bonds point symmetrically → all dipoles cancel → net μ = 0
CHCl₃: 3 C–Cl bonds in one direction + 1 C–H opposite → partial cancellation → moderate μ
CH₂Cl₂: 2 C–Cl bonds on same side + 2 C–H on other → vectors add constructively → HIGHEST net dipole moment

Order: CH₂Cl₂ > CHCl₃ > CCl₄ (= 0)

Ex 6.5 — C₅H₁₀ doesn't react with Cl₂ in dark but gives single monochloro compound C₅H₉Cl in sunlight. Identify.

Answer: Cyclopentane (C₅H₁₀)

• Doesn't react in dark → not an alkene (alkenes react with Cl₂ in dark by addition). Must be an alkane or cycloalkane (free radical halogenation needs UV light).
• C₅H₁₀ with only ONE monochloro compound → ALL hydrogens must be equivalent!
• In cyclopentane (pentagon ring), all 10 H atoms are equivalent → replacing any H gives the same product → single monochloro compound: chlorocyclopentane (C₅H₉Cl) ✓

Ex 6.9 — Which reacts faster in SN2: (i) CH₃Br or CH₃I (ii) (CH₃)₃CCl or CH₃Cl?

(i) CH₃I reacts faster than CH₃Br in SN2.
Both are methyl halides (same steric environment). For the same alkyl group, reactivity in SN2: R–I > R–Br > R–Cl >> R–F. I⁻ is a better leaving group (larger, more polarisable, weaker C–I bond).

(ii) CH₃Cl reacts faster than (CH₃)₃CCl in SN2.
Methyl chloride has no steric hindrance (only H atoms). (CH₃)₃CCl is tertiary — three bulky CH₃ groups completely block back-side nucleophilic attack → SN2 rate ≈ 0.

(i) 1-Bromo-1-methylcyclohexane + NaOEt:
Products: 1-methylcyclohex-1-ene (major, more substituted, Zaitsev) + methylenecyclohexane (minor)

(ii) 2-Chloro-2-methylbutane + NaOEt:
Products: 2-methylbut-2-ene (major, Zaitsev — trisubstituted) + 2-methylbut-1-ene (minor)

(iii) 2,2,3-Trimethyl-3-bromopentane + NaOEt:
Products: 2,3-dimethylpent-2-ene and 2,2-dimethylpent-3-ene; the more substituted is major by Zaitsev.

Ex 6.12 — Explain why: (i) dipole of chlorobenzene lower than cyclohexyl chloride (ii) alkyl halides immiscible with water (iii) Grignard in anhydrous conditions

(i) Chlorobenzene vs cyclohexyl chloride dipole moment:
In chlorobenzene, the lone pair on Cl is in resonance with the π system of the ring, partially back-donating into the ring → reduces effective electron withdrawal by Cl → net dipole moment is lower. In cyclohexyl chloride (sp³ C), there's no resonance → full C–Cl polarity → higher dipole moment.

(ii) Why immiscible with water:
To dissolve in water, the strong H-bonds between water molecules must be broken. The energy released when new haloalkane–water interactions form is insufficient to compensate. Hence haloalkanes are slightly soluble/immiscible with water.

(iii) Grignard in anhydrous conditions:
Grignard reagents (R–MgX) are extremely reactive towards any proton source. Even water is acidic enough to destroy them: R–MgX + H₂O → R–H + Mg(OH)X. To prevent this, all moisture must be excluded → dry ether solvent, dry glassware.

C₆H₅CHClC₆H₅ (diphenylchloromethane) is more easily hydrolysed.

Ex 6.20 — Aqueous KOH gives alcohols, alcoholic KOH gives alkenes. Why?

Aqueous KOH: In water, OH⁻ is heavily solvated (surrounded by water molecules) and relatively small. It acts as a nucleophile and attacks the electrophilic carbon (Cδ+) of C–X bond → nucleophilic substitution → alcohol.

Alcoholic KOH: In alcohol solvent, OH⁻ is less solvated → more "free" and more basic (larger, bulkier effective size with solvent sheath). This bulky base prefers to abstract the β-hydrogen rather than attack the hindered carbon. This leads to elimination → alkene.

Q. (a) Why are haloarenes much less reactive than haloalkanes towards nucleophilic substitution? Give two reasons. (b) Write the product when chlorobenzene is heated with NaOH at 623K, 300 atm.

📋 CBSE Board 2022 | 2 Marks
Q. Although chlorine is an electron-withdrawing group, yet it is ortho-para directing in EAS. Explain.
Chlorine has two opposing effects on the benzene ring:

Inductive effect (–I): Cl withdraws electrons from the ring through σ-bonds, making the ring electron-poor → DEACTIVATES the ring (slower EAS than benzene).

Resonance effect (+R): The lone pair of Cl donates into the ring through resonance, increasing electron density specifically at ortho and para positions → DIRECTS electrophile to o/p.

The inductive effect (stronger) controls reactivity (deactivation). The resonance effect controls orientation (o/p directing). Net result: Cl is a deactivating, o/p-directing group.
📋 CBSE Board 2020 | 3 Marks
Q. Explain with mechanism why the presence of –NO₂ group at ortho and para positions activates chlorobenzene towards nucleophilic substitution but –NO₂ at meta position has no effect.
In nucleophilic substitution of chlorobenzene, OH⁻ attacks the ring in a slow step to form a carbanion intermediate (Meisenheimer complex). This carbanion must be stabilised for the reaction to proceed.

ortho/para –NO₂: When –NO₂ is at ortho or para to –Cl, the negative charge of the carbanion intermediate falls on carbons that are directly adjacent to the –NO₂ group. The electron-withdrawing –NO₂ group can stabilise this negative charge through resonance → lower activation energy → faster reaction.

meta –NO₂: When –NO₂ is at meta, the resonating structures of the carbanion never place the negative charge on the carbon bearing the –NO₂ group. Therefore, –NO₂ cannot stabilise the carbanion through resonance at meta position → no rate enhancement.

HOTS | LEVEL 2
Q. Dipole moment of CH₂Cl₂ is higher than CHCl₃ even though CHCl₃ has more Cl atoms. Explain.
In CCl₄: all four C–Cl bonds are equivalent and arranged tetrahedrally → their dipole vectors cancel → μ = 0.

In CHCl₃: three C–Cl bonds point in one general direction, but one C–H bond points the other way. The C–H dipole partially opposes the resultant of three C–Cl dipoles → partial cancellation → moderate net dipole.

In CH₂Cl₂: two C–Cl bonds on one side, two C–H bonds on the other. The two C–Cl dipoles add (they're on the same side) AND the two C–H dipoles also add in the same direction (C–H dipole goes from C to H, opposite to C–Cl). So ALL bond dipoles reinforce → LARGEST net dipole moment!

Order: μ(CH₂Cl₂) > μ(CHCl₃) > μ(CCl₄) = 0

12. Why must Grignard reagents be prepared in dry conditions?
Water increases the yield
Water destroys Grignard reagent: R–MgX + H₂O → R–H + Mg(OH)X
Water is needed as solvent
Water causes halogen exchange
✅ Grignard reagents are highly reactive towards any proton source — even water! The C–Mg bond (C is δ−) reacts with H from water to give R–H (hydrocarbon). Even alcohol and amines can destroy it. That's why dry ether (not water) is used as solvent.

Wurtz reaction
Fittig reaction
Wurtz-Fittig reaction
Sandmeyer's reaction
✅ Wurtz = alkyl+alkyl, Fittig = aryl+aryl, Wurtz-Fittig = alkyl+aryl. All use Na in dry ether.

The iodoform molecule itself

The carbon atom in CHI₃
The H atom in CHI₃
✅ Iodoform itself is NOT the active antiseptic. When applied, it slowly releases free iodine (I₂), which is the actual antiseptic. Because of its objectionable smell, iodoform has been replaced by other iodine-containing formulations.

✅ In CCl₄, all four C–Cl bonds are arranged symmetrically (tetrahedral). The four dipole vectors are equal in magnitude and point in directions that cancel exactly → net μ = 0. CCl₄ is non-polar despite having polar C–Cl bonds!
