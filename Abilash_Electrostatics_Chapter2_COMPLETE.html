01
Conservative Forces & Introduction
🎡 Think of it like a ROLLER COASTER!

When a roller coaster goes up a hill, it gains potential energy (stores energy). When it comes back down, that energy converts to kinetic energy (speed). The total stays the same! This is exactly what happens with charges in an electric field.

What is a Conservative Force?

A force is called a conservative force if the work done by the force in moving an object between two points is independent of the path taken — it depends only on the initial and final positions. The energy can be stored and retrieved without any loss.

Examples of Conservative Forces:

  • 🌿 Gravitational force — lifting a book to a shelf stores PE; dropping it recovers it
  • 🔄 Spring force — compressing stores PE; releasing recovers it
  • ⚡ Coulomb (Electrostatic) force — moving charges in an electric field
Your phone battery stores electrostatic potential energy in a capacitor. Hydro-electric dams use gravitational PE. Same concept, different force!
🏔️
Mountain Path Analogy Whether you hike up a mountain via the zigzag trail or go straight up the cliff, the height gained is the same — so the gravitational PE gained is also the same. Same with charges: no matter what path you take to move charge A to point P, the work done is always the same!

Why Coulomb Force is Conservative

Coulomb's law: F = kq₁q₂/r² — both gravity and Coulomb force have the same inverse-square dependence on distance (1/r²). This mathematical structure is exactly why both are conservative forces.

🌍 Gravitational Force

F = Gm₁m₂/r²

Mass ↔ replaces ↔ Charge
G ↔ replaces ↔ k = 1/4πε₀

⚡ Coulomb Force

F = kq₁q₂/r²

Both are inverse-square laws — BOTH are conservative!

Setting Up the Concept

Imagine a positive charge Q sitting at the origin. Now you want to bring a small positive test charge q from a far-away point R to a closer point P.

  • Q and q are both positive → they REPEL each other
  • You must push q against this repulsion → you do POSITIVE work
  • This work gets stored as electrostatic potential energy

Key Equations for This Section

Work done (R→P): W_RP = ∫Fₑₓₜ·dr

Potential energy difference: ΔU = U_P – U_R = W_RP    (Eq. 2.2)

PE at a point (taking U_∞ = 0): W_∞P = U_P    (Eq. 2.3)

"Work done by EXTernal force against the field = INCREASE in Potential Energy." External → Increase. Like pumping water uphill — the pump (external) does work, water gains PE!
02
Electrostatic Potential (V)
📏 Height is to gravitational PE... as Voltage is to Electrostatic PE!

Just like "height" tells us about the potential energy per unit mass in gravity, "Voltage" (Electric Potential) tells us about the potential energy per unit charge in electricity. A mountaineer at 1000m has certain gravitational PE. A charge at a point where V = 100V has 100 × q joules of PE!

Why We Define Potential (V) — Not Just PE

The work done to bring charge q to a point is proportional to q itself (since the force is qE, and W = qV). So if we divide the work by q, we get a quantity that only depends on the electric field, not on how much test charge we use. That's the genius of defining Electric Potential!

Electrostatic Potential (V) at a point in an electric field is defined as the work done by an external force in bringing a unit positive charge (without acceleration) from infinity to that point against the electric field.


V = W_∞P / q    OR    V_P – V_R = (U_P – U_R)/q

V = W / q

V = Electric Potential (in Volts, V)
W = Work done by external force (in Joules, J)
q = Charge (in Coulombs, C)
1 Volt = 1 Joule/Coulomb

📐 SI Unit of Potential

Volt (V)

1 V = 1 J/C = 1 kg·m²/(A·s³)

Named after Count Alessandro Volta 🇮🇹

🔑 Key Property

Only potential DIFFERENCE is physically meaningful.

We can add any constant to V — only V₂ – V₁ matters!

Important Sign Convention

  • If Q > 0 (positive source charge): V > 0 at all points, more positive near Q
  • If Q < 0 (negative source charge): V < 0 at all points, more negative near Q
  • Work done by field moving + charge: from high V to low V (downhill)
  • Work done by field moving – charge: from low V to high V (attracted toward + charge)
🎯 "Positive charges are lazy — they naturally move from HIGH potential to LOW potential (like a ball rolling downhill). Negative charges are rebels — they move from LOW to HIGH potential!"
🔋 In your phone battery (3.7V), the positive terminal is at a higher potential than the negative terminal. When current flows, positive charges (conventional current) flow from high V to low V — from + to – terminal through the circuit.

Alessandro Volta — The Man Behind the Volt 🇮🇹

Count Alessandro Volta (1745–1827) was an Italian physicist who invented the first battery (voltaic pile) by stacking moist cardboard disks between different metals. He proved that "animal electricity" (Galvani's frog experiments) was just normal electricity generated by metal contacts. The unit "Volt" is named in his honor.

Potential Due to a Point Charge
🎯 Like ripples on water, but in reverse!

Drop a stone in water — ripples spread outward, getting weaker with distance. Electric potential from a point charge is similar: it's strongest right next to the charge and gets weaker as you move away. But it decreases as 1/r (not 1/r² like the field). Think of the potential as the "height" of a hill centered on the charge!

📐 DERIVATION: Potential due to a Point Charge Q at distance r
1
Setup: Place charge Q at origin O. Find potential at point P, at distance r from O. We bring a unit +ve test charge from infinity to P along the radial direction.
2
Force at intermediate point P' (distance r'):
The Coulomb force on unit +ve charge at P' is:
F = Q/(4πε₀r'²) r̂'  (directed radially outward if Q > 0)
3
Small work done against this force (dr' is negative since we move inward):
ΔW = –Q/(4πε₀r'²) · Δr'
(Negative sign: moving inward means Δr' < 0, but work done is positive — they cancel nicely!)
4
Integrate from r' = ∞ to r' = r:
W = –∫[∞ to r] Q/(4πε₀r'²) dr'
W = Q/(4πε₀) × [1/r'] from ∞ to r
W = Q/(4πε₀r) – Q/(4πε₀×∞)
W = Q/(4πε₀r)
5
By definition, this work = V(r):
V(r) = Q / (4πε₀r) = kQ/r

where k = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C²  |  ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²/N·m²

This formula works for BOTH positive and negative Q!
• Q > 0 → V > 0 (positive potential)
• Q < 0 → V < 0 (negative potential)
• As r → ∞, V → 0 ✓ (consistent with our reference)
🎵 "V is kQ/r — one r below. E is kQ/r² — two r below. Potential has fewer r's than field — Potential is 'higher' mathematically!"

Solved Example (Board Type)

Example 2.1 — Board Pattern 2 Marks

(a) Calculate the potential at a point P due to a charge of 4 × 10⁻⁷ C located 9 cm away.

(b) Hence find the work done in bringing a charge of 2 × 10⁻⁹ C from infinity to P. Does the answer depend on the path?

(a) Given: Q = 4×10⁻⁷ C, r = 9 cm = 0.09 m, k = 9×10⁹ N·m²/C²

V = kQ/r = (9×10⁹ × 4×10⁻⁷) / 0.09 = 4×10⁴ V = 40,000 V

(b) W = qV = (2×10⁻⁹) × (4×10⁴) = 8×10⁻⁵ J

No, work done does NOT depend on the path, because electrostatic force is a conservative force.

Potential Due to an Electric Dipole
🧲 A Dipole is like a tiny barbell of charges!

Imagine holding a dumbbell with one hand pushing (+q) and the other pulling (–q), separated by distance 2a. The total charge is zero, but there's a "direction" to it — that's the dipole moment p!

What is an Electric Dipole?

An electric dipole consists of two equal and opposite charges +q and –q separated by a small distance 2a. Its total charge is zero. It is characterized by the dipole moment vector p:

p = q × 2a    Direction: from –q to +q

SI unit of dipole moment: C·m (Coulomb-metre) or debye (D)

🌊 Water molecule (H₂O) is a classic example of an electric dipole! The oxygen atom pulls electrons more strongly (electronegative), creating a –ve end, while hydrogen side is +ve. This dipole nature of water makes it the "universal solvent" and gives it its amazing properties!
📐 DERIVATION: Potential due to a Dipole at General Point P
1
By superposition principle:
Total potential = sum of potentials from +q and –q:
V = (1/4πε₀)[q/r₁ – q/r₂]   ...(2.9)
where r₁ = distance from +q to P, r₂ = distance from –q to P
2
Geometry (by cosine rule):
r₁² = r² + a² – 2ar cosθ
r₂² = r² + a² + 2ar cosθ
3
For r >> a (we're far from the dipole):
Neglect a² compared to r²:
r₁² ≈ r²(1 – 2a cosθ/r)
r₂² ≈ r²(1 + 2a cosθ/r)
4
Using Binomial theorem (1+x)^n ≈ 1+nx for small x:
1/r₁ ≈ (1/r)(1 + a cosθ/r)
1/r₂ ≈ (1/r)(1 – a cosθ/r)
5
Substitute into V = (q/4πε₀)[1/r₁ – 1/r₂]:
V = (q/4πε₀r)[(1 + a cosθ/r) – (1 – a cosθ/r)]
V = (q/4πε₀r)(2a cosθ/r)
Since p = 2qa:
V = p cosθ / (4πε₀r²) = p·r̂ / (4πε₀r²)

Valid for r >> a (far from dipole)

Special Cases — MUST REMEMBER for Board!

📍 On the Axial Line (θ = 0° or 180°)

cos 0° = 1, cos 180° = –1

V_axial = ±p/(4πε₀r²)

+ sign for θ=0°, – sign for θ=180°

📍 On the Equatorial Line (θ = 90°)

cos 90° = 0

V_equatorial = 0

⚠️ Potential is ZERO on equatorial plane!

"Dipole potential falls as 1/r², but field falls as 1/r³. Always TWO r's for dipole potential vs THREE r's for dipole field. The field has one extra r!"
Property Point Charge Dipole
Potential formula V = kQ/r V = kp cosθ/r²
V falls as 1/r 1/r²
E falls as 1/r² 1/r³
Depends on angle θ? No Yes — depends on cosθ

Potential Due to a System of Charges
🎵 Superposition Principle — Like mixing sounds!

When multiple musicians play, the total sound at your ear is just the sum of each instrument's sound. Similarly, the total potential at any point from multiple charges is just the algebraic sum of individual potentials!

Superposition Principle for Potential: The total potential at a point due to a system of charges is the algebraic sum of the potentials due to individual charges:

V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ... = Σ(kqᵢ/rᵢ)

Note: Potential is a scalar — so just add numbers (with signs), no vector addition needed!

"POTENTIAL adds like a bank account — just sum up all credits and debits (+ and – charges). No direction headache! FIELD is a vector (headache). Potential is a scalar (easy!)."

Potential Due to a Uniformly Charged Spherical Shell

For a shell of radius R with total charge q:

  • Outside (r ≥ R): V = kq/r (acts like all charge at centre)
  • On surface (r = R): V = kq/R
  • Inside (r < R): V = kq/R = constant (same as surface — no field inside!)
The potential INSIDE a spherical shell is constant and equal to the potential at the surface! This is because E = 0 inside the shell, so no work is done in moving a charge inside — hence constant potential.
⚡ Lightning rods on buildings work on this principle! The conducting rod keeps the potential constant over the building, providing a safe path for lightning to dissipate to the ground.

Solved Example 2.2 (Board Type)

Example 2.2 — System of Charges 3 Marks

Two charges 3×10⁻⁸ C and –2×10⁻⁸ C are located 15 cm apart. At what point on the line joining the two charges is the electric potential zero? (Take V∞ = 0)

Let +3×10⁻⁸ C be at origin O. Let –2×10⁻⁸ C be at A (x = 15 cm).

Case 1: P lies BETWEEN O and A at distance x from O:

V = k[3×10⁻⁸/x – 2×10⁻⁸/(15–x)] = 0

⟹ 3/(x) = 2/(15–x) ⟹ 3(15–x) = 2x ⟹ 45 = 5x ⟹ x = 9 cm

Case 2: P lies BEYOND A (x > 15 cm from O):

V = k[3×10⁻⁸/x – 2×10⁻⁸/(x–15)] = 0

⟹ 3/x = 2/(x–15) ⟹ 3(x–15) = 2x ⟹ x = 45 cm → x = 45 cm

✅ So potential is zero at 9 cm (between charges) and 45 cm (beyond –ve charge) from the +ve charge.

Equipotential Surfaces
🏔️ Like Contour Lines on a Map!

On a mountain map, contour lines connect all points at the same height. Similarly, equipotential surfaces connect all points in an electric field that have the same electric potential. A hiker walking along a contour line doesn't gain or lose height — just like moving a charge along an equipotential requires no work!

An equipotential surface is a surface at every point of which the electric potential has the same value. Work done in moving a charge from one point to another on an equipotential surface is always zero.

Key Properties of Equipotential Surfaces

🔄 Property 1: Perpendicular to Field

Electric field lines are always perpendicular (normal) to equipotential surfaces.

Why? If E had a component along the surface, work would be done moving a charge on it — but then it wouldn't be equipotential! Contradiction. So E must be ⊥ to surface.

⚡ Property 2: No Work Done

Work done in moving any charge along an equipotential surface = 0

W = qΔV = q × 0 = 0

📦 Property 3: Never Intersect

Two equipotential surfaces can never intersect each other.

Why? If they intersected, a point on both surfaces would have TWO different values of V — impossible!

📏 Property 4: Closer = Stronger Field

Equipotential surfaces are closer together where E is stronger.

E = –dV/dl, so if E is large, equal ΔV occurs over smaller distance → closer surfaces.

Shapes of Equipotential Surfaces

Relation Between Electric Field and Potential

E = –dV/dl

The negative sign means: E points in the direction of decreasing V
|E| = rate of change of V with distance (V/m = N/C)

Two important conclusions:
(i) Electric field is in the direction in which the potential decreases most steeply.
(ii) Magnitude of E = change in potential per unit displacement normal to the equipotential.
"E goes DOWNHILL! Just like water flows downhill, electric field lines point from high potential to low potential. E = –dV/dl — the MINUS sign is the 'downhill' sign!"
🏔️ When you move from a high-altitude area to a low-altitude area, gravity does positive work on you. Similarly, when a positive charge moves from high V to low V (following E field), the electric force does positive work. The "minus sign" in E = –dV/dl captures this perfectly!

Potential Energy of a System of Charges
🏗️ Building a city of charges — you need energy to assemble them!

Imagine placing stones one by one to build a wall. The first stone needs no energy (nothing to push against). But each new stone must be placed against the force from existing stones. Total energy stored = sum of all this work. Same with charges!

Two-Charge System

📐 PE of Two Charges q₁ and q₂ separated by r₁₂
1
Bring q₁ from ∞ to r₁ — zero work (no field yet)
2
Bring q₂ from ∞ to r₂ — work done = q₂ × (potential at r₂ due to q₁)
Work = q₂ × kq₁/r₁₂
3
U = kq₁q₂/r₁₂ = q₁q₂/(4πε₀r₁₂)

Like charges (q₁q₂ > 0)

U > 0 (positive PE)

You must do work AGAINST repulsion to bring them together → stored as positive PE

Unlike charges (q₁q₂ < 0)

U < 0 (negative PE)

They attract each other — work is done BY the field, not by you → PE is negative

Three-Charge System

U = k[q₁q₂/r₁₂ + q₁q₃/r₁₃ + q₂q₃/r₂₃]

Sum of all PAIRS of interaction energies

📌 Pattern for n charges: Add all pair interactions

For 2 charges: 1 pair interaction

For 3 charges: 3 pair interactions (12, 13, 23)

For 4 charges: 6 pair interactions

General: n(n–1)/2 pairs

"For 3 charges, think of a TRIANGLE — each SIDE of the triangle gives one pair interaction. PE = sum of energies of all three sides!"

Potential Energy in an External Field

PE of a Single Charge in External Field

If a charge q is placed at a point where the external electric potential is V, its potential energy is:
U = qV

PE of a Dipole in an External Field

🧭 Like a compass needle in Earth's magnetic field!

A compass needle (like a dipole) aligns with Earth's field when you let it go. Rotating it away from alignment stores potential energy. Same with an electric dipole in an external field!

📐 PE of a Dipole in Uniform External Field E
1
Dipole moment p makes angle θ with field E.
Torque on dipole: τ = pE sinθ (tends to align p with E)
2
Work done in rotating dipole from θ₀ to θ:
W = ∫τdθ = pE∫sinθ dθ = pE[–cosθ] from θ₀ to θ
3
Taking θ₀ = 90° as reference (U = 0 when p ⊥ E):
U = –pE cosθ = –p·E

θ = 0° (p parallel to E)

U = –pE (minimum PE)

✅ STABLE equilibrium — dipole stays aligned

θ = 180° (p anti-parallel to E)

U = +pE (maximum PE)

⚠️ UNSTABLE equilibrium — slightest nudge and it flips!

"Stable is Cosy (like a ball at bottom of bowl — minimum PE). Unstable is Scary (like a ball on top of hill — maximum PE). θ = 0° is cosy for a dipole!"
🔬 Microwave ovens work by rapidly rotating water molecules (dipoles) using oscillating electric fields. The friction from this rotation heats your food! That's U = –pE cosθ in action!

Conductors in Electrostatic Fields
🏢 A conductor is like a perfect apartment building for electrons!

Electrons in a conductor are FREE to move anywhere in the building. When an electric field is applied (like a fire alarm), electrons rush to one side. This creates an internal field opposing the external one. Eventually, everything reaches equilibrium — like the building stabilizing after an earthquake!

Properties of Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

🔑 Property 1: E = 0 inside a conductor

In electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field inside a conductor is ZERO. If it weren't, free electrons would move (not equilibrium!).

🔑 Property 2: No free charge inside

All excess charge resides on the SURFACE of the conductor. (By Gauss's law — if E = 0 inside, by Gauss's law, net charge inside any Gaussian surface = 0)

🔑 Property 3: Conductor is an equipotential

The entire conductor (surface + interior) is at the same potential. Because E = 0 inside → no work done moving charge inside → constant potential.

🔑 Property 4: E ⊥ to surface

The electric field just outside a conductor's surface is perpendicular to the surface. The surface is an equipotential, so E must be normal to it.

🔑 Property 5: Sharp edges → high σ → high E

Charge density σ is highest at sharp points/edges. This leads to very high E there — the basis of lightning rods!

Electrostatic Shielding

Electrostatic shielding — A hollow conductor completely shields its interior from external electric fields. The field inside a hollow conductor is ZERO regardless of the external field.

🚗 Faraday cage! A car acts as a Faraday cage — if lightning strikes, all charge distributes on the outer surface and the people inside are safe! MRI machines, electronic equipment in labs, and even your microwave door's metal mesh all use this principle!
"CAVES are safe from storms (inside a conductor = safe from E field). CAR = CAVE on wheels — that's why you're safe in a car during lightning!"

Dielectrics and Polarisation
🧸 Dielectrics are like fluffy sponge toys — they can be stretched but don't let electrons escape!

In a conductor, electrons are free to move away entirely. In a dielectric (insulator like glass, rubber, paper), electrons are tightly bound but can shift slightly within each atom/molecule when an electric field is applied. This creates tiny dipoles — that's polarisation!

Types of Dielectrics

Non-polar Dielectrics

e.g., N₂, O₂, CO₂, benzene

In these molecules, the centres of positive and negative charge coincide normally. External E field displaces them slightly, creating induced dipoles.

Polar Dielectrics

e.g., H₂O, HCl, NH₃

These molecules have permanent dipole moments. Normally oriented randomly. External E field aligns them partially.

Polarisation (P) is defined as the dipole moment per unit volume of the dielectric:

P = net dipole moment / volume

Dielectric constant (K or εᵣ): The ratio of the capacitance with dielectric to that without dielectric: K = C/C₀

Also written as: ε = Kε₀ = εᵣε₀

Effect of Dielectric in Capacitor

When a dielectric is inserted between capacitor plates:

  • The electric field inside is reduced: E = E₀/K
  • Voltage decreases: V = V₀/K
  • Capacitance INCREASES: C = KC₀
  • The bound charges on the dielectric surface create an opposing field
🔋 Electrolytic capacitors (in your phone, laptop) use a thin oxide layer (dielectric) between metal plates. The higher the dielectric constant K, the more charge can be stored in the same space — that's why researchers are always hunting for materials with high K!

Capacitors and Capacitance
🪣 A capacitor is like a water BUCKET for electric charge!

Just like a bigger bucket holds more water at the same level, a bigger capacitor holds more charge at the same voltage. Capacitance = how big is your bucket? More capacitance = more charge storage per volt!

A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge (and energy). It consists of two conductors (plates) separated by an insulator (dielectric).

Capacitance (C) is defined as the ratio of charge stored on one plate to the potential difference between the plates:

C = Q / V

SI Unit: Farad (F) = Coulomb/Volt (C/V)

1 Farad is a VERY large unit. Typical capacitors are in μF (10⁻⁶), nF (10⁻⁹), or pF (10⁻¹²).

Parallel Plate Capacitor — The Most Important Type

📐 DERIVATION: Capacitance of Parallel Plate Capacitor
1
Surface charge density: σ = Q/A
2
Electric field between plates (uniform, like two infinite sheets):
E = σ/ε₀ = Q/(ε₀A)
3
Potential difference:
V = E × d = Qd/(ε₀A)
4
Capacitance C = Q/V:
C = ε₀A/d

With dielectric: C = Kε₀A/d

"C = ε₀A/d — BIGGER area = BIGGER C (more room to store charge). BIGGER gap d = SMALLER C (charges are farther apart, weaker attraction). It's like a parking lot — more space, more cars!"
💻 The touchscreen of your phone uses an array of tiny capacitors! When your finger touches the screen, it changes the capacitance at that spot (your finger = additional capacitor). The phone detects this change to know where you touched!

Spherical Capacitor

For a Spherical Conductor of radius R:

C = 4πε₀R

An isolated sphere with charge Q and radius R acts as a capacitor with the "other plate" at infinity.

Earth's capacitance ≈ 4πε₀ × 6.4×10⁶ ≈ 711 μF

Combinations of Capacitors

Series Combination

🚰 Series = Water pipes one after another. Same water (charge) flows through all!

Same charge on all capacitors. Total voltage = sum of individual voltages. Net capacitance DECREASES (harder to push the same amount through more resistance).

1/C_series = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ...

Same charge Q on each. Voltages ADD: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃

Parallel Combination

🛣️ Parallel = Multiple lanes on a highway. Same voltage everywhere!

Same voltage across all capacitors. Total charge = sum of individual charges. Net capacitance INCREASES!

C_parallel = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ...

Same voltage V across each. Charges ADD: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃

"SERIES — Series drivers add reciprocals (1/C like 1/R for resistors). PARALLEL — Parallel additions! Like lanes on a highway, just add! C_p = C₁+C₂+C₃"
Trick to remember series vs parallel for capacitors:
• Capacitors in SERIES: Like resistors in PARALLEL (reciprocal rule)
• Capacitors in PARALLEL: Like resistors in SERIES (direct addition)
This is the OPPOSITE of resistors — common exam trap!

Energy Stored in a Capacitor
⚡🔋 A capacitor is like a spring — stores energy when charged, releases when discharged!

Just like compressing a spring stores elastic PE, pushing charge onto a capacitor stores electrical PE. More charge = harder to push more (repulsion), just like more compression = stiffer spring force!

📐 DERIVATION: Energy Stored in a Capacitor
1
At any instant, if charge on capacitor = q, potential = q/C.
Small work done to add charge dq: dW = (q/C)dq
2
Total work done to charge capacitor from 0 to Q:
W = ∫₀^Q (q/C)dq = Q²/(2C)
3
This work is stored as energy U:
U = Q²/2C = ½CV² = ½QV

(Three equivalent forms — all give energy in Joules)

"Half-C-V-squared — like ½mv² for kinetic energy, ½kx² for spring energy, ½CV² for capacitor energy! The ½ appears whenever energy = integral of F·dx type expressions. All HALF-TIMES formulas!"

Energy Density in Electric Field

u = ½ε₀E²

Energy stored per unit volume of electric field
Units: J/m³

This is derived from: U = ½CV² for parallel plate capacitor, where C = ε₀A/d and V = Ed:

U = ½(ε₀A/d)(Ed)² = ½ε₀E²(Ad) = u × Volume

⚡ Camera flash! That blinding flash in a camera comes from a capacitor. The battery slowly charges the capacitor over a few seconds (you hear it "charging"), storing energy U = ½CV². Then this energy is DUMPED in microseconds through the flash tube — that's why it's so bright! It's all about storing and releasing energy rapidly.

What Happens to Energy When Dielectric is Inserted?

Battery Connected (V = constant)

  • C increases to KC
  • Q increases to KQ₀
  • U increases to KU₀
  • Extra energy from battery

Battery Disconnected (Q = constant)

  • C increases to KC
  • V decreases to V₀/K
  • U decreases to U₀/K
  • Energy lost to dielectric polarization

Potential Energy in an External Field — Deep Dive
🎯 External Field vs Self-Created Field — A Critical Distinction!

So far (in Section 7), we calculated the PE of charges due to forces between THEMSELVES. Now we ask: what is the PE of a charge placed in a field created by some OTHER (external) source? Think of it like this — what is the PE of a ball placed on a table? The table's support (external field) stores energy in the ball, but the ball's own weight doesn't "create" the table!

2.8.1 — PE of a Single Charge in External Field

When a charge q is placed at a point r where the external electric potential is V(r), the potential energy of the charge is:

U = q·V(r)    ...(Eq. 2.27)

This is the work done by an external force in bringing charge q from ∞ to point r in the external field

🔑 KEY DISTINCTION — Very Important for Board!

There are TWO different situations:

  • Section 2.7: PE of a system of charges due to their mutual interaction — U = kq₁q₂/r₁₂
  • Section 2.8: PE of a charge in an EXTERNAL field (field created by other unspecified sources) — U = qV(r)

In Section 2.8, we DON'T care about the sources creating E — we just know V at each point!

The Electron Volt (eV) — Bonus Unit!

When an electron (charge e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C) accelerates through a potential difference ΔV = 1 Volt:

Energy gained = eΔV = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C × 1V = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ J

This is defined as 1 electron volt (1 eV)

📐 PE of system of two charges q₁, q₂ in an External Field

Work done = q₁V(r₁)   [using Eq. 2.27]

Work against external field = q₂V(r₂)

Solved Example 2.4 — Four Charges on Square (CBSE FAVOURITE!)

Example 2.4 — PE of Square Configuration 5 Marks

Four charges +q, –q, +q, –q are placed at corners A, B, C, D of a square of side d. (a) Find work required to assemble this arrangement. (b) A charge q₀ is brought to centre E — how much extra work?

Part (a): Work to assemble (sum all pair interactions)

There are 6 pairs of charges: AB, BC, CD, DA (sides) and AC, BD (diagonals)

Side pairs (distance = d):

AB: +q×(–q): W_AB = k(+q)(–q)/d = –kq²/d

BC: –q×(+q): W_BC = –kq²/d

CD: +q×(–q): W_CD = –kq²/d

DA: –q×(+q): W_DA = –kq²/d

Sum of 4 sides = –4kq²/d

Diagonal pairs (distance = d√2):

AC: +q×(+q): W_AC = kq²/(d√2) = +kq²/(d√2)

BD: –q×(–q): W_BD = kq²/(d√2) = +kq²/(d√2)

Sum of 2 diagonals = +2kq²/(d√2) = +√2 kq²/d

Total U = –4kq²/d + √2 kq²/d = (kq²/d)(√2 – 4)

= –kq²(4–√2)/d = –q²(4–√2)/(4πε₀d)

This matches the book result: U = –q²(4–√2)/(4πε₀d) ✓


Part (b): Extra work to bring q₀ to centre E

Each corner is at distance d√2/2 from E.

V at E = k[q/r + (–q)/r + q/r + (–q)/r] = k × 0/r = 0 V

(Potential from A and C (+q) cancels potential from B and D (–q))

Extra work = q₀ × V_E = q₀ × 0 = ZERO

No work needed to bring ANY charge to the centre!

General result: For any symmetric arrangement where equal positive and negative charges are placed at symmetric positions, the potential at the centre (or any symmetry point) can be ZERO — meaning no work is needed to bring a charge there!

PE of Dipole in External Field — Complete Derivation
🧭 The Spinning Dipole — Like a Stubborn Door!

Imagine trying to shut a heavy door against a spring. The more you push it away from its natural position (aligned with spring), the more energy you store. An electric dipole in a field is just like this — rotating it away from the field direction stores energy. Let go and it snaps back!

📐 FULL DERIVATION: U = –pE cosθ = –p·E
1
Setup: Dipole (charges +q and –q, separation 2a, dipole moment p = 2qa) in uniform external field E. Dipole makes angle θ with E.
2
Torque on dipole: τ = p × E, magnitude = pE sinθ
This torque tends to rotate the dipole to align with E.
3
External torque applied: τ_ext = pE sinθ (equal and opposite, so no angular acceleration — quasi-static rotation).
Work done rotating from θ₀ to θ₁:
W = ∫[θ₀ to θ₁] pE sinθ dθ = pE[–cosθ] from θ₀ to θ₁ = pE(cosθ₀ – cosθ₁)   ...(2.31)
4
Choosing reference (U=0 when θ₀ = π/2):
When the dipole is perpendicular to E (θ = 90°), we set U = 0. This is the natural reference because at θ = 90°, the work done by external and internal potentials cancel out.
Substituting θ₀ = π/2 (cos π/2 = 0):
U(θ) = pE(cos π/2 – cosθ) = –pE cosθ = –p·E

...Eq. 2.32 — This is the potential energy of the dipole

5
Why θ₀ = π/2 is the natural choice:
At θ = 90°, the +q is at r₁ and –q is at r₂. The work done against the external field in bringing +q and –q are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign — they cancel! So the net external field interaction energy is zero at θ = 90°, making it the natural zero of PE.

Solved Example 2.6 — Heat Released by Rotating Dipoles

Example 2.6 — Dipole Energy (Classic Board Question) 3 Marks

A molecule has permanent dipole moment 10⁻²⁹ C·m. 1 mole of substance is fully polarised in field E = 10⁶ V/m. The field direction is suddenly changed by 60°. How much heat is released?

Total dipole moment: p = 6×10²³ × 10⁻²⁹ = 6×10⁻⁶ C·m

Initial angle: θᵢ = 0° (aligned with field)

Final angle: θ_f = 60° (after field rotates 60°)

Uᵢ = –pE cos0° = –6×10⁻⁶ × 10⁶ × 1 = –6 J

U_f = –pE cos60° = –6×10⁻⁶ × 10⁶ × 0.5 = –3 J

ΔU = U_f – Uᵢ = –3 – (–6) = +3 J (PE increased)

Since PE increased by 3 J, the system LOST 3 J of kinetic energy → Heat released = 3 J

Energy is conserved: PE gained = KE lost = heat released

Electrostatics of Conductors — All 6 Results
🏙️ A Conductor is a Perfect City for Electrons!

In a metallic conductor, the outer valence electrons detach from their atoms and become FREE electrons — like citizens free to move anywhere in the city. The positive ions (nucleus + bound electrons) stay fixed like buildings. When no external disturbance exists (electrostatic equilibrium), everything is perfectly calm — no current, no net field, nothing moving!

The 6 Fundamental Results of Conductor Electrostatics

Result 1: E = 0 Inside a Conductor

Statement: In electrostatic equilibrium, the electric field is ZERO at every interior point of a conductor.

Proof/Reason: If E ≠ 0 inside, free electrons would experience force (F = eE) and accelerate — this means charges are moving, which contradicts electrostatic equilibrium! So in the static case, charges must have distributed themselves such that E = 0 everywhere inside.

Implication: The conductor acts as a perfect "E-field shield" for its interior.

Result 2: E ⊥ to Surface at Every Point

Statement: At the surface of a charged conductor, the electric field is always perpendicular (normal) to the surface.

Proof: If E had a tangential component (along the surface), free surface charges would experience force and move — contradicting equilibrium. So E must be purely normal to surface.

Formula: E = σ/ε₀ n̂   where σ = surface charge density, n̂ = outward normal

Result 3: No Excess Charge Inside (Interior)

Statement: In electrostatic equilibrium, the interior of a conductor has no excess free charge. All excess charge resides on the surface.

Proof (using Gauss's Law):

  • Draw any Gaussian surface S inside the conductor
  • Since E = 0 everywhere on S (Result 1), total flux = 0
  • By Gauss's Law: Q_enclosed = ε₀ × flux = 0
  • Since this holds for ANY S (even infinitesimally small), no charge inside

Result 4: Conductor is an Equipotential

Statement: The entire volume of a conductor (and its surface) is at the same constant potential in electrostatic equilibrium.

Proof: Since E = 0 inside and E has no tangential component on surface → no work is done in moving a test charge anywhere inside or on the conductor → no potential difference → constant potential throughout!

Note: If the conductor is charged, the potential JUST OUTSIDE the surface differs from the surface potential because there IS an E field just outside.

Result 5: E at Surface = σ/ε₀

Derivation using Pill Box (Gaussian Surface)
1
Choose a "pill box" (tiny cylinder) as Gaussian surface straddling the conductor surface at point P. Area = δS, height = negligible.
2
Flux: Inside conductor, E = 0 → no flux through inner face. E is ⊥ to surface outside → flux through outer face = E·δS.
Flux through sides = 0 (E ⊥ to area vector of sides).
Total flux = E·δS
3
Charge enclosed = σ·δS
By Gauss's Law: E·δS = σ·δS/ε₀
E = σ/ε₀

Result 6: Electrostatic Shielding

Statement: The electric field inside the cavity of a conductor is ZERO, regardless of the external field configuration. No field can penetrate into the cavity — the cavity is completely shielded.

Mechanism: Any external field induces charge redistribution on the outer conductor surface. These induced charges create an internal field that exactly cancels the external field inside. The cavity remains field-free.

Important detail: If charges ARE placed inside the cavity, then the field inside is NOT zero (it's due to those placed charges). But the outside world still doesn't "see" these charges directly.

Example 2.7 — Real World Conductor Situations (Board LOVES This!)

Q: Why does a comb run through dry hair attract bits of paper?

A: Friction between comb and hair transfers charge — comb becomes charged. The charged comb POLARISES the paper molecules (creates induced dipoles). The nearer end of each paper molecule is attracted more strongly than the far end is repelled → net attraction.

Wet hair: friction reduced → comb doesn't charge → no attraction on rainy days!

Q: Why are aircraft tyres slightly conducting?

A: Aircraft moving through air builds up static charge by friction (triboelectric effect). If tyres were perfect insulators, charge would accumulate on the aircraft until a spark discharge occurs near fuel — FIRE HAZARD! Slightly conducting tyres allow this charge to safely leak to the ground upon landing.

Q: Why do vehicles carrying inflammable materials have metallic ropes on ground?

A: Same principle as aircraft tyres. Vehicle motion generates static charge. The metallic chain provides a continuous conducting path to Earth — the charge continuously dissipates as the vehicle moves, preventing dangerous accumulation.

Q: Bird on high-voltage wire is safe; man touching it dies. Why?

A: Current flows only when there's a POTENTIAL DIFFERENCE creating a complete circuit. The bird touches only the wire — both its feet are at the same potential (the wire). No potential difference across the bird → no current → no shock!

The man standing on the ground (ground potential ≈ 0) and touching the wire (high potential) completes a circuit. Huge potential difference → large current through body → fatal!

"Conductor Rules — 'ESCAPE': E=zero inside, Surface has all charge, Constant potential, All field is ⊥, Pill-box gives E=σ/ε₀, Everything shielded in cavity!"

Dielectrics and Polarisation — Complete Treatment
🍰 Conductor vs Dielectric — Loose Sand vs Sticky Dough!

In a conductor, electrons are like loose sand grains — apply a breeze (E field) and they all fly to one side completely. In a dielectric (insulator), electrons are like dough — you can stretch and deform it slightly, but it never flows freely. Each molecule gets slightly deformed, creating tiny dipoles — that's polarisation!

Polar vs Non-Polar Molecules

Non-Polar Molecules

Centres of +ve and –ve charge coincide. No permanent dipole.

Examples: H₂, O₂, CO₂, N₂, CH₄

External E field: +ve and –ve centres displaced in OPPOSITE directions → creates an induced dipole moment

When E removed → dipole disappears

Polar Molecules

Centres of +ve and –ve charge are permanently separated. Permanent dipole moment.

Examples: H₂O, HCl, NH₃, SO₂

Normally: random orientation (thermal agitation) → net p = 0

External E field: Dipoles tend to align with E → net p ≠ 0

Polarisation (P)

Polarisation P is the dipole moment per unit volume of the dielectric:

P = net dipole moment / volume

For linear isotropic dielectrics: P = ε₀χₑE

where χₑ (chi-e) = electric susceptibility of the dielectric medium (dimensionless constant). Higher χₑ = more easily polarised.

What Happens Inside a Polarised Dielectric

When a rectangular dielectric slab is placed in uniform field E₀:

  • Volume elements inside: no net charge (positive of one dipole cancels negative of adjacent dipole)
  • At the surfaces perpendicular to E₀: unneutralized charges → induced surface charge ±σₚ
  • These surface charges create an opposing field Eᵢₙ inside the dielectric
  • Net field inside dielectric: E = E₀ – Eᵢₙ (reduced, but NOT zero unlike conductor!)
KEY DIFFERENCE — Conductor vs Dielectric:
• Conductor: Internal field COMPLETELY cancels external → E_net = 0 inside
• Dielectric: Internal polarisation field only PARTIALLY opposes external → E_net = E₀/K (reduced but non-zero)
This is why "dielectric constant K > 1" — the field is reduced by factor K!
💡 Glass is a dielectric (K ≈ 5–10). When you put glass between capacitor plates, the polarisation inside the glass creates a field opposing the capacitor field, reducing the net field. This allows the capacitor to hold MORE charge at the same voltage (C = KCo). That's why glass/ceramic capacitors exist!

The Parallel Plate Capacitor — Complete Treatment
📐 Two Infinite Charged Sheets Facing Each Other!

Remember from Chapter 1 — a single infinite sheet of charge density σ creates a uniform field σ/2ε₀ on each side. Now put two such sheets facing each other: outside, the fields cancel (zero!); between them, the fields ADD UP to give σ/ε₀. That's the magic of a parallel plate capacitor!

📐 COMPLETE DERIVATION: Electric Field in Parallel Plate Capacitor
1
Setup: Two large parallel plates, each area A, separation d (d² << A so we can treat as infinite planes). Plate 1: charge +Q, surface charge density σ = Q/A. Plate 2: charge –Q, surface charge density –σ.
2
Field from each plate alone:
Plate 1 (+σ): E₁ = σ/(2ε₀) pointing AWAY from plate 1 (outward on both sides)
Plate 2 (–σ): E₂ = σ/(2ε₀) pointing TOWARD plate 2 (inward on both sides)
3
Superposition — three regions:
Region I (above plate 1): E₁ upward, E₂ upward (both away from their respective plates) — WAIT: E₂ from plate 2 below points upward in Region I. E₁ from plate 1 also points upward (away from +ve plate). They ADD? No — let's be careful with directions.

Actually: In Region I (above plate 1): E from plate 1 (+σ) = σ/2ε₀ UPWARD. E from plate 2 (–σ) = σ/2ε₀ UPWARD (field from –ve plate points toward it, so in Region I above both plates, it points DOWNWARD). These CANCEL.
E_I = σ/(2ε₀) – σ/(2ε₀) = 0  ...(2.39)
Region II (between plates): E from plate 1 points downward; E from plate 2 also points downward (toward –ve plate). They ADD:
E_II = σ/(2ε₀) + σ/(2ε₀) = σ/ε₀ = Q/(ε₀A)  ...(2.40)
Region III (below plate 2): By symmetry = 0. ...(2.41)
4
Potential difference V between plates:
V = E × d = (σ/ε₀) × d = Qd/(ε₀A)
5
Capacitance C = Q/V:
C = ε₀A/d

C increases with area A, decreases with separation d

"Plates are like hands clapping — outside, the claps cancel (E=0). Between your hands, the sound is loudest (E = σ/ε₀). Two fields adding up inside, cancelling outside!"

Effect of Dielectric on Capacitor — Two Cases
🛡️ Dielectric is like a Speed Breaker for Electric Field!

Without dielectric: E is full speed. With dielectric: the polarization charges slow E down by factor K. Less E means less V (for same Q). Less V means MORE Q can fit at the same voltage. So Capacitance INCREASES by K! It's like adding lanes to a highway — more traffic (charge) can flow!

Dielectric Partially Filling the Capacitor

📐 Capacitance with dielectric slab of thickness t between plates (d apart)
1
Setup: Plate separation = d. Dielectric slab of thickness t (t < d), dielectric constant K, fills part of the gap. Remaining air gap = (d – t).
2
Electric field:

In dielectric: E = E₀/K = Q/(Kε₀A)

V = (Q/ε₀A)[d – t + t/K]
4

C = ε₀A / [d – t + t/K]

Special case t = d (full dielectric): C = Kε₀A/d (as expected!)
Special case K → ∞ (conductor): C = ε₀A/(d–t) (as if conductor removes gap t)

  • Q increases: Q = KQ₀ (more charge flows from battery)

  • Energy source: Battery provides extra energy

  • Q stays same (no path for charge to flow)

  • V decreases: V = V₀/K
  • E decreases: E = E₀/K
  • U decreases: U = Q²/2C = U₀/K
  • Energy "lost" goes into polarizing the dielectric!

Van de Graaff Generator
🌩️ The World's Greatest Charge Pump — Like Bucket Brigade to the Sky!

Imagine passing buckets of water up a chain to fill a tank at the top. The Van de Graaff generator does the same with electric charge — it uses a conveyor belt (moving rubber belt) to carry small charges up to a large metal sphere on top, continuously pumping up the potential to millions of volts!

A Van de Graaff generator is a device used to build up very high voltages (of the order of a few million volts). It uses the principle that:

  • The charge given to a hollow conductor always moves to the outer surface
  • Electric field outside a conductor is σ/ε₀ — so we can keep adding charge indefinitely until E exceeds dielectric strength of air

Working Principle — Step by Step

  1. Bottom (Spray Comb): A pointed comb connected to a high voltage source (say +10kV) sprays charge onto the moving belt via "corona discharge" (sharp points at high voltage ionise nearby air, which then deposits on belt)
  2. Belt Moves Up: The positively charged belt carries charge upward (motor drives the belt)
  3. Top (Collecting Comb): Inside the sphere, another comb with sharp points collects charge from belt and transfers it to the INNER surface of the sphere
  4. Charge Migrates to Outer Surface: By Result 3 of conductors — excess charge instantly migrates from inner to OUTER surface of the sphere!
  5. Process Repeats: More and more charge keeps accumulating on the outer surface, building up the potential
  6. Limit: The process continues until the electric field near the sphere exceeds 3×10⁶ V/m (dielectric strength of air) → air breaks down → corona discharge → sparks!
Maximum potential: V_max = R × E_breakdown = R × 3×10⁶ V

For a sphere of radius R = 1m: V_max = 3×10⁶ V = 3 MV!
In practice, high-pressure gas (SF₆) is used to increase breakdown voltage to 10–25 MV!

Applications

  • Nuclear physics research: Accelerate ions/protons to MeV energies to bombard atomic nuclei
  • Medical: Radiation therapy for cancer (use high-energy particles)
  • Demonstration: Physics demonstrations — hair standing on end!
  • Science museums: The ball with millions of volts you can safely touch (because current is tiny — Van de Graaff has very small current capacity)
⚡ Why is it SAFE to touch a Van de Graaff sphere even at millions of volts? Because it has VERY low current capacity! Danger from electricity is primarily from CURRENT (amperes), not just voltage. A Van de Graaff at 1 MV but with only nanoamperes of current will give you a harmless (but startling!) shock. But 230V from your wall socket with amperes of current can be fatal!

Corona Discharge / Action of Sharp Points: At sharp tips, charge density σ is very high → E = σ/ε₀ is very high → nearby air molecules get ionised → air conducts → charge leaks off. This principle is used both in Van de Graaff (to spray/collect charge) and in lightning rods (to safely discharge clouds)!

🍕 Why a 1 Farad Capacitor Would Be the Size of a Football Field!

The math shows that to make a capacitor with just 1 Farad of capacitance using plates 1cm apart in air, you'd need plates covering about 10⁹ m² — that's a square 30 km on each side! Fringing effect: For a parallel plate capacitor with plates of finite area, the electric field lines bend outward near the edges of the plates. This means:

  • The surface charge density σ is not strictly uniform near plate edges
  • For d² << A (plates much larger than gap), fringing is negligible in central region

Effect of Dielectric on Capacitance — Full NCERT Derivation
🧊 The Dielectric Acts Like a Cushion Between the Plates!

Imagine squeezing a sponge between your hands. The sponge resists, creating opposing pressure — reducing the net "squeeze". A dielectric does the same: it gets polarised (develops opposing charges) that partially cancel the plate charges' effect, reducing the field and voltage. Result: same Q fits at lower V → bigger capacitance!

📐 COMPLETE DERIVATION: C = Kε₀A/d with Dielectric (NCERT Method — Eq. 2.46 to 2.54)
1
Without dielectric (vacuum between plates):
E₀ = σ/ε₀,   V₀ = E₀d = σd/ε₀,   C₀ = Q/V₀ = ε₀A/d   ...(Eq. 2.46)
2
Insert dielectric (full gap): The dielectric gets polarised → creates bound surface charges ±σₚ on its faces. These create an opposing field inside.
Net surface charge density = σ – σₚ (plate charge minus bound dielectric charge)
New field: E = (σ – σₚ)/ε₀   ...(Eq. 2.47)
3
Linear dielectric assumption: For linear dielectrics, σₚ ∝ E₀ ∝ σ.
So (σ – σₚ) = σ/K where K is the dielectric constant (K > 1)   ...(Eq. 2.49)
Physical meaning: The bound charges reduce the effective surface charge density by factor K.
4
New potential difference:
V = Ed = (σ – σₚ)d/ε₀ = σd/(Kε₀) = Qd/(KAε₀)   ...(Eq. 2.50)
5
New capacitance:
C = Q/V = Q/(Qd/KAε₀) = KAε₀/d
C = Kε₀A/d = εA/d   ...(Eq. 2.51)

where ε = Kε₀ = permittivity of medium   ...(Eq. 2.52)

6
Key relationships derived:
Permittivity: ε = Kε₀   ...(Eq. 2.52)
Dielectric constant: K = ε/ε₀   ...(Eq. 2.53) — dimensionless!
Ratio: K = C/C₀   ...(Eq. 2.54) ← MOST IMPORTANT for board!
The dielectric constant K tells us by what factor capacitance INCREASES from its vacuum value.
K = C/C₀    i.e.,    C = KC₀

K = dielectric constant (always > 1 for any real material)

Example 2.8 — Dielectric Slab of Thickness (3/4)d 3 Marks

A dielectric slab of constant K has same area as plates but thickness (3/4)d. How does capacitance change when inserted between plates (separation d)?

Air gap thickness = d – (3d/4) = d/4. Dielectric thickness t = 3d/4.

Field in air gap: E₀ = V₀/d (original field without dielectric)

Field in dielectric: E = E₀/K

New potential V = E₀(d/4) + (E₀/K)(3d/4) = E₀d[1/4 + 3/(4K)] = V₀(K+3)/(4K)

New capacitance: C = Q/V = Q·4K/[V₀(K+3)] = C₀ · 4K/(K+3)

C = 4K/(K+3) × C₀

Since K > 1: 4K/(K+3) > 1, so capacitance increases. ✓

Check: K→∞ (conductor): C → 4C₀ = ε₀A/(d/4) — conductor removes the d/4 air gap effectively ✓

General formula for partial dielectric slab (thickness t, constant K, in capacitor of separation d):
C = ε₀A / (d – t + t/K)
This is derived by treating air gap and dielectric as capacitors in SERIES.

This gives K×A/d an enormous value — some supercapacitors reach 3000 Farads! They're used in regenerative braking in hybrid cars to capture and release energy rapidly.

Energy Stored in a Capacitor — Complete NCERT Derivation
⛽ Charging a Capacitor is Like Filling a Tank Uphill!

Imagine pumping water uphill into a tank. The first litre is easy (not much water already up there). Each subsequent litre is harder (more water above means more back-pressure). The total work done = energy stored. Charging a capacitor is identical: the first bit of charge goes on easily, each subsequent bit fights the charge already there!

📐 COMPLETE DERIVATION: U = Q²/2C = ½CV² = ½QV (NCERT Eq. 2.68–2.69)
1
Intermediate state: Suppose at some moment, capacitor has charge Q' on plate 1 and –Q' on plate 2. Potential difference at this stage = V' = Q'/C.
2
Work to transfer small charge δQ' from plate 2 to plate 1:
δW = V' · δQ' = (Q'/C) · δQ'   ...(Eq. 2.68)
3
Integrate from 0 to Q:
W = ∫₀^Q (Q'/C)dQ' = (1/C) × [Q'²/2]₀^Q = Q²/(2C)
4
Three equivalent forms:
U = Q²/2C = ½CV² = ½QV   ...(Eq. 2.69)

Use Q²/2C when Q is known | ½CV² when V is known | ½QV when both known

Energy Density — Energy Per Unit Volume

📐 DERIVATION: u = ½ε₀E² (NCERT Eq. 2.70–2.73)
1
For parallel plate capacitor: U = Q²/2C = (Aσ)²/(2 × ε₀A/d) = (Aσ)²d/(2ε₀A) = (A²σ²d)/(2Aε₀) = (Aσ²d)/(2ε₀)   ...(Eq. 2.70)
2
Since E = σ/ε₀ → σ = ε₀E → σ² = ε₀²E²
Substitute: U = A(ε₀²E²)d/(2ε₀) = ½ε₀E² × (Ad)   ...(Eq. 2.72)
3
Volume between plates = A × d
Energy density = Energy / Volume = ½ε₀E²(Ad)/(Ad)
u = ½ε₀E²   ...(Eq. 2.73)

Units: J/m³ | Valid for ANY electric field configuration, not just capacitors!

Though derived for a parallel plate capacitor, u = ½ε₀E² is a UNIVERSAL result — the energy density of an electric field is ½ε₀E² at every point in space, for any field configuration!

Example 2.10 — Classic Energy Transfer Problem (Board Favourite!)

Example 2.10 — 900pF Capacitor Energy Transfer 3 Marks

(a) A 900pF capacitor is charged by 100V battery. Find energy stored. (b) Capacitor disconnected from battery and connected to another uncharged 900pF capacitor. Find final energy. Where did the rest go?

(a) Q = CV = 900×10⁻¹² × 100 = 9×10⁻⁸ C

U₁ = ½QV = ½ × 9×10⁻⁸ × 100 = 4.5×10⁻⁶ J

(b) Battery disconnected → Q conserved = 9×10⁻⁸ C

New total C = 900+900 = 1800 pF

Charge distributes equally (same capacitance): Q' = Q/2 = 4.5×10⁻⁸ C on each

New V' = Q'/C = 4.5×10⁻⁸/900×10⁻¹² = 50V

Total energy = 2 × ½CV'² = 2 × ½×900×10⁻¹² × 2500 = 2.25×10⁻⁶ J

Energy lost = 4.5×10⁻⁶ – 2.25×10⁻⁶ = 2.25×10⁻⁶ J (exactly HALF!)

Where it went: During the transient period when charge redistributes, a brief current flows through the connecting wire. This current × resistance = heat + electromagnetic radiation. This loss occurs regardless of wire resistance (even for superconducting wires, energy is lost as electromagnetic radiation)!

General result: When two equal capacitors are connected, energy loss is ALWAYS 50% of original energy!

"Capacitor energy = HALF-QV. The 'HALF' appears because you start with zero charge and end with Q — average charge = Q/2. Average voltage = V/2. Energy = average charge × average voltage = (Q/2) × (V/2) × 2... hmm, simpler: ½ comes from integration!"

Official NCERT Summary — All 12 Points

Point 1 — Conservative Force & PE

Electrostatic force is conservative. Work done by external force in bringing charge q from R to P = q(Vₚ – V_R) = difference in potential energy.

Point 2 — Definition of Potential

Potential at a point = work done per unit charge by external agency in bringing charge from ∞ to that point. Potential is arbitrary to within an additive constant; only potential DIFFERENCE is physically significant.

V(r) = Q/(4πε₀r) for point charge Q at origin

Point 3 — Dipole Potential

V(r) = p·r̂/(4πε₀r²) — valid for r >> a (far from dipole)

Also exact for a point dipole at origin.

Point 4 — Superposition Principle

V = (1/4πε₀)(q₁/r₁ₚ + q₂/r₂ₚ + ... + qₙ/rₙₚ)

Potential is a scalar — just add algebraically!

Point 5 — Equipotential Surfaces

E at any point is perpendicular to the equipotential surface through that point. E is in direction of steepest decrease of potential.

Point 6 — PE of System of Charges

U = q₁q₂/(4πε₀r₁₂) for two charges

This is the work done by external agency in assembling the configuration from infinity.

Point 7 — PE in External Field

PE of charge q in external potential V(r) = qV(r)

PE of dipole p in uniform field E = –p·E

Point 8 — Electrostatics of Conductors

E = 0 inside conductor | E = (σ/ε₀)n̂ just outside surface | Charges reside only on surface | Potential constant within and on surface | E = 0 in charge-free cavity.

Point 9 — Capacitance

C = Q/V | 1F = 1 C/V | For parallel plate (vacuum): C₀ = ε₀A/d

Point 10 — Dielectric Effect

C = KC₀ where K = dielectric constant (>1). Polarisation reduces net field → reduces V → increases C.

Point 11 — Series & Parallel

Series: 1/C = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ...

Parallel: C = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ...

Point 12 — Energy Stored

U = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/2C

Energy density: u = ½ε₀E² (J/m³)

Points to Ponder — 7 Deep Conceptual Points!

🤔 Point 1 — How can charges be "at rest" if there's a force?

Electrostatics studies forces between stationary charges. But if there's force, why don't they move? Answer: each charge is held at rest by some unspecified external force opposing the net Coulomb force. We calculate what that force would need to be — that's electrostatics!

🤔 Point 2 — Capacitor confines field to small region

A capacitor is specifically designed so that field lines are confined between the plates. This means even though field strength may be considerable, the potential difference (V = Ed for small d) is manageable. This is how capacitors store large energy in small volumes!

🤔 Point 3 — E is discontinuous; V is continuous at conductor surface

At the surface of a charged spherical shell: E jumps from 0 (inside) to σ/ε₀ (outside) — discontinuous! But V is continuous across the surface (both inside and at surface = q/4πε₀R). This distinction often confuses students.

🤔 Point 4 — Dipole oscillates in field if undamped

Torque τ = p×E rotates the dipole toward alignment. But it overshoots! Without damping (friction/radiation), the dipole oscillates about E like a pendulum. Only with dissipation does it eventually align with E stably.

🤔 Point 5 — Self-potential is infinite!

The potential due to a point charge q at its OWN location = kq/0 → INFINITY. This is the "self-energy problem" in classical electrodynamics. When we write U = qV(r), the V(r) refers only to the potential due to OTHER charges, NOT the charge q itself!

🤔 Point 6 — qV(r) uses only external V

In the expression U = qV(r), V(r) is the potential due to external charges only. Including q's own potential would give infinity. This is why in calculating PE of a system, we always work in pairs.

🤔 Point 7 — Shielding is ONE-WAY!

A conductor shields its cavity from external charges (no external field penetrates). BUT the reverse is NOT true: charges placed INSIDE a cavity DO affect the outside! The exterior surface gets induced charges equal to the interior charge. The field outside IS affected by interior charges.

Example: A charge +Q inside a hollow sphere → +Q appears on outer surface → outside field exists as if +Q is at centre. Shielding doesn't work in reverse!

(c) Near end gets charge –q (attracted), far end gets +q (repelled) — conductor is polarised, net charge = 0 but redistribution → net attraction.

Q9: Three identical capacitors each of capacitance C are connected (i) all in series (ii) all in parallel and charged to same voltage V. Compare: (a) total energy stored (b) charge on each capacitor.

Q10: Two identical capacitors are connected in parallel and charged to V. They are then disconnected from the battery and from each other. Each is then connected in series. Find final voltage. Compare energy before and after.

If connected + to –: they ADD voltages → total V_series = V + V = 2V, and effective C = C/2

But total energy = 2 × ½CV² = CV² (unchanged — no charge flows!)

Voltage across series combination = 2V

This is actually how camera flash triggers — capacitors charged separately then switched to series for double-voltage flash discharge!

Q11: A charge +Q is placed at the centre of a grounded (V=0) conducting spherical shell of radius R. Find the charge induced on the shell and the potential at the centre.

Inner surface: –Q induced (by induction). Outer surface would have +Q but grounding allows charge to flow to/from earth.

For V at outer surface = 0: k(Q + Q_outer)/R = 0 → Q_outer = –Q (charge flows from earth)

HOTS — Application

Q2: A capacitor is charged using a battery. The battery is then removed. If the separation between plates is doubled, what happens to (a) charge Q (b) voltage V (c) capacitance C (d) energy U?

(Battery disconnected → Q is CONSTANT)

(a) Q stays same (no path for charge to flow)

(b) C = ε₀A/d → if d doubles, C halves. V = Q/C → V doubles

(c) C = ε₀A/d → C halves

(d) U = Q²/2C → C halves, so U doubles

Where does the extra energy come from? YOU provide it by pulling the plates apart against the attractive force between them! Mechanical work → electrical energy.

HOTS — Analytical

Q3: A parallel plate capacitor is connected to a battery (V constant). If the area of plates is doubled and separation is also doubled, find the change in: (a) capacitance (b) charge (c) energy stored.

(Battery connected → V is CONSTANT)

C = ε₀A/d → if A doubles AND d doubles → C stays same

Q = CV → C same, V same → Q stays same

U = ½CV² → C same, V same → U stays same

Interesting! Doubling both A and d together keeps everything unchanged.

Q4: Two conducting spheres of radii r₁ and r₂ (r₁ > r₂) are connected by a thin conducting wire. System is given charge Q. Show that (a) smaller sphere has higher surface charge density (b) electric field near smaller sphere is stronger.

(a) When connected, both spheres are at same potential V = kQ₁/r₁ = kQ₂/r₂

→ Q₁/Q₂ = r₁/r₂

σ₁/σ₂ = (Q₁/4πr₁²)/(Q₂/4πr₂²) = (Q₁/Q₂)(r₂/r₁)² = (r₁/r₂)(r₂/r₁)² = r₂/r₁

(b) E = σ/ε₀ → since σ₂ > σ₁, E near smaller sphere is greater

This is why sharp points concentrate charge and field → lightning rods!

❌ Mistake 1: Confusing V and U

V (Potential) = Work done per unit charge = W/q    (per unit charge — like a "price per kg")

U (PE) = Actual work done = qV    (total bill = price × quantity)

❌ Mistake 2: Series/Parallel Capacitor Confusion

Capacitors in SERIES: 1/C = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ (OPPOSITE of resistors in series!)

Capacitors in PARALLEL: C = C₁ + C₂ (OPPOSITE of resistors in parallel!)

❌ Mistake 3: Forgetting sign of work

Work by FIELD on +charge: positive when moving from HIGH V to LOW V

Work by EXTERNAL force: positive when moving from LOW V to HIGH V (against field)

They are always equal and opposite!

❌ Mistake 4: Dipole potential formula wrong angle

V = kp cosθ/r² where θ is angle between position vector r and dipole moment p

θ = 0° on axis (towards +q), θ = 90° on equatorial (V = 0)

❌ Mistake 5: Units in numericals

Always convert: cm → m, μC → C, μF → F before plugging into formulas!

9 cm = 0.09 m  |  3μC = 3×10⁻⁶ C  |  10pF = 10×10⁻¹² F
